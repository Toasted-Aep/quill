# Art Mode - research dossier

Findings from parallel domain research (8 dimensions) into how real digital
painting applications are built. This is the evidence base for the Art Mode
design; it deliberately contains no Quill-specific decisions.


## tools-and-ux

### The irreducible tool set: what users check for in the first 60 seconds
Across Krita, Procreate, Fresco, CSP, Sketchbook and Concepts, the tools that are ALWAYS present and always on the top-level toolbar are: brush, eraser, bucket/fill, eyedropper, selection+transform, and layers. Everything else varies. Procreate's entire top bar is literally brush / smudge / erase / layers / color plus a wrench (actions) and magic-wand (adjustments) and S-curve (selection) and arrow (transform) — nine affordances total for an app that is the market leader on tablets (help.procreate.com handbook, Interface section). Sketchbook collapses further to a radial 'lagoon' puck. The mechanism worth copying is not the tool list but the *flatness*: each of those nine is one tap from any state, and none of them opens a modal.

- **Used by:** Procreate (9 top-level affordances), Autodesk Sketchbook (radial lagoon menu), Adobe Fresco (single left rail: brush/eraser/smudge/fill/select/transform/text/shape).
- **Relevance:** Quill's Art Mode beta should ship a fixed 8-slot tool rail and resist adding a 9th. Anything not in the rail is a modifier of a rail tool, not a new tool. This is also the natural WinUI structure: a CommandBar-less custom rail, since WinUI's CommandBar overflow behaviour is wrong for a drawing app.

### Flood fill that actually works on anti-aliased lineart (the single most-complained-about tool in every paint app)
Naive scanline flood fill (Smith seed-fill: push seed span, expand left/right on the scanline, push spans above/below) with a binary tolerance test produces two visible defects on AA lineart: jagged fill edges, and a 1px halo of un-filled semi-transparent pixels between the fill and the line. The production fix is a THREE-stage pipeline, which Krita 5.2 exposes as separate controls (docs.krita.org/en/reference_manual/tools/fill.html): (1) compute a *soft* coverage mask, not a boolean — Krita calls the second knob 'Spread'/'Opacity Spread': pixels within `spread` of the seed colour get alpha 1.0, pixels between `spread` and `threshold` get alpha ramped linearly to 0, so the mask inherits the AA of the source; (2) 'Grow Selection' = signed morphological dilation of that mask by N px (implement with a chamfer/Euclidean distance transform and threshold at N, which is O(n) and gives sub-pixel radii), plus Krita's 'Stop Growing at the Darkest and/or More Opaque Pixels' which halts dilation on a per-pixel basis once the reference luminance starts increasing again — this is what stops the grow from eating the lineart it was supposed to tuck under; (3) 'Feather' = Gaussian blur of the mask; 'Anti-aliasing' = an edge-direction-aware smooth applied only where the mask edge is high-contrast/jagged, distinct from feather because it smooths along the edge rather than isotropically. Krita additionally exposes 'Close Gap', the minimum gap size the fill may NOT propagate through (implemented as a morphological closing on the boundary mask before the flood, so hairline breaks in the lineart are bridged). Fill modes are three: current selection / contiguous region / all regions of similar colour. Reference is selectable: current layer, all visible layers, or colour-labelled layers.

- **Used by:** Krita 5.2+ Fill Tool and its separate 'Enclose and Fill' tool; Clip Studio Paint ('Close gap', 'Area scaling', 'Refer to other layers'); Procreate ColorDrop (single threshold, no grow — and it is Procreate's most complained-about tool for exactly the halo reason).
- **Relevance:** This is a Win2D-friendly workload: the flood/dilate/blur stages are all separable and can run on CPU over a tile-cached reference composite. Ship tolerance + 'sample all layers' + grow(+stop at darkest) + antialias on day one. Shipping a binary flood fill will read as 'this app is a toy' faster than a missing smudge tool.

### ColorDrop-style drag-to-tune tolerance (no dialog, no re-do loop)
Procreate's ColorDrop: drag the active colour swatch onto the canvas and hold without releasing; after a dwell the threshold UI arms, a bar appears at the top of the canvas, and horizontal finger movement scrubs the threshold percentage with the fill re-previewing live (help.procreate.com articles zmlayd / zpejed). Mechanically this requires the flood to be fast enough to re-run at 60Hz on drag — achieved by computing the *distance field to seed colour* once on pointer-down (one pass over the reference buffer) and then re-thresholding that scalar field per frame, which is a trivial per-pixel compare. The threshold is then sticky for subsequent fills.

- **Used by:** Procreate ColorDrop Threshold; Clip Studio's fill tolerance is by contrast a slider in a palette (slower, and CSP users routinely undo-retry).
- **Relevance:** Highest ratio of 'feels magic' to implementation cost in this whole research area. The precompute-distance-field-then-rethreshold trick is ~40 lines and turns fill from a guess-and-undo tool into a direct-manipulation one. Do this in the beta.

### Canvas navigation as a single view matrix with gesture-solved similarity transform
Pan/zoom/rotate is one 2D affine (scale s, rotation θ, translation t, plus a view-only mirror flag). Two-finger manipulation solves the similarity transform that maps the previous two contact points onto the current two: s = |p1'-p0'|/|p1-p0|, θ = atan2 of the difference of the two vectors' angles, t from the centroid delta. Procreate layers three refinements on top: (a) 'Quick Pinch to Fit' — a pinch whose velocity exceeds a threshold snaps to fit-to-screen rather than applying its literal scale; (b) rotation snap with a dead zone — small θ within ~±5-7° of 0/90/180/270 collapses to exact, with hysteresis so it does not chatter; (c) rotation is gated behind an angular-motion threshold so a pure zoom does not slowly rotate the canvas. Krita and CSP add explicit 'reset rotation' (5) and mirror-view (M) keys. Flip-horizontal for composition checking is a VIEW flag only — it must never touch document pixels — and requires inverting the pointer mapping so drawing while flipped still works.

- **Used by:** Procreate (pinch/pinch-twist/quick-pinch-to-fit, four-finger tap = full screen); Krita (4/5/6 rotate/reset/zoom, M = mirror view); Clip Studio (rotate + flip on the command bar); Concepts (infinite canvas, so fit-to-content instead of fit-to-page).
- **Relevance:** Quill already has a notebook canvas with a view transform; Art Mode should reuse it and add rotation + mirror. The dead-zone/hysteresis detail is the difference between 'nice' and 'why is my canvas always crooked'. Flip-horizontal is a 20-line feature that artists rate absurdly highly — put it in the beta.

### Symmetry: mirror the input samples, not the rasterised result
Correct implementation duplicates each incoming stroke *sample* (position, pressure, tilt, velocity, and — critically — the direction vector) through each symmetry matrix and dabs all copies into the same stroke pass. Mirroring the finished raster instead is wrong for any brush with directional or textured stamping, wrong for anything with a blend accumulation buffer, and doubles the compositing cost. Modes: vertical axis, horizontal axis, both (4-way), and radial N-fold where copy k is rotated by 2πk/N about a pivot (some apps add a mirror per wedge → 2N copies). CSP models this as a 'Symmetrical ruler' object with a settable line and a division count, toggled globally by View ▸ Snap ▸ Snap to special rulers — meaning symmetry, perspective and other guides share one master on/off, which is a good UX invariant. In CSP symmetry also applies to fills and selections, not just brush strokes (help.clip-studio.com, Drawing while snapping to a ruler).

- **Used by:** Clip Studio Paint (Symmetrical ruler, arbitrary axis + N divisions); Krita (Multibrush tool + mirror-paint toolbar toggles); Procreate (Drawing Guide ▸ Symmetry: vertical/horizontal/quadrant/radial with rotational-symmetry sub-option); Sketchbook (X/Y/XY + radial, a marquee feature of the app).
- **Relevance:** Cheap (a list of 3x2 matrices applied in the stroke pipeline), highly demoable, and a genuine differentiator for a note-taking app adding art. Vertical + horizontal + radial-N in the beta; arbitrary-angle axis can wait.

### Perspective/ruler assistants and the 'lock at stroke start' rule
An assistant is an object exposing `project(point) -> point` and `distance(point)`. Vanishing point: project onto the ray from the VP through the stroke's ANCHOR point (the first sample). Parallel ruler: project onto a fixed direction. Ellipse/concentric: project onto the conic. The non-obvious production rule: at pointer-down, pick the single nearest/most-relevant assistant and LOCK to it for the whole stroke. Re-evaluating per sample makes long lines snake between two vanishing points. Krita adds a global 'snap-to-assistant' toggle plus per-assistant enable, and a snap-strength/'assistant preview' so lines can be softly attracted (lerp raw→projected by weight) rather than rigidly clamped; Krita's 2-point assistant bundles two VPs plus the vertical parallel ruler and draws square grid planes so the artist can see distortion before drawing (docs.krita.org/en/user_manual/painting_with_assistants.html). CSP exposes per-direction snap toggles on a selected perspective ruler via the Object tool, so you can disable the vertical while drawing horizontals. Krita also snaps ruler handles to exact H/V when Shift is held during placement.

- **Used by:** Krita (Vanishing Point, 2-Point, Parallel Ruler, Ellipse, Concentric Ellipse, Fish Eye Point, Spline); Clip Studio Paint (Perspective ruler 1/2/3-point with per-direction snap buttons); Procreate (Drawing Guides: 2D grid / isometric / perspective with up to 3 VPs, plus 'Assisted Drawing' as a per-layer property); Concepts (precision toolbar with a live-adjustable perspective grid, arguably the best on tablet).
- **Relevance:** Tier B, not beta — EXCEPT the degenerate case: a plain straight-line/parallel ruler and a snap-to-horizontal/vertical modifier, which is nearly free and covers most note-adjacent drawing. Procreate's 'Assisted Drawing is a layer property' model is worth stealing later: it makes 'why is my stroke snapping?' answerable.

### QuickShape / shape recognition via dwell-gating and least-squares fitting
Draw freehand, then HOLD the pen still at the end of the stroke. A dwell detector (movement < ~2-3 px for ~0.4-0.6s while still in contact) fires shape recognition on the accumulated point list: classify closed vs open (endpoint distance vs path length), then fit — straight line by total-least-squares/PCA on the points; circle/ellipse by direct algebraic conic fit (Fitzgibbon's direct least-squares ellipse fit is the standard, it is constraint-normalised so it cannot degenerate to a hyperbola); polygon by Ramer–Douglas–Peucker simplification followed by vertex-angle snapping. Procreate then keeps the shape LIVE: 'Edit Shape' exposes draggable nodes, dragging between nodes scales uniformly, and continuing to hold and drag adjusts scale and rotation before commit (help.procreate.com/procreate/handbook/guides/quickshape). Sketchbook markets the same idea as 'Predictive Stroke'. The dwell gate is the whole trick — automatic recognition without it destroys expressive line work.

- **Used by:** Procreate QuickShape; Autodesk Sketchbook Predictive Stroke; Concepts (fits everything to editable vectors by default, since its document model is vector); Fresco shape tool.
- **Relevance:** Extremely on-brand for a pen-first note app — Quill likely already wants this in note mode. Ship the line + ellipse + rect case in the beta with dwell-gating; skip node editing until wave 2. Note: it must be undoable back to the raw stroke in one Ctrl+Z.

### Selection + transform: mask representation, marching ants, and single-resample transforms
Selection is an 8-bit alpha mask the size of the canvas (or a sparse tiled mask), NOT a path — this makes wand/feather/grow/invert/intersect trivial and matches how fill and layer masks already work. The visible 'marching ants' outline is generated by marching squares over the 0.5 iso-contour, cached, and animated by scrolling a dash phase (do NOT re-extract contours per frame). Transform must keep a reference to the ORIGINAL pixels plus a single accumulated 3x3 matrix; every nudge/rotate re-samples from the original once. The common bug is resampling the already-resampled buffer per interaction, which turns a few rotations into mush. Preview at interaction time uses GPU bilinear; on commit, resample with a higher-quality kernel (Mitchell–Netravali B=1/3,C=1/3, or Catmull-Rom for sharper) — Win2D exposes this via DrawImage with a high-quality interpolation mode. Free-transform variants users expect: freeform (corner handles independent), uniform (aspect-locked), distort/perspective (homography from 4 point correspondences — solve the 8-DOF system), warp (mesh). Procreate splits these into four buttons at the bottom of the transform HUD plus 'Magnetics' (axis snapping) and interpolation quality (Nearest/Bilinear/Bicubic).

- **Used by:** Procreate (Transform HUD: Freeform/Uniform/Distort/Warp + Magnetics + Snapping + interpolation choice); Krita (Transform Tool with Free/Perspective/Warp/Cage/Liquify and a persistent 'transform mask' that stays non-destructive); Photoshop/CSP equivalents.
- **Relevance:** Beta needs: rectangle + freehand-lasso + magic-wand selection, invert/deselect, and move/uniform-scale/rotate with a single high-quality commit resample. Distort/warp/liquify are wave 2. Skipping selection entirely is not viable — 'move this bit over there' is a top-3 action in every session.

### Colour picker UX: shape, swatches, harmony, and why sRGB-HSV feels wrong
Two shapes dominate: hue-ring + SV-square (Krita default, CSP, Fresco) and Procreate's Disc (hue ring + saturation/brightness disc) with alternate 'Classic' (SV square), 'Harmony', 'Value' (numeric HSB/RGB/Hex) and 'Palettes' tabs behind a segmented control (help.procreate.com/procreate/handbook/colors/colors-interface). Harmony modes generate companion swatches by hue offsets: complementary (+180°), split-complementary (+150/+210), analogous (±30), triadic (±120), tetradic. The important technical point: HSV/HSL over sRGB is not perceptually uniform — sliding saturation at fixed 'V' swings perceived lightness wildly (a saturated yellow and a saturated blue at V=100 differ by ~70 in L*), which is the root cause of 'my colours went muddy'. Björn Ottosson's Oklab/Okhsl gives an HSL-shaped selector where L is actual perceived lightness and the gamut is normalised per-hue; several modern tools have moved to it. Swatch UX essentials: a recent-colours strip that auto-populates (not a manual 'add' step), one user palette, and a persistent 'previous colour' half-swatch so you can flip back and forth (Krita and Photoshop both keep FG/BG pairs; Procreate keeps a previous-colour split rectangle).

- **Used by:** Procreate (5-tab colour panel + companion window); Krita (Advanced Colour Selector, configurable shape/geometry, plus on-canvas selector on Shift+I / Shift+N / Shift+M); Clip Studio (colour wheel + colour set + approximate colour); Fresco (wheel + Adobe Color harmonies).
- **Relevance:** Beta: ONE selector (hue ring + SV square), hex entry, auto-recents (last 20, dedup by ΔE), one saved palette, previous-colour flip. Build it in Oklab-backed HSL if the extra hour is affordable — it is the cheapest way to make Quill's colour feel better than Krita's out of the box. Harmony wheel is wave 2.

### Eyedropper: composite-sampled, radius-averaged, and reachable without leaving the brush
Three invariants: (1) sample from the COMPOSITED visible image by default, not the active layer — sampling the active layer looks broken to every user; expose 'sample current layer' as an option only. (2) Offer an NxN average (1/3/5/9 px) because sampling a single pixel of an anti-aliased or textured painting returns an outlier. (3) It must be a modifier on the brush, not a tool switch: Krita = hold Ctrl (Ctrl+left = foreground, Ctrl+right = background); Photoshop/Fresco = hold Alt; Procreate = tap-and-hold on the canvas, which pops a split-circle loupe showing new colour over old, and releases straight back to painting. The loupe-with-old/new-halves is the detail that makes it usable on a touch screen where the finger occludes the sample point.

- **Used by:** Krita (Ctrl modifier, configurable radius + 'blend' amount that mixes into the current colour); Procreate (tap-hold loupe, timing configurable in Gesture Controls); Photoshop/Fresco (Alt/Option).
- **Relevance:** Beta. On Windows with a pen: barrel-button-press = eyedropper is a strong default (most pen barrel buttons are otherwise wasted), plus Alt for mouse/keyboard users, plus long-press for pure touch.

### Brush size/opacity quick adjust: on-canvas drag beats any panel
Three established idioms, all of which avoid a round trip to a panel: (a) Krita — hold Shift and drag horizontally on the canvas; drag right grows, left shrinks, with a live circular preview of the brush footprint at the cursor. (b) Photoshop/CSP — Alt+Right-drag (horizontal = size, vertical = hardness/opacity). (c) Procreate — a fixed vertical sidebar with two sliders (top = size, bottom = opacity) that is always visible and never occludes the drawing hand; grabbing a slider then moving the finger AWAY from the sidebar switches it to fine/geared mode so small movements make small changes. Procreate also maps Apple Pencil hover: pinch while hovering = size, slide while hovering = opacity. The common requirement across all three: a transient HUD showing the numeric value and a true-scale brush circle, which disappears on release.

- **Used by:** Krita (Shift-drag, plus Shift+I/N/M on-canvas selectors); Procreate (sidebar + geared fine mode + hover gestures); Photoshop/Clip Studio (Alt+right-drag); Sketchbook (the 'puck' — a draggable on-canvas widget with size on one axis and opacity on the other, arguably the best single-hand solution).
- **Relevance:** Beta must have at least one of these plus [ and ] keys. Sketchbook's puck is the best fit for a Windows tablet-first app because it works identically for pen, touch and mouse and needs no keyboard. Also: size/opacity must be remembered PER BRUSH, not globally — forgetting this is a top user complaint in every forum.

### Layer panel interactions and the minimum viable layer feature set
The features that are load-bearing (as opposed to nice): visibility toggle, opacity, blend mode, alpha lock (paint only where existing alpha > 0 — implemented as a per-layer flag that multiplies the destination alpha into the source coverage), clipping mask (clip to the alpha of the layer below — a group-scoped composite step), layer mask, merge down, duplicate, reorder by drag, and a thumbnail. Procreate's gesture layer is worth studying: pinch two layers together = merge; swipe right on a layer = add to multi-selection; two-finger tap on a layer = grab its opacity and scrub horizontally on the canvas; two-finger swipe right = toggle alpha lock; two-finger hold = select all non-transparent content of that layer as a selection (help.procreate.com gestures). On blend modes: apps ship 25-30 but usage is concentrated in ~6 — Normal, Multiply, Screen, Add/Linear Dodge, Overlay, and one of Colour/Hue. Shipping 6 well (correct premultiplied maths, correct behaviour with layer opacity and group isolation) beats 28 with subtle compositing bugs.

- **Used by:** Procreate (gesture-driven layer panel); Krita (full layer types: paint/vector/group/filter/fill/clone/file, plus per-layer alpha-inherit); Clip Studio (layer folders, reference layer designation which the fill tool then consumes).
- **Relevance:** Beta: paint layers only, no groups, no adjustment layers. Visibility, opacity, alpha lock, clipping mask, merge down, duplicate, reorder, delete, rename, 6 blend modes. Clipping mask + alpha lock are the two that unlock real workflow (shading, lineart colouring) for almost no cost — do not cut them. Groups can wait; adjustment layers are wave 3.

### Pen vs touch vs mouse: routing by PointerDeviceType and palm rejection
The correct default on a Windows 2-in-1 is asymmetric roles: PEN = draw, TOUCH = navigate (pan/zoom/rotate + gestures), MOUSE = draw with a pressure-simulation fallback (either constant 1.0 or velocity-derived taper — velocity-derived is preferred because a mouse-drawn line otherwise looks like a marker). This must be a settable preference because a meaningful minority draw with a finger. Windows specifics: an active pen reports hover/InRange BEFORE contact — Microsoft's guidance notes hover distance is what lets Windows predict intent and enable palm rejection (learn.microsoft.com pen-and-stylus-interactions). Implement your own gate on top: while a pen is in range, ignore new touch contacts entirely for ~500ms after the pen leaves range; additionally reject touch contacts with large contact rectangles. The eraser end of the pen (`PointerPointProperties.IsEraser` / inverted barrel) should switch to the eraser tool and switch BACK on flip — this is the single most delightful pen behaviour and users test for it. Barrel button (`IsBarrelButtonPressed`) is best mapped to eyedropper or pan. Also mandatory: consume `GetIntermediatePoints()` — the pen digitiser reports at 120-360Hz while WinUI raises pointer events at frame rate, so ignoring intermediate points loses over half the stroke samples and produces visible corners on fast strokes. And disable press-and-hold-for-right-click and edge gestures over the canvas.

- **Used by:** Microsoft's own Windows Ink guidance (InkPresenter defaults to pen-only input, with `InputDeviceTypes` opt-in for mouse/touch — i.e. the platform itself ships the pen-only default); Procreate (a global 'Disable touch actions' toggle plus per-gesture assignment in Prefs ▸ Gesture Controls); Krita (tablet settings panel with per-device pressure curves).
- **Relevance:** This is the highest-risk area for a WinUI app and the one where a Windows-native app can genuinely beat Procreate-on-iPad in a review. Intermediate points, eraser-flip, and a hover brush-outline preview are all beta-critical and all cheap. Do NOT use InkCanvas/InkPresenter for the paint surface — you need raw pointer events into a Win2D pipeline; use InkPresenter's *input model* as the spec, not its implementation.

### Reference images as a first-class, always-available panel
Two established shapes: a floating, resizable, always-on-top companion window (Procreate's Canvas/Image/Face reference window, which supports its own pinch-zoom and — crucially — colour picking directly from the reference image), and a docked panel storing multiple images embedded in the document (Krita's Reference Images docker, whose images are saved inside the .kra so the document is self-contained). Both allow the 'Canvas' mode: a mirror of your own artwork at a fixed small size so you can judge the composition thumbnail while zoomed in — this is the same cognitive trick as flip-horizontal and is cheap once you already render to an offscreen target.

- **Used by:** Procreate (Reference companion window with Canvas/Image/Face modes); Krita (Reference Images Tool + docker, images embedded or linked); Clip Studio (sub view palette with eyedropper from sub view).
- **Relevance:** Beta-worthy in a minimal form: one floating panel, drag-drop or paste an image, pan/zoom it, eyedrop from it. The 'Canvas' self-mirror mode is nearly free and is a delight. Persist references in the notebook document so notebooks stay portable — this matters more for Quill than for a single-file paint app.

### Undo: tile-delta command stack, not full-layer snapshots
A stroke on a 4096x4096 RGBA layer is 64MB if snapshotted naively; 100 undo steps is 6.4GB. The standard solution is a tiled layer store (64x64 or 256x256 tiles) with copy-on-write: at stroke start, mark the layer clean; each tile touched by the stroke is cloned into the undo record before its first write. A typical stroke touches a handful of tiles, so an undo step costs tens of KB. Krita, GIMP and Photoshop all use variants of this (GIMP's is literally called the tile pyramid / shadow tiles). Undo granularity must be per-STROKE, not per-dab, and gestures matter: Procreate maps two-finger tap = undo, three-finger tap = redo, and holding either scrubs rapidly through up to 250 steps — the hold-to-repeat is what makes gestural undo usable rather than a novelty.

- **Used by:** Krita/GIMP (tiled COW undo), Procreate (250-step limit, gesture undo/redo with hold-to-repeat), Clip Studio (configurable undo depth).
- **Relevance:** Architectural, must be decided before any tool is written — retrofitting tiled COW onto a flat bitmap layer is a rewrite. Also: bucket fill, transform commit and layer ops must all push onto the same stack, and Quill's existing note-mode undo stack should be the same abstraction so Ctrl+Z always means one thing.

### Stroke stabilisation / smoothing as a first-class brush property
Three algorithms, all cheap, that users perceive as 'this app has a good brush engine': (a) simple moving/weighted average over the last N input samples (low latency, mild smoothing); (b) exponential smoothing p_out += α(p_in - p_out) — one line, but introduces lag proportional to α; (c) the 'rope'/'pulled string' model (Krita's Stabilizer, Lazy Nezumi's Lazy Mouse): the drawn point trails the cursor at a fixed distance D and only moves when the cursor exceeds that distance, so the output is a chord-simplified path — this yields extremely clean slow lines at the cost of the brush 'catching up' at stroke end, which requires a finish-stroke flush. Krita exposes Basic/Weighted/Stabilizer plus 'Delay' (a dead-zone radius) and 'Finish line' (docs.krita.org/en/reference_manual/tools/freehand_brush.html). Separately, all of these operate on the resampled/interpolated spine — the stroke path should be fitted to a Catmull-Rom or centripetal Catmull-Rom spline and dabs spaced by arc length, otherwise fast strokes show dab clumping.

- **Used by:** Krita (three stabiliser modes + delay + finish-line); Clip Studio ('Stabilization' 0-100 per sub-tool); Procreate (Streamline, per-brush, plus separate Stabilization and Motion Filtering in the brush studio); Sketchbook (Predictive Stroke).
- **Relevance:** A per-brush 'Streamline 0-100' slider using the rope model is roughly a day of work and is the difference between 'my lines are shaky' and 'this feels professional'. Beta. Note the interaction with QuickShape and assistants: order must be raw samples → stabiliser → assistant projection → symmetry → dabs.

### Smudge/blend as a table-stakes tool, and why it is more than a brush mode
Smudge is a stateful brush: it maintains a small colour buffer (the 'dab pickup'), and each dab (1) samples the destination under the dab into the pickup buffer, blended by a rate/strength parameter, and (2) composites the pickup buffer back at the new position. Two families: 'dulling'/single-colour pickup (fast, gives a soft airbrush-blend look) and 'smearing'/full-tile pickup with offset (gives the dragged-paint look, the one users actually want). Krita exposes both as Colour Smudge brush modes with a 'Smudge Length' (how fast the pickup buffer refreshes) and 'Smudge Radius'. Procreate makes Smudge a top-level tool that reuses whatever brush is selected, so texture carries into the smear — an elegant reduction: it is not a separate engine, it is a per-stroke mode of the existing engine.

- **Used by:** Krita (Colour Smudge brush engine, Smearing vs Dulling); Procreate (Smudge tool = any brush in smudge mode); Fresco (smudge on the left rail); Clip Studio (Blend tools: blur, blend, fingertip).
- **Relevance:** Procreate's model — smudge is a MODE of the current brush, not a separate tool with its own presets — is the right call for a small team. If the brush engine already stamps textured dabs, adding a pickup buffer is a modest change. Borderline beta/wave-2: I would put it in wave 2 unless the brush engine lands early, because a bad smudge is worse than none.

### Text and vector shapes: the features safest to cut from a v1 art mode
Every reference app has text, but its role is peripheral in the painting workflow (it is load-bearing in CSP because of comics/manga, and in Concepts because of design annotation). Text is also disproportionately expensive to do properly: text layout, font enumeration, per-character formatting, editing hit-testing, rasterisation on transform, and re-editability across save/load. Similarly, true vector shape layers require a second document model (paths, strokes, fills, boolean ops) alongside the raster one.

- **Used by:** Clip Studio (text is central — speech balloons, vertical text); Concepts (vector-native document, every stroke is a path); Procreate (text added late, in 5.0, and remains minimal); Krita (vector layers exist but are the weakest part of the app by common consent).
- **Relevance:** Cut both from the beta. Quill is a NOTE app — it already has text elsewhere, and users who need a caption can place a Quill text element next to the art canvas or on a layer above. Adding a half-working text tool inside Art Mode buys nothing and costs weeks. Same for vector layers: keep Art Mode purely raster with a tiled document, and let note mode own vector/ink.

### Recommendations
- CUT LINE — TIER A (must-have for a usable beta, ~10 items, all done excellently): (1) brush engine with per-brush size/opacity/flow/streamline and pressure curve; (2) eraser as a blend mode of the same engine, plus pen-flip-to-erase; (3) tiled raster layers with visibility, opacity, alpha lock, clipping mask, merge down, duplicate, reorder, delete, and exactly 6 blend modes (Normal, Multiply, Screen, Add, Overlay, Colour); (4) bucket fill done properly — soft coverage mask, tolerance, sample-all-layers reference, grow with stop-at-darkest, antialias, plus ColorDrop-style drag-to-tune threshold; (5) eyedropper as a modifier (barrel button / Alt / long-press loupe) sampling the composite with an NxN average; (6) selection (rect, lasso, wand) + move/uniform-scale/rotate with a single high-quality commit resample; (7) canvas navigation: two-finger pan/zoom/rotate with snap and dead zone, quick-pinch-to-fit, reset rotation, and view-only flip horizontal; (8) colour: one hue-ring + SV-square selector (Oklab-backed if possible), hex field, auto-recents, one palette, previous-colour flip; (9) symmetry: vertical, horizontal, radial-N, implemented by mirroring input samples; (10) tiled copy-on-write undo/redo with gesture undo (and hold-to-repeat). Plus two infrastructure items that are not 'features' but are non-negotiable: correct pen/touch/mouse routing with palm rejection and GetIntermediatePoints, and import/export PNG + a self-contained document format.
- JUSTIFY THE CUT LINE: those ten cover the complete loop of 'sketch → line → flat colour → shade → adjust → export' for a single illustration. A user can finish a real piece. Everything below the line only makes an already-finishable piece faster or fancier. The specific things I am deliberately cutting that will feel painful — smudge, perspective assistants, text, adjustment layers, gradients, layer groups — are each individually defensible to cut because none of them BLOCKS finishing a drawing; whereas cutting alpha lock or clipping mask, which look like advanced features, actually blocks the standard lineart-colouring workflow and would make Art Mode feel like MS Paint with layers.
- TIER B (strong second wave, in priority order): smudge as a mode of the current brush; the remaining ~15 blend modes; layer groups; gradient tool (linear/radial with dither); adjustment operations as one-shot filters (hue/sat, curves, levels, blur, sharpen) before committing to adjustment LAYERS; perspective assistants (2-point + parallel + ellipse) with lock-at-stroke-start and per-assistant snap toggles; QuickShape node editing; selection refine (feather, grow/shrink, invert, transform selection); reference-image panel upgraded with the Canvas self-mirror mode; brush editor exposing dab shape/spacing/scatter/texture; layer masks; distort and warp transform; per-document canvas resize/crop; PSD export.
- TIER C (luxury, explicitly post-1.0): vector layers; text with full layout; liquify; filter/adjustment layers with live re-edit; animation timeline and onion skinning; time-lapse replay of the painting; colour harmony wheel and palette generation from an image; fish-eye and spline assistants; comic panel/frame tools; brush texture authoring and brush pack import; CMYK/colour management; cloud sync of art documents; 3D reference posing.
- ARCHITECTURAL DECISIONS TO MAKE BEFORE WRITING THE FIRST TOOL, because they cannot be retrofitted: (a) tiled copy-on-write layer storage — this determines undo cost, memory ceiling, and whether a 4096px canvas is viable; (b) the stroke pipeline order — raw pointer samples → resample/arc-length spacing on a centripetal Catmull-Rom spine → stabiliser → assistant projection → symmetry expansion → dab stamping — because inserting symmetry or assistants after rasterisation is wrong and slow; (c) whether Art Mode documents live inside a Quill notebook page or as a separate document type, since that decides the undo/save/reference-embedding story; (d) tool state is stored per-tool and per-brush, never globally.
- WINUI/WIN2D SPECIFICS: do NOT build the paint surface on InkCanvas/InkPresenter — treat Microsoft's ink input guidance as the behavioural spec (pen-only default, eraser tip, barrel button, hover) but take raw PointerPressed/Moved/Released on a CanvasVirtualControl/CanvasSwapChainPanel and drive your own Win2D pipeline. Consume PointerPoint.GetIntermediatePoints() on every move event. Disable press-and-hold-right-click and edge/flick gestures over the canvas. Use CanvasVirtualControl region invalidation so a stroke only re-composites the dirty tiles. Use a CanvasCommandList / CanvasRenderTarget per layer and cache the composite below and above the active layer so painting only recomposites three surfaces, not N.
- BEST FIRST DEMO (what to build first to prove the direction): brush + eraser + one layer + the drag-to-tune ColorDrop fill + symmetry + flip-horizontal. Those five make a 90-second demo that feels categorically different from every other Windows note app, and each is small. Then layers, then selection/transform, then colour polish.
- OPINIONATED DEFAULTS FOR A PEN-FIRST WINDOWS APP: pen draws, touch navigates, mouse draws with velocity-derived pressure; pen flip = eraser and flips back; barrel button = eyedropper; Sketchbook-style draggable on-canvas puck for size/opacity (works identically for pen, touch and mouse, needs no keyboard); [ / ] for size; two-finger tap = undo, three-finger tap = redo; double-tap with two fingers on the canvas = fit; hold-after-stroke = QuickShape. Ship a one-screen gesture cheat sheet in the app — every reference app has one and users go looking for it in the first ten minutes.

### Pitfalls
- Binary flood fill. A boolean tolerance test on anti-aliased lineart produces jagged fill edges AND a 1px halo of the old colour along every line. The fix is a soft coverage mask (two thresholds: full-opacity spread, fade-to-zero tolerance) plus morphological grow. Krita's 'Stop Growing at the Darkest and/or More Opaque Pixels' exists specifically because naive grow then eats the lineart it was supposed to tuck under — you will hit both bugs in that order.
- Fill sampling only the active layer. Users keep lineart on one layer and colour on another; if the fill's reference defaults to the current (empty) layer it floods the whole canvas and reads as a broken tool. Default the reference to 'all visible layers'.
- Flip-horizontal implemented as a document transform. It must be a view-matrix mirror flag only. Baking it flips layer coordinates, breaks any stored geometry, and cannot be toggled freely — which defeats its entire purpose as a composition-checking glance.
- Mirroring the rasterised result to implement symmetry. Wrong for directional/textured brushes, wrong for any stroke that accumulates into a buffer before compositing, and 2x the fill-rate. Mirror the input samples — including each sample's direction and tilt — into the same stroke pass.
- Re-evaluating which assistant to snap to per sample. A long line then snakes between two vanishing points. Lock the assistant at pointer-down and hold it for the stroke.
- Rotation gesture without a dead zone and hysteresis. Every pinch-zoom imparts a fraction of a degree and the canvas quietly drifts off-axis over a session. Users experience this as 'the app is crooked' and cannot articulate why.
- Accumulating resamples during transform. Nudge-rotate-nudge-rotate against the already-resampled buffer turns crisp lineart into mush in about six interactions. Keep the source pixels and one accumulated matrix; resample once on commit.
- Full-layer snapshot undo. 4096x4096 RGBA is 64MB per step. You will discover this only when someone opens a large canvas, and fixing it means rewriting the layer store. Decide on tiled copy-on-write before the first tool ships.
- Dropping intermediate pointer points. The digitiser reports at 120-360Hz, WinUI raises events at frame rate. Without GetIntermediatePoints() you lose more than half the samples and fast strokes get visible polygonal corners — which developers often misdiagnose as a smoothing/spline problem and try to fix with more interpolation, making latency worse.
- Treating touch and pen as one pointer type. Palm contacts become strokes. Gate on pen hover/InRange plus contact-rect size, and keep touch suppressed for a few hundred ms after the pen leaves range.
- Linear pressure mapping. Raw pressure→size feels weak and unresponsive on most Windows digitisers, and the low end is noisy. Ship an editable response curve with a sane default, and handle the pressure spike/hook at stroke start and end (a very short stroke should not begin at full width).
- HSV over sRGB as the only colour model. Dragging saturation swings perceived lightness enormously and produces the universal 'my colours went muddy' complaint. Okhsl/Oklab-backed selectors solve it for roughly the cost of two conversion functions.
- Eyedropper sampling a single pixel of the active layer. On textured or anti-aliased paint a 1px sample is an outlier and the picked colour visibly does not match what the user pointed at. Sample the composite, with an NxN average option.
- Shipping 28 blend modes. Users use six. The other twenty-two are surface area for premultiplied-alpha and group-isolation bugs that are hard to notice and embarrassing to ship.
- Over-eager shape recognition. Auto-snapping strokes to shapes without a dwell gate destroys expressive line work and makes the app feel like it is fighting the user. Gate on hold-still-at-end, and make one Ctrl+Z restore the raw stroke rather than deleting it.
- Global rather than per-brush tool state. Switching brush and losing your carefully-set size and opacity is a top-ranked complaint in every paint app forum. Same for per-tool state on eraser and smudge.
- Re-extracting the selection contour every frame to animate marching ants. Cache the marching-squares contour and animate only the dash phase; otherwise selections tank the frame rate on large masks.
- Rebuilding the whole layer composite every frame. Cache the flattened stack below and above the active layer so painting composites three surfaces, and invalidate only dirty tiles. Without this, a 4K canvas with eight layers will not hold 60fps and no amount of brush optimisation will save it.
- A stabiliser with no finish-line flush. Rope/lazy-mouse smoothing leaves the drawn point trailing behind the cursor; if you do not flush the remaining path on pointer-up, every stroke ends short of where the user stopped, which feels subtly and maddeningly wrong.

## undo-and-format

### Tile-level copy-on-write snapshots are the standard undo mechanism (NOT full-layer snapshots)
The canonical pixel store is a sparse dictionary of fixed-size tiles (MyPaint and Krita both use 64x64) keyed by (tileX, tileY). Every tile carries a `readonly`/shared flag. Taking a snapshot is O(number of allocated tiles) pointer work, not a pixel copy: MyPaint's `save_snapshot()` literally 'marks all the tiles of the surface as read-only, then just shallow-copies the tiledict'. When a paint operation later needs to write a tile, the accessor checks `if t.readonly and not readonly:` and clones that one tile into a private writable copy, leaving the snapshot pointing at the original. `load_snapshot()` swaps the live tiledict back to the saved one and diffs the two dicts to compute the exact dirty region to repaint. Net effect: an undo record costs (tiles actually touched by the stroke) x (tile bytes), and untouched tiles cost literally zero. A 64x64 RGBA8 tile is 16 KB; RGBA16 is 32 KB. A long stroke across a 4000x4000 canvas typically touches a few hundred tiles => a few MB, versus 64 MB for a full-layer RGBA8 snapshot of that canvas.

- **Used by:** MyPaint (lib/tiledsurface.py, `_Tile.readonly` + save_snapshot/load_snapshot — read directly from source); Krita (KisTiledDataManager + KisTile/KisTileData, 64x64 tiles, per KDE community wiki Krita/Tile_Data_Format and the KRA format docs); GIMP (tile/shadow-buffer based undo, developer.gimp.org/core/specifications/undo)
- **Relevance:** This is the single most important architectural decision and it must be made BEFORE the painting engine is written, because it dictates that the authoritative pixel store is a CPU-side tile grid. If Quill makes a Win2D `CanvasRenderTarget` the source of truth, every undo requires a GPU->CPU readback (`GetPixelBytes`) which stalls the pipeline and makes tile snapshots useless. Design: CPU tile store = truth; Win2D textures = a render cache invalidated per dirty tile.

### Stroke transaction lifecycle: one undo entry per stroke, tiles captured lazily on first touch
Krita wraps pixel edits in a KisTransaction; KisTransactionData is the actual QUndoCommand pushed on the stack. The transaction opens on pointer-down, the engine records the pre-state of each tile the moment it is *first* written within that transaction (guarded by a HashSet<TileCoord> of already-captured tiles so a tile is snapshotted at most once per stroke even if the brush crosses it 500 times), and commits on pointer-up producing exactly one user-visible undo step. Krita's newer engine deliberately moved away from per-memento hash tables — 'every KisMemento had to have two hash tables at least 4KiB each ... those tables had to be created every time a new transaction was requested' — to a centralized history store with three hash tables per data manager.

- **Used by:** Krita (community.kde.org/Krita/Transactions_Design; GSoC 2018 tiles3 work); GIMP undo groups, where nested groups collapse so only the outermost is user-visible
- **Relevance:** Do not snapshot on pointer-down (you don't know the footprint yet) and do not snapshot per dab (catastrophic). Capture-on-first-write inside an open transaction is the correct middle. Also adopt GIMP-style nestable undo groups so composite tools (e.g. 'fill + feather') read as one Ctrl+Z.

### Two-tier history: pixel snapshots for raster ops, command/operation records for structural ops
Layer add/delete/reorder/rename/opacity/blend-mode/visibility changes carry no pixel payload; they are stored as tiny command objects that record the inverse operation (e.g. RemoveLayer{index, layerRef} inverts AddLayer). GIMP notes many operations 'require very little Undo memory (e.g., changing visibility of a layer)'. Critically, a deleted layer's command record must hold a *strong reference* to the deleted layer's tile dict — because tiles are COW-shared and refcounted, holding that reference is free until something else diverges, and it is what makes 'undo delete layer' O(1). Repeated same-property changes are coalesced/compressed into a single history point (GIMP explicitly compresses repeated visibility toggles).

- **Used by:** GIMP (UndoType-tagged stack entries, gimage->dirty counter); Krita (KisNode hierarchy commands alongside KisTransactionData on the same QUndoCommand stack)
- **Relevance:** Both kinds must live on ONE ordered stack, otherwise undo order desynchronizes from user actions. Model as `interface IUndoStep { void Undo(); void Redo(); long MemoryCost { get; } }` with a TileSnapshotStep and a set of structural steps.

### Destructive filters and large-region ops use the same tile mechanism, bounded by affected region
A blur/levels/curves applied to a selection is not special-cased: the filter iterates the affected tile range, the first write to each tile triggers the same COW capture, and the whole thing is one transaction. The difference is footprint — a full-layer filter touches every allocated tile, so a single undo step can cost as much as a full-layer snapshot. Two mitigations used in practice: (a) evaluate the filter into a scratch destination and swap tile dicts wholesale (the old dict *is* the undo record, zero extra copy); (b) offer non-destructive filter/adjustment layers so the common case never writes pixels at all and undo is just a parameter-change command. Photoshop's Snapshots exist partly to give users a manual anchor before an expensive destructive op.

- **Used by:** Krita (filter masks / adjustment layers as non-destructive alternative); Photoshop (adjustment layers, Smart Filters, History Snapshots that persist regardless of history-state count)
- **Relevance:** Prefer the swap-the-dict approach for whole-layer ops — it makes an expensive-looking operation cost nothing extra. Budget accounting must still charge the step its true retained bytes so one full-canvas filter doesn't silently evict 40 stroke steps.

### Memory budgeting: dual limit (minimum depth guaranteed + byte ceiling), evict oldest first
GIMP uses exactly two knobs: 'the minimal number of undo levels, which GIMP will maintain regardless of how much memory they consume, and the maximum undo memory, beyond which GIMP will begin to delete the oldest items from the Undo History ... unless this would result in fewer points being present than the minimal number.' Each undo step reports a memsize; the stack maintains a running total; on push, evict from the *bottom* (oldest) until total <= budget OR count == minimum. Eviction drops the step's tile references, and refcounting frees only the tiles no newer snapshot still shares. Photoshop instead uses a pure count limit (default 20, max 1000) plus separately-purgeable memory, which is why users routinely blow out RAM.

- **Used by:** GIMP (docs.gimp.org System Resources prefs: 'Minimal number of undo levels' + 'Maximum undo memory'); Photoshop (History States 20 default / 1000 max, Edit > Purge > Histories)
- **Relevance:** Ship GIMP's model, not Photoshop's: a byte budget is the only one that behaves sanely across a 1024px sketch and an 8000px painting. Suggested defaults: min 20 steps guaranteed, 1 GB ceiling on a 16 GB machine (scale to ~1/8 of physical RAM). Expose live 'History memory: 340 MB / 1.0 GB' in the UI — it teaches users why deep history costs.

### Cold-history compression and swap instead of hard eviction
Rather than discarding old steps at the budget line, hold their tiles in a compressed form. Krita compresses tile data with LZF and can spill tiles to a swap file managed by the tile system, which 'is responsible for storing pixel data of a paint device in memory or in a swap file, retrieving the pixel data, and storing undo data.' LZ4/LZF on 16 KB RGBA tiles typically gets 2-4x on painted content and 50x+ on flat/empty regions, at GB/s speeds, so demoting steps older than ~N to compressed form roughly triples effective history depth for microseconds of CPU per tile on the cold path.

- **Used by:** Krita (KisTileData with LZF compression and swap file); Procreate (LZ4-compressed layer chunks)
- **Relevance:** A tiered policy is cheap to add later if the step interface exposes `Compact()` / `Rehydrate()` from day one. Use `System.IO.Compression` Deflate for v1 or K4os.Compression.LZ4 for speed; do it on a background thread so it never touches the stroke path.

### Redo semantics, the save marker, and the dirty counter
Standard linear model: a new action after undo truncates (and releases) the entire redo stack. GIMP tracks document dirtiness with a signed counter rather than a bool: 'zero means clean, positive values show operations since last save, and negative values indicate the user has undone past a saved state', and warns never to set dirty directly — always push an undo action. This gives correct behavior for the common case of undoing back to the exact saved state, where the title bar asterisk should disappear. Photoshop optionally offers Non-Linear History (undoing then acting keeps the branch), which is powerful but confuses most users and complicates memory accounting.

- **Used by:** GIMP (gimage->dirty semantics, developer.gimp.org undo spec); Photoshop (History Options > Allow Non-Linear History, off by default)
- **Relevance:** Implement the signed counter / saved-index marker; it's ~5 lines and eliminates a whole class of 'it says unsaved but nothing changed' bugs. Skip non-linear history.

### OpenRaster (.ora): the minimal, interoperable zip-with-manifest layered format
A ZIP whose FIRST entry must be `mimetype`, STORED uncompressed, containing exactly `image/openraster` (same trick as ODF/EPUB — makes the type sniffable from the first bytes without unzipping). Then `stack.xml` (UTF-8) whose root `<image w= h=>` sets canvas size and whose `<stack>`/`<layer>` elements carry `src` (a path relative to archive root, e.g. `data/layer2.png`), `x`/`y` offsets (layers may extend beyond canvas), `opacity`, `visibility`, `composite-op`. `data/` holds one PNG per raster layer. `Thumbnails/thumbnail.png` is required: non-interlaced PNG, 8 bits/channel, at most 256x256, aspect preserved, no upscaling. `mergedimage.png` is required since spec 0.0.2: the flattened render, 8 or 16 bit PNG, no decoration. Only STORED and DEFLATED compression are allowed; entry names are case-sensitive UTF-8.

- **Used by:** MyPaint, Krita, GIMP, Pinta, drawpile, Nomacs (openraster.org/baseline/file-layout-spec.html)
- **Relevance:** Ship .ora export/import even if Quill's native format is richer — it is the only free layered interchange format and costs maybe 300 lines. Also: adopt its two best ideas natively — uncompressed `mimetype` first entry, and a mandatory embedded thumbnail + merged composite so Explorer previews and other apps never need Quill's engine.

### Krita (.kra): zip + maindoc.xml + raw per-layer TILE files, not per-layer PNGs
Top level: `mimetype`, `maindoc.xml`, `documentinfo.xml`, `preview.png`, `mergedimage.png`, and `<DocName>/layers/`. `maindoc.xml` describes the KisNode hierarchy and maps each layer to a binary file (`layer2`, plus siblings `layer2.defaultpixel` and `layer2.icc`). The layer binary is a tiny ASCII-ish header — VERSION (2), TILEWIDTH 64, TILEHEIGHT 64, PIXELSIZE (4 for RGBA8), DATA (tile count) — followed by one record per tile: TILE_LEFT, TILE_TOP, compression algorithm name (LZF), compressed byte count, then a 1-byte flag (0 uncompressed / 1 compressed) and the payload. Only tiles containing data are written, so sparse layers cost nothing. Pixels are stored planar and channel-swapped: all B, then all G, then all R, then all A. `.defaultpixel` gives the colour of the infinite unallocated area — which is how Krita supports an unbounded canvas.

- **Used by:** Krita; third-party readers such as 2shady4u/godot-kra-psd-importer (docs/KRA_FORMAT.md) and Krita's own KisKraLoader -> KisTiledDataManager
- **Relevance:** This is the format Quill should copy structurally, because it is *the same tile layout as the in-memory undo store* — save and load become bulk tile serialization with no resampling, no compositing, and no format conversion, and sparse/unbounded canvases fall out for free. Note the two extra ideas worth stealing: a per-layer default pixel, and per-layer ICC profiles.

### PSD layering and its compression codec menu
PSD's fourth section, 'Layer and Mask Information', holds layer count, per-layer records (bounds, channel count/ids, blend mode key, opacity, clipping, flags, mask data, blending ranges, unicode name) followed by channel image data stored per-channel (not interleaved). Each channel block begins with a 2-byte compression word: 0 = raw, 1 = RLE (PackBits, the Macintosh ROM/TIFF algorithm, with a per-scanline byte-count table up front), 2 = ZIP, 3 = ZIP with prediction (horizontal delta filter applied before Deflate). PhotoshopAPI benchmarks report ZipPrediction 'usually reaches the best compression ratios and is faster than both Raw and Rle while only being marginally (~5%) slower than Zip'.

- **Used by:** Adobe (adobe.com/devnet-apps/photoshop/fileformatashtml); PhotoshopAPI (photoshopapi.readthedocs.io concepts/compression)
- **Relevance:** Two transferable lessons: (1) planar per-channel storage plus a delta/prediction filter before Deflate materially beats interleaved RGBA — this is exactly what PNG's filters do and why Krita stores planar BGRA. (2) Store the compression method as a per-block tag, not a global format decision, so you can switch codecs later without a format version bump.

### Compression choice for paint data: PNG vs raw+Deflate vs LZ4-class codecs
Three real options. (a) Per-layer PNG (what .ora mandates): universally readable, Deflate + adaptive per-scanline filters gives good ratios, but forces encode/decode of the *entire layer* even when one tile changed, and PNG encode is slow (tens of ms for a large layer). (b) Raw tiles + Deflate per tile (PSD's ZIP-with-prediction, essentially): random-access per tile, incremental save possible, ratios within a few percent of PNG when you apply a horizontal delta filter first, and channel-planar ordering improves it further because alpha and each colour channel are individually far more compressible than interleaved RGBA. (c) LZ4/LZF per tile (Krita, Procreate): 5-10x faster than Deflate at maybe 20-30% worse ratio — the right choice for autosave and scratch/swap where wall-clock latency matters more than bytes. SQLite's docs note a compressed archive database is 'the same size (+/-1%) as an equivalent ZIP archive'.

- **Used by:** OpenRaster (PNG); Krita (LZF per tile); Procreate (LZ4 per chunk); Photoshop (ZIP-with-prediction)
- **Relevance:** Recommended split: LZ4 per tile for autosave/journal writes (must be sub-100ms and invisible), Deflate-with-prediction per tile inside the ZIP for explicit Save (smaller files, user is already waiting), PNG only at the .ora/.png export boundary. Do not use PNG as the native per-layer container — it forecloses incremental save forever.

### Thumbnails, merged composite, and why both are non-negotiable
Both .ora and .kra require a small preview (`Thumbnails/thumbnail.png` <= 256x256 / `preview.png`) AND a full-resolution flattened `mergedimage.png`. The thumbnail lets file managers render previews without any format knowledge. The merged image lets *any* viewer — including a future version of your own app that can't parse an old layer encoding — show the user their painting. It is the single most effective forward-compatibility and data-recovery feature in the format: even a catastrophically unparseable document still yields the artwork.

- **Used by:** OpenRaster (mergedimage.png mandatory since 0.0.2); Krita (mergedimage.png + preview.png); Procreate (QuickLook/Thumbnail.png)
- **Relevance:** Write both, always, on every save. Also register a Windows Shell thumbnail provider later if you want Explorer previews. Treat mergedimage as the recovery-of-last-resort path: if layer parsing throws, open the document read-only from mergedimage and tell the user.

### Versioning and forward compatibility in a zip-manifest format
Because the manifest is XML/JSON and the payload is a set of named entries, forward compatibility comes from three rules used by ODF/ORA/KRA-style formats: (1) a version attribute on the manifest root, with major.minor semantics — readers refuse a higher MAJOR but tolerate a higher MINOR; (2) unknown elements/attributes and unknown archive entries are preserved on round-trip rather than dropped, so an older build resaving a newer document doesn't silently destroy features it doesn't understand; (3) new features are additive and default-safe, so a missing attribute has a defined meaning. ORA's own history is instructive: mergedimage.png went from optional to mandatory at 0.0.2, i.e. they used the version to tighten guarantees.

- **Used by:** OpenRaster (spec version 0.0.6, mimetype-first entry for sniffing); ODF/OOXML lineage that .kra explicitly follows ('based on the open document standard')
- **Relevance:** Put `formatVersion: {major, minor}` in the manifest on day one, decide the unknown-entry preservation policy on day one (it's near-impossible to retrofit), and gate opening with a clear 'this file was made with a newer Quill' dialog rather than a parse exception.

### THE CORRUPTION BUG: a ZIP cannot be updated in place, and writing over the original destroys it
ZIP's central directory sits at the END of the file, and entry offsets are absolute. Changing any member's size shifts every subsequent offset, so every real implementation rewrites the archive from start to finish: in 7-Zip and peers, 'adding a new zip entry ... will result a complete rewrite of the archive, from start to finish. Also, delete an existing entry, or update/modifying an existing entry will lead the same rewrite.' In .NET, `ZipArchiveMode.Update` opened on the destination path is a trap: it buffers the archive and rewrites it on Dispose — if the process is killed, the machine loses power, or ANY exception is thrown between truncation and completion, the user's file is left truncated or zero-filled. The identical failure mode is documented for plain in-place config writes: data was 'cut off after the file had been zero'd, but before some internal cache had been flushed to disk, resulting in lots of NULLs.' Additionally, on Windows the file is opened with the destination path held for the whole (potentially multi-second) write, so antivirus, OneDrive sync, or a crash mid-write all land on the live document.

- **Used by:** Universal — the 7-Zip discussion on rewriting archives, and antonymale.co.uk/windows-atomic-file-writes.html on the zero'd-file failure
- **Relevance:** This is precisely how the previous attempt destroyed real paintings. The rule is absolute: NEVER open a FileStream or ZipArchive on the user's document path for writing. Not with Update mode, not with FileMode.Create, not 'just for a quick metadata tweak'. There should be exactly one code path in the entire app that writes to a .quill path, and it should be the atomic replace helper below.

### THE FIX: write to a sibling temp file, flush to stable storage, then atomically replace
Five steps, in this order, none optional. (1) Create the temp in the SAME DIRECTORY as the target (same volume — a cross-volume replace degrades into a non-atomic copy), e.g. `<dir>/.quill-save-<guid>.tmp`, with a hidden attribute. (2) Write the complete new ZIP into it via `ZipArchiveMode.Create`. (3) Force it to stable storage before it becomes the document — `fileStream.Flush(flushToDisk: true)`, which maps to Win32 `FlushFileBuffers`; without this the atomic rename is atomic but not durable, since 'write() typically copies into the page cache and does not mean the data reached stable storage', and you can end up with a correctly-named file full of zeros. (4) Atomically swap: `File.Replace(temp, destination, backupPath)`, which on Windows calls the Win32 `ReplaceFile` API — Microsoft's documented replacement for Transactional NTFS, where changes are 'completely applied or not applied at all' — and on Unix uses POSIX `rename()`, whose atomicity is required by the specification. `ReplaceFile` additionally preserves the destination's ACLs, attributes, and creation time and can write a backup copy in one call. (5) Delete the backup only after Replace returns. Note Microsoft explicitly discourages Transactional NTFS ('TxF may not be available in future versions'), so ReplaceFile/MoveFileEx is the supported path. `MoveFileEx` with `MOVEFILE_REPLACE_EXISTING | MOVEFILE_WRITE_THROUGH` is the lower-level equivalent when there is no existing destination metadata to preserve.

- **Used by:** Win32 ReplaceFile / MoveFileEx (Microsoft, via antonymale.co.uk analysis); .NET File.Replace internals (zenn.dev analysis of the runtime source); Python atomicwrites; every serious document app
- **Relevance:** Concrete .NET 8 gotchas to encode: `File.Replace` throws `FileNotFoundException` if the destination does not exist, so FIRST SAVE must use `File.Move(temp, dest)` instead — branch on `File.Exists`. `File.Replace` throws on cross-volume, so never use `Path.GetTempPath()` for the staging file. Transient `IOException` with HRESULT 0x20/0x21 (sharing/lock violation) from antivirus or OneDrive is common — retry 3-5 times with 50-200ms backoff before surfacing an error. On failure at ANY step, delete the temp and leave the original completely untouched; never delete-then-move, which opens a window where the document does not exist.

### Autosave files and recovery journals: the second line of defence
Krita's model is a good template with three distinct artifacts. (a) Autosave: for an unsaved document, a hidden file in the temp dir named like `.krita-5124-document_1-autosave.kra` (PID embedded so concurrent instances don't collide); for a named document, `.myimage.kra-autosave.kra` beside the original. Written on a timer, itself via the atomic-replace pattern. On next launch, opening the original prompts 'a newer autosave exists, open it instead?'; accepting opens the autosave under the original's name and removes the autosave. A clean exit deletes the autosave, so its mere existence is the crash signal — no separate lock file needed. (b) Backup: on each successful save the previous version is moved aside to `file.kra~`, giving one generation of rollback. (c) A finer-grained option for painting specifically is an append-only journal: after each committed stroke, append {layerId, tile coords, LZ4 tile bytes} to a sidecar log and fsync; on crash, replay the log onto the last full snapshot. This bounds data loss to one stroke instead of one autosave interval, and appends are cheap and crash-safe by construction because a torn final record is simply discarded at replay.

- **Used by:** Krita (docs.krita.org/en/user_manual/autosave.html — autosave naming, PID, the recovery prompt, and the `~` backup file); SQLite's rollback journal / WAL as the general pattern (sqlite.org/atomiccommit.html, sqlite.org/wal.html)
- **Relevance:** Autosave interval ~5 min is fine only if you also have the stroke journal; without it, artists lose real work. The journal is not exotic — it's an append + flush per stroke commit, and Quill already has a natural transaction boundary at pointer-up. Also: autosave must go through the same atomic path, or a crash *during autosave* becomes the new corruption source.

### The SQLite-as-document-format alternative to a ZIP container
SQLite explicitly positions itself as an application file format: writes are atomic across application, OS, and power failures because 'database writers write a copy of those portions of the database file that they are going to modify into a second file, the journal file, before writing to the database file'. The pitch against ZIP/pile-of-files is precise: an SQLite archive with compressed blobs is 'the same size (+/-1%) as an equivalent ZIP archive, and it has the advantage of being able to update individual files without rewriting the entire document.' For a tiled painting app the schema is natural: a `tiles(layer_id, x, y, codec, bytes)` table plus `layers`, `document`, `thumbnail` — saving a stroke means updating a few dozen rows inside one transaction, not rewriting a 400 MB archive.

- **Used by:** SQLite documentation (sqlite.org/appfileformat.html, atomiccommit.html); adopted in practice by Fossil, and by many DCC and note apps for exactly the incremental-write property
- **Relevance:** Worth a serious look for Quill's NATIVE format, since Quill is a note app that likely already has a database. It converts 'save a huge painting' from an O(document) rewrite into an O(changed tiles) transaction, and it makes crash safety the library's problem rather than yours. Trade-offs: files aren't inspectable with a zip tool, and you still need ZIP-based .ora/.kra-style export for interchange. If you choose ZIP anyway, the atomic-replace discipline above becomes mandatory rather than merely advisable.

### Recommendations
- Make a CPU-side sparse tile grid (64x64, RGBA8 or RGBA16 premultiplied) the authoritative pixel store, with reference-counted, copy-on-write tiles carrying an immutable/readonly flag. Win2D surfaces are a render cache only. Every other decision in this document depends on this one, and it cannot be retrofitted.
- Implement undo as tile-level COW snapshots: open a transaction on pointer-down, capture each tile's pre-state exactly once on first write (guarded by a HashSet<TileCoord>), commit one undo step on pointer-up. Never snapshot whole layers, never snapshot per dab.
- Put pixel steps and structural steps (layer add/delete/reorder/property) on a single ordered stack behind one IUndoStep interface exposing Undo/Redo and a MemoryCost in bytes. Support nestable undo groups so composite tools produce one Ctrl+Z.
- Budget history with GIMP's two knobs, not Photoshop's one: guarantee a minimum step count (~20) regardless of size, and evict oldest-first above a byte ceiling (~1/8 of physical RAM, default 1 GB). Surface live usage in the UI.
- Design IUndoStep with Compact()/Rehydrate() from day one so cold history can be LZ4-compressed (and later spilled to disk) on a background thread instead of being discarded outright.
- Track document dirtiness with GIMP's signed counter / saved-stack-index marker so undoing back to the saved state correctly clears the modified indicator.
- Prefer non-destructive adjustment/filter layers wherever feasible; for genuinely destructive whole-layer ops, evaluate into a scratch tile dict and swap dicts, so the old dict IS the undo record at zero extra cost.
- For the native format, structurally copy .kra rather than .ora: a container holding a manifest plus per-layer binary tile files whose layout matches the in-memory tile store, so save/load is bulk tile serialization with no compositing or resampling. Include a per-layer default pixel and per-layer ICC profile.
- Store tiles planar per-channel with a horizontal prediction/delta filter before compression (PSD's ZIP-with-prediction, and the reason PNG filters work). Tag the codec per tile block, not globally, so codecs can change without a format version bump. Use LZ4 for autosave/journal, Deflate-with-prediction for explicit save, PNG only at export.
- Always write BOTH a <=256x256 thumbnail and a full-resolution merged composite into every saved document. The merged image is your last-resort recovery path when layer parsing fails, and the thumbnail is what file managers need.
- Put {major, minor} formatVersion in the manifest immediately, refuse higher major / tolerate higher minor, and decide the unknown-entry-and-attribute preservation policy on day one so older builds cannot silently strip newer features on resave.
- ABSOLUTE RULE: never open a write handle or ZipArchive on a user document path. Route every write through exactly one AtomicSave helper: stage to a hidden sibling temp file in the same directory, write fully, call Flush(flushToDisk: true), then File.Replace(temp, dest, backup) — falling back to File.Move(temp, dest) when the destination does not yet exist. Delete the backup only after Replace returns. On any failure, delete the temp and leave the original byte-for-byte untouched.
- Never use ZipArchiveMode.Update on the document, and never use Path.GetTempPath() for the staging file (File.Replace requires the same volume). Wrap the replace in a 3-5 attempt retry with 50-200ms backoff to survive transient sharing/lock violations from antivirus and OneDrive.
- Ship autosave (hidden sibling or temp-dir file, PID in the name, deleted on clean exit so its presence is the crash signal, recovery prompt on next open of the original) plus one generation of backup (file.quill~ written by File.Replace's backup parameter). Autosave must itself use the atomic path.
- Add an append-only per-stroke journal (layerId + tile coords + LZ4 tile bytes, appended and flushed at each stroke commit, replayed onto the last snapshot at recovery) so a crash costs one stroke rather than one autosave interval. Quill already has the transaction boundary at pointer-up.
- Seriously evaluate SQLite as the native container instead of ZIP: it gives crash-atomic commits from the library, turns saving into an O(changed tiles) transaction instead of an O(document) archive rewrite, and matches file sizes within ~1% of ZIP. Keep an .ora exporter regardless, for interchange.
- Write a crash test into CI before shipping: save a large document in a loop while a supervisor kills the process at randomized offsets, then assert on every restart that the document opens and equals either the previous or the new version. Corruption bugs are invisible until they are catastrophic.

### Pitfalls
- Opening ZipArchiveMode.Update (or any write handle) on the user's document path. It buffers and rewrites the archive on Dispose; a crash, power loss, or ANY thrown exception mid-write leaves the file truncated or zero-filled. This is exactly how the previous attempt destroyed real paintings.
- Believing an atomic rename alone makes data durable. File.Replace/rename is atomic with respect to the NAME, not the CONTENT. Without Flush(flushToDisk: true) / FlushFileBuffers on the temp file first, a power loss can leave a correctly-named document full of zeros — worse than a crash mid-save, because the good original is already gone.
- Staging the temp file in %TEMP% instead of beside the destination. File.Replace requires the same volume and throws on cross-volume; and even where a move succeeds it silently degrades into a non-atomic copy, reopening the corruption window.
- File.Replace throws FileNotFoundException when the destination does not exist, so the very first save of a new document fails if you don't branch to File.Move. Easy to miss because it only breaks the new-document path.
- Delete-then-move (or truncate-then-write) as a 'simpler' replace. It creates a window in which the document does not exist at all, and it discards the ACLs, attributes, and creation time that ReplaceFile preserves.
- Ignoring transient IOException (sharing/lock violation, HRESULT 0x20/0x21) from antivirus scanners, OneDrive/Dropbox sync, or Explorer's thumbnailer holding the file. Without a retry loop these surface as random 'save failed' errors on ordinary users' machines.
- Autosave written non-atomically. A crash during autosave then becomes the new corruption source, and it is worse than the original bug because it fires every few minutes without the user asking.
- Full-layer snapshots for undo. A 4000x4000 RGBA8 layer is 64 MB; twenty steps is 1.3 GB. It will appear to work fine during development on small test canvases and fall over on the first real painting.
- Snapshotting a tile every time the brush touches it instead of once per transaction. A single long stroke can cross the same tile hundreds of times; without a per-transaction 'already captured' set, memory and latency explode.
- Making a Win2D CanvasRenderTarget the source of truth for pixels. Every undo then needs a GPU->CPU readback that stalls the pipeline, and tile snapshots lose all their benefit. The tile store must be CPU-side.
- Copying the tile dict deeply on snapshot instead of marking tiles readonly and shallow-copying. The whole point of COW is that snapshot cost is proportional to tiles MODIFIED, not tiles ALLOCATED.
- Per-memento hash tables — the mistake Krita's old tile engine made and explicitly fixed: allocating multi-KB hash tables for every transaction. Centralize history storage in the data manager instead.
- Forgetting that a deleted layer's undo record must hold a strong reference to its tiles. With refcounted COW tiles this is free, but if you drop the reference, 'undo delete layer' resurrects an empty layer — a silent data-loss bug that looks like it works.
- Charging every undo step the same nominal memory cost. A single full-canvas destructive filter can retain as much as fifty strokes; without true byte accounting it silently evicts the user's entire recent history.
- Using per-layer PNG as the native storage format because .ora does. It forecloses incremental saving forever (any change re-encodes whole layers) and PNG encode is slow. PNG belongs at the export boundary only.
- Interleaved RGBA tile bytes fed straight to Deflate. Planar per-channel ordering plus a horizontal prediction filter is materially smaller for the same codec — this is why Krita stores planar BGRA and why PSD's ZIP-with-prediction beats plain ZIP.
- Skipping mergedimage.png to save space. It is the only thing that lets a future or third-party reader recover the artwork when the layer encoding cannot be parsed, and it costs one flatten you already perform for display.
- Dropping unknown manifest attributes and archive entries on load. An older build then silently destroys newer features when the user resaves — and this policy is effectively impossible to retrofit once documents exist in the wild.
- Shipping non-linear history because Photoshop offers it. It is off by default there for good reason: it confuses users and makes memory accounting and eviction substantially harder.
- Relying on Transactional NTFS (TxF) for atomic multi-file updates. Microsoft explicitly discourages it and warns it 'may not be available in future versions of Microsoft Windows'.

## layers-compositing

### Porter-Duff source-over is the only compositing operator a painting app needs by default, and its premultiplied form is what makes layer stacks associative
The W3C Compositing and Blending Level 1 spec gives the general operator as co = αs × Fa × Cs + αb × Fb × Cb with αo = αs × Fa + αb × Fb. For source-over, Fa = 1 and Fb = 1 − αs, giving non-premultiplied co = αs × Cs + αb × Cb × (1 − αs), αo = αs + αb × (1 − αs). In premultiplied form (c = α × C stored directly) this collapses to the single expression co = cs + cb × (1 − αs), applied identically to RGB and A. The premultiplied form is associative: over(over(a,b),c) == over(a,over(b,c)). That is the property that lets you cache 'everything above the active layer' and 'everything below the active layer' as two flattened buffers and composite only three images per frame while the user paints, no matter how many layers exist. The non-premultiplied form is NOT associative (it requires a divide by αo to renormalize at each step), which is why every serious compositor stores premultiplied.

- **Used by:** W3C Compositing and Blending Level 1 (w3c.github.io/compositing spec, §general formula and §simple-alpha-compositing); the associativity argument is spelled out in Bartosz Ciechanowski's 'Alpha Compositing' article. Direct2D's D2D1_ALPHA_MODE_PREMULTIPLIED is the default for render targets, and Win2D's CanvasAlphaMode.Premultiplied mirrors it.
- **Relevance:** Quill's Art Mode should store every layer as a premultiplied CanvasRenderTarget (DirectXPixelFormat.B8G8R8A8UIntNormalized + CanvasAlphaMode.Premultiplied). Win2D already hands you this: CanvasRenderTarget defaults to premultiplied. The above/below cache trick is the single highest-leverage performance decision in the whole layer system and it is only legal because of premultiplied associativity — bake it into the architecture from day one rather than retrofitting.

### Premultiplied vs straight alpha — the specific bugs each one causes
Straight (unassociated) alpha stores RGB independent of A. Its failure mode is interpolation: any bilinear/mipmap filter averages RGB and A independently, so a fully transparent texel whose RGB is black (the common default) drags neighbouring semi-transparent pixels toward black, producing dark fringes/halos at every soft edge. GPUs cannot premultiply before the texture unit filters, so this is structural, not fixable by shader code after the sample. Premultiplied alpha stores c = α×C, so filtering averages c and α consistently and the reconstructed colour c/α is correct — this is why 'GPUs prefer premultiplication'. Premultiplied's own failure modes are: (a) precision loss at low alpha — at 8bpc, α=8/255 leaves ~3 bits of colour resolution, so repeated unpremultiply/premultiply round-trips (which is exactly what non-separable blend modes and many effects do internally) quantise badly; (b) invalid states where c > α (superluminous pixels) which unpremultiplication turns into values > 1.0 that then clamp; (c) you cannot losslessly recover the colour of a fully transparent pixel, which breaks 'recolour then raise alpha' workflows. Erasing to α=0 destroys hue information permanently.

- **Used by:** Real-Time Rendering blog 'GPUs prefer premultiplication' (realtimerendering.com); Inigo Quilez 'Premultiplied Alpha vs Not-Premultiplied Alpha blending' (iquilezles.org/articles/premultipliedalpha); the dark-fringe-on-downscale symptom is a recurring Adobe Substance Designer bug report. Win2D's own EffectPrecision doc warns that built-in effects produce premultiplied output but clamping is reasoned about in unpremultiplied space.
- **Relevance:** Two concrete rules for Quill. (1) Never expose straight alpha internally; convert only at PNG import/export boundaries (Win2D: CanvasAlphaMode.Straight on the loaded bitmap, or a PremultiplyEffect/UnPremultiplyEffect pair). (2) If Quill supports high layer counts with low-opacity airbrush work, 8bpc premultiplied will visibly band. Win2D lets you raise intermediates via CanvasDrawingSession.EffectBufferPrecision or per-effect BufferPrecision to Precision16Float — do this for the composite chain even if layer storage stays 8bpc. Check IsBufferPrecisionSupported first.

### Blend modes are a modification to the source colour BEFORE source-over, not a replacement for it — and this is where most hand-rolled implementations get alpha wrong
The spec composes blending and compositing in two stages. First the blend function B(Cb, Cs) is computed on UNPREMULTIPLIED colours. Then the blended colour is weighted by backdrop alpha: Cs' = (1 − αb) × Cs + αb × B(Cb, Cs). Only then is ordinary source-over run using Cs' in place of Cs. The middle step is the one people omit. Its meaning: where the backdrop is transparent there is nothing to blend with, so the source must pass through unmodified — a Multiply layer over empty canvas must show its own colour, not black. Direct2D states the fully expanded equivalent for its Blend effect: OpRGB = f(FRGB, BRGB) × FA × BA + FRGB × FA × (1 − BA) + BRGB × BA × (1 − FA), with OA = FA × (1 − BA) + BA. Note the three terms are exactly 'both present → blend', 'source only → source', 'backdrop only → backdrop'. Alpha is always plain source-over regardless of blend mode; no separable blend mode ever changes output alpha.

- **Used by:** W3C Compositing and Blending Level 1 §blending; Microsoft Direct2D 'Blend effect' documentation (learn.microsoft.com/windows/win32/direct2d/blend) which publishes the expanded three-term form.
- **Relevance:** If Quill hand-writes any blend mode as a PixelShaderEffect, implement exactly this three-term form and unpremultiply the inputs before calling f(). The single most common visible bug — 'my Multiply layer turns the transparent areas black' — is the missing (1 − αb) term. Also: because alpha output is unconditionally source-over, a blend-mode layer can never make the canvas more transparent; only an eraser (DestinationOut) can.

### The separable blend-mode formulas, exactly as specified
All operate per-channel on unpremultiplied [0,1] values. Multiply: B = Cb × Cs. Screen: B = Cb + Cs − Cb×Cs. Darken: min(Cb,Cs). Lighten: max(Cb,Cs). HardLight: if Cs ≤ 0.5 then Multiply(Cb, 2Cs) else Screen(Cb, 2Cs − 1). Overlay: B = HardLight(Cs, Cb) — i.e. the same function with arguments swapped, which is why Overlay and HardLight are duals. ColorDodge: if Cb == 0 → 0; else if Cs == 1 → 1; else min(1, Cb/(1 − Cs)). ColorBurn: if Cb == 1 → 1; else if Cs == 0 → 0; else 1 − min(1, (1 − Cb)/Cs). Note both dodge/burn need those explicit guard clauses — the naive division produces NaN/Inf at the endpoints and this is a classic source of black or white speckle. SoftLight: if Cs ≤ 0.5 then Cb − (1 − 2Cs) × Cb × (1 − Cb); else Cb + (2Cs − 1) × (D(Cb) − Cb), where D(Cb) = ((16Cb − 12) × Cb + 4) × Cb for Cb ≤ 0.25, else sqrt(Cb). The piecewise D() is a cheap polynomial approximation to sqrt near zero that keeps the derivative continuous — using plain sqrt everywhere gives a visible discontinuity in gradients. Difference: |Cb − Cs|. Exclusion: Cb + Cs − 2×Cb×Cs.

- **Used by:** W3C Compositing and Blending Level 1 §blending, which is normative for CSS/SVG and is the same set Adobe standardised in the PDF 1.4 transparency model.
- **Relevance:** These are all cheap and correct to implement directly in HLSL if Quill ever needs modes Direct2D lacks or needs them in a different colour space. But see the next finding — Win2D already ships all of them, so hand-writing is only justified for gamma-space control.

### The non-separable modes (hue/saturation/colour/luminosity) — and the fact that Direct2D implements them DIFFERENTLY from the spec
The W3C/PDF algorithm works directly in RGB using four helpers. Lum(C) = 0.30×R + 0.59×G + 0.11×B. Sat(C) = max(R,G,B) − min(R,G,B) — note this is the simple range, NOT HSL saturation. SetLum(C, l): d = l − Lum(C); add d to all three channels; then ClipColor. ClipColor(C): L = Lum(C), n = min channel, x = max channel; if n < 0 then C = L + ((C − L) × L)/(L − n); if x > 1 then C = L + ((C − L) × (1 − L))/(x − L) — i.e. desaturate toward the luma-grey until in gamut, preserving hue and luminosity. SetSat(C, s): identify min/mid/max channels; if Cmax > Cmin then Cmid = ((Cmid − Cmin) × s)/(Cmax − Cmin) and Cmax = s, else Cmid = Cmax = 0; Cmin = 0. Then: Hue = SetLum(SetSat(Cs, Sat(Cb)), Lum(Cb)); Saturation = SetLum(SetSat(Cb, Sat(Cs)), Lum(Cb)); Color = SetLum(Cs, Lum(Cb)); Luminosity = SetLum(Cb, Lum(Cs)). Direct2D instead documents a genuine RGB→HSL→RGB round trip (same kR=0.30, kG=0.59, kB=0.11 luma weights, H in [−1,5], S and L in [0,1], six hue sectors), followed by an out-of-gamut correction that 'minimally reduces saturation while preserving hue and luminosity'. The gamut step matches ClipColor in spirit, but HSL saturation, (max−min)/(1−|2L−1|), is NOT the spec's Sat() = max−min. So Direct2D's Hue and Saturation modes will diverge from Photoshop/Krita/CSS on any colour whose lightness is far from 0.5; Color and Luminosity should agree closely since they only depend on the luma transfer.

- **Used by:** W3C Compositing and Blending Level 1 §blendingnonseparable (identical to Adobe PDF 1.4); Microsoft's Direct2D Blend effect doc publishes the HSL conversion and gamut-correction approach explicitly.
- **Relevance:** This is a concrete, testable incompatibility. If Quill wants Photoshop/Procreate-matching Hue and Saturation layers, the built-in Win2D BlendEffectMode.Hue / .Saturation are not sufficient — write those two as a PixelShaderEffect implementing SetLum/SetSat/ClipColor. Colour and Luminosity can stay native. Budget a visual diff test against a reference image for exactly these four modes.

### What Win2D gives for free versus what must be hand-written
FREE — CompositeEffect exposes all 13 D2D1_COMPOSITE_MODE values: SourceOver, DestinationOver, SourceIn, DestinationIn, SourceOut, DestinationOut, SourceAtop, DestinationAtop, Xor, Plus, Copy, BoundedCopy, MaskInvert. BlendEffect exposes 26 modes: Multiply, Screen, Darken, Lighten, Dissolve, ColorBurn, LinearBurn, DarkerColor, LighterColor, ColorDodge, LinearDodge, Overlay, SoftLight, HardLight, VividLight, LinearLight, PinLight, HardMix, Difference, Exclusion, Hue, Saturation, Color, Luminosity, Subtract, Division. That is a strict superset of the W3C/Photoshop core set plus the Photoshop 'light' family — the only Photoshop mode genuinely absent is Normal, which is just CompositeEffect/SourceOver. Also free: OpacityEffect (layer opacity), AlphaMaskEffect (layer masks and clipping), CropEffect (bounds control), Transform2DEffect (layer transforms with selectable interpolation), and for adjustment layers ColorMatrixEffect, LinearTransferEffect (levels), TableTransferEffect and DiscreteTransferEffect (curves/LUTs), GammaTransferEffect, SaturationEffect, HueRotationEffect, TemperatureAndTintEffect, ContrastEffect, ExposureEffect, HighlightsAndShadowsEffect. MUST BE HAND-WRITTEN — spec-exact Hue/Saturation; any blend mode you want evaluated in a colour space other than the render target's; group knockout/isolation semantics; layer clipping-group grouping logic; the layer tree itself and its caching. Win2D's PixelShaderEffect accepts compiled HLSL (fxc) with 0–8 inputs, and ComputeSharp.D2D1.WinUI lets you author D2D1 pixel shaders in C# and use them as ICanvasImage, which is a far more maintainable route than checking .cso blobs into the repo.

- **Used by:** Win2D BlendEffectMode and CompositeEffect API reference (microsoft.github.io/Win2D/WinUI3); D2D1_COMPOSITE_MODE reference (learn.microsoft.com); Sergio0694/ComputeSharp wiki §'D2D1 support' and the ComputeSharp.D2D1.WinUI NuGet package which explicitly targets Win2D PixelShaderEffect interop.
- **Relevance:** Quill should build the layer compositor as a Win2D effect graph and lean on the built-ins. Reach for PixelShaderEffect only for (a) the two divergent non-separable modes and (b) any future linear-light blending path. Given the project is .NET 8 / WinUI 3, ComputeSharp.D2D1.WinUI is the right way to author those shaders.

### Layer opacity vs blend mode: the ordering is not a free choice
In the W3C/PDF model, layer opacity multiplies the SOURCE ALPHA before compositing: αs_effective = αs × opacity, and the blend result is then composited with that alpha. It is NOT a lerp between the backdrop and the fully-blended result. The two agree only when the backdrop is fully opaque (αb = 1). Over a partially transparent backdrop they differ, because scaling αs also changes how much backdrop shows through in the (1 − αs) term. Direct2D's BlendEffect has no opacity property at all, which forces the correct structure: apply OpacityEffect to the layer image first (it scales premultiplied RGB and A together, preserving premultiplication), then feed that into BlendEffect. Fill opacity vs layer opacity in Photoshop is a further split: layer opacity scales the layer plus its effects/styles; fill opacity scales only the pixel content and is applied inside the group before styles.

- **Used by:** W3C Compositing and Blending Level 1 (opacity folds into αs); Direct2D Blend effect has no opacity property, Win2D OpacityEffect is the separate node.
- **Relevance:** Fix the node order as OpacityEffect → BlendEffect → CompositeEffect and never offer a 'blend then fade' shortcut. If Quill later adds layer effects/styles, that is when the fill-opacity vs layer-opacity distinction becomes necessary; until then a single opacity is fine.

### sRGB vs linear-light compositing — where each is actually correct, and the exact lever in Direct2D
Physically, light adds linearly, so alpha blending, blurs, resampling, and gradients are 'correct' only on linear-light values. Gamma-encoded (sRGB) blending makes a 50% blend of pure red and pure green darker than it should be and famously produces a dark band where complementary colours meet; linear blending fixes that but makes mid-grey gradients look washed out to eyes calibrated on decades of Photoshop output. Photoshop's historical default is to blend in the gamma-encoded working space, with an opt-in 'Blend RGB Colors Using Gamma 1.0' switch — so 'artist-expected' and 'physically correct' are genuinely different targets and every painting app picks a default. Krita's answer is a separate scene-linear painting mode at high bit depth, and its docs note that HSY-based blend modes give more correct results in linear space but are hardcoded to sRGB/Rec.709 luma weights. In Direct2D the lever is the render-target pixel format: with B8G8R8A8UIntNormalized, stored values are sRGB-encoded and all blending happens in gamma space; with a *_SRGB format the hardware converts to linear on read and back on write, so blending happens in linear space — Microsoft's own docs note that at feature levels above D3D_FEATURE_LEVEL_9_3 sRGB-format blending occurs in linear space, and below it occurs in sRGB space. Crucially, linear-light work at 8bpc crushes shadow detail; linear demands 16bpc float minimum.

- **Used by:** Microsoft 'Supported Pixel Formats and Alpha Modes' (learn.microsoft.com/windows/win32/direct2d/supported-pixel-formats-and-alpha-modes); D2D1_GAMMA / D2D1_GAMMA_1_0 which exists precisely to let gradients interpolate linearly; Krita Manual 'Scene Linear Painting'; Krita's 2022 colour-management status doc (phabricator.kde.org T15221).
- **Relevance:** Recommendation: default Quill's Art Mode to gamma-space (sRGB-encoded, 8bpc premultiplied) compositing, because that is what every artist coming from Procreate/Photoshop/Clip Studio expects and it is the cheapest path. Design the layer storage so the pixel format is a per-document property, not a compile-time constant, so a future 16bpc-float linear mode is a document setting rather than a rewrite. Do NOT half-do it: mixing linear blending with 8bpc storage will look worse than either pure option.

### Alpha lock, clipping masks, layer masks and groups all reduce to composite modes and mask nodes — no new math
ALPHA LOCK (transparency lock): paint the stroke into the layer with Porter-Duff source-atop. Source-atop is defined as Fa = αb, Fb = 1 − αs, so the source only appears where the destination already has alpha and the destination alpha is preserved exactly. This is precisely alpha-lock semantics, available directly as D2D1_COMPOSITE_MODE_SOURCE_ATOP. Erasing is the dual: destination-out (Fa = 0, Fb = 1 − αs). LAYER MASK: a separate single-channel surface; the layer's effective alpha becomes α_layer × mask. Non-destructive because the pixel data is untouched. In Win2D, either store the mask as an alpha-only surface and use AlphaMaskEffect, or store it as greyscale and convert with LuminanceToAlphaEffect before masking. CLIPPING MASK: composite the run of clipped layers together first, then intersect the group's alpha with the base layer's alpha (source-in against the base, or AlphaMaskEffect with the base as mask), then composite that whole result onto the stack. The base layer's alpha acts as the clip; the base layer itself is still drawn normally. GROUPS: a group is a nested composite — its children are composited into their own buffer, then that buffer is treated as a single layer with its own opacity, blend mode and mask. Two spec-level knobs: an ISOLATED group initialises its backdrop to fully transparent (α0 = 0) instead of inheriting the parent backdrop, so children's blend modes cannot see outside the group; a KNOCKOUT group composites every child against the group's INITIAL backdrop rather than against the accumulation of preceding siblings. Photoshop's 'pass through' group mode is the non-isolated case, and it is the reason a group set to Pass Through behaves differently from the same group set to Normal.

- **Used by:** D2D1_COMPOSITE_MODE (SourceAtop, DestinationOut, SourceIn) reference; Adobe PDF 1.4 transparency model §transparency groups, where the isolated-group change is literally the single initialisation statement α0 = 0.0 and knockout is a subscript b selecting 0 vs i−1 as the backdrop; Procreate Handbook §Masks; Photoshop clipping-mask behaviour.
- **Relevance:** Big simplification for Quill: alpha lock costs nothing — it is one CanvasDrawingSession composite mode on the stroke commit path, not a separate code path. Clipping masks and groups both want the same primitive, 'composite a sub-run of layers into a temporary buffer', so build that as the recursive core of the compositor. Recommend shipping isolated-by-default groups (simpler, more predictable) and treating Pass Through as an explicit later feature, since non-isolated groups force the parent backdrop into the group's input and complicate caching.

### Non-destructive adjustment layers force the backdrop to be materialised, which breaks the naive linear composite chain
An ordinary pixel layer only needs itself plus the running accumulation. An adjustment layer is a function of everything beneath it: it takes the accumulated backdrop as its INPUT, transforms it, and the result replaces the backdrop within the adjustment's mask/clip region. Structurally this means the compositor cannot be a flat left-to-right fold — it must be able to hand the current accumulation to an effect node. In a Win2D effect graph this is natural (the accumulated ICanvasImage becomes the effect's Source), but it defeats certain optimisations because the adjustment must be re-evaluated whenever ANY layer below it changes. The standard mitigation is the same above/below cache: cache the composite below the adjustment, so painting on a layer beneath it invalidates the cache but painting above it does not. Adjustment layers also compose with masks and clipping exactly like pixel layers — a Curves layer clipped to a base layer is the single most common real-world use. Implementable adjustments map almost one-to-one onto Win2D nodes: Levels → LinearTransferEffect; Curves → TableTransferEffect (per-channel LUT with interpolation) or DiscreteTransferEffect; Hue/Saturation → HueRotationEffect + SaturationEffect; Color Balance / Photo Filter / Invert / Black&White → ColorMatrixEffect; Exposure → ExposureEffect; Brightness/Contrast → ContrastEffect; Gradient Map → a 1D LUT sampled by luminance, which needs a small custom shader.

- **Used by:** Adobe PDF 1.4 / Photoshop adjustment-layer semantics; Win2D effect catalogue (Microsoft.Graphics.Canvas.Effects); Win2D's own docs describe the effect graph as a DAG of transforms where each node's input may be any ICanvasImage including a previously composited result.
- **Relevance:** Design the layer model so a layer is a discriminated union — PixelLayer | AdjustmentLayer | GroupLayer — from the start, even if Quill ships only pixel layers in v1. Retrofitting 'this layer reads its backdrop' into a compositor built as a flat fold is a rewrite. The cheap v1 win is Curves and Levels via TableTransferEffect/LinearTransferEffect, which are pure configuration, plus clipping so they behave like artists expect.

### Layer transforms and the resampling trap
A layer transform (move/scale/rotate/flip/free-transform) should be stored as a matrix on the layer and applied at composite time via Transform2DEffect, not baked into the pixels, so repeated adjustment does not accumulate resampling loss. Two mechanisms matter. First, interpolation mode: nearest-neighbour for pixel art, linear for cheap, cubic/high-quality-cubic or MultiSampleLinear for final quality — Win2D's Transform2DEffect exposes InterpolationMode, and Direct2D's high-quality cubic is the closest to Photoshop's 'Bicubic'. Second, and critically, resampling MUST happen on premultiplied data or you get the dark-fringe artefact described earlier; Direct2D keeps everything premultiplied through the graph, so this is handled, but any hand-rolled sampling in a custom shader must premultiply first. Note that transforming a layer changes its bounds: Direct2D effects propagate bounds, and BlendEffect's output is documented as the UNION of its two inputs' bounds. A stack of blended, transformed layers can therefore grow the effective output rectangle far beyond the canvas, causing Direct2D to allocate oversized intermediates.

- **Used by:** Direct2D Blend effect doc, 'The output bitmap for this effect is always the size of the union of the two input images'; Win2D Transform2DEffect InterpolationMode; the premultiply-before-filter requirement from the GPU filtering literature (realtimerendering.com).
- **Relevance:** Insert a CropEffect clamped to the canvas rectangle after each layer's transform (and ideally after each blend) in the composite graph. Without it, Quill will silently allocate huge intermediate textures and lose performance for non-obvious reasons. Also keep transforms live until the user commits, then optionally bake — this is the difference between a toy and a real transform tool.

### Flatten and merge semantics are not the same operation and neither is 'just render it'
MERGE DOWN of layer B (blend mode M, opacity o, mask m) onto layer A must produce a single layer whose Normal-mode composite over any arbitrary future backdrop equals the original two-layer result. This is only exactly achievable when A is fully opaque everywhere B is non-transparent. Where A is partially transparent, the blend function saw a partly-transparent backdrop and the fused result cannot be reproduced by a Normal layer — every app accepts this approximation, which is why merging a Multiply layer onto a partially transparent layer subtly changes appearance. The honest implementation is: render B's contribution against A exactly as the compositor would, write the result into A, set A's blend to Normal and opacity to 1, discard B. FLATTEN of the whole document is easier because the bottom is usually an opaque background: composite the entire stack into one buffer. If the document has a transparent background, flattening must preserve alpha, and the result is exact because the whole stack's blend against 'nothing' is well-defined. Group flatten additionally has to resolve isolation: an isolated group flattens exactly; a pass-through (non-isolated) group cannot be flattened without changing appearance, because its children were blending with content outside the group. Merging also destroys mask and adjustment information — masks get baked into alpha, adjustment layers get baked into pixels.

- **Used by:** PDF 1.4 transparency model group-compositing and backdrop-removal machinery exists precisely to make nested groups composite exactly; the backdrop-removal step subtracts out the contribution of an inherited backdrop so a group can be composited once and still give the right answer.
- **Relevance:** Warn the user (or simply disallow) when merging down onto a layer with partial alpha under a non-Normal blend mode — or accept the approximation like everyone else, but do it knowingly. Undo for merge must snapshot both source layers, not just the result. If Quill adds pass-through groups later, flatten must either isolate them first or refuse.

### Practical Win2D composite-graph construction and its performance cliffs
The straightforward mapping is: for each layer, image = CropEffect(Transform2DEffect(AlphaMaskEffect(layerBitmap, mask))); then apply OpacityEffect; then accumulate = (blend == Normal) ? CompositeEffect(accumulate, image, SourceOver) : BlendEffect(accumulate as Background, image as Foreground, mode). This produces a chain of N effect nodes for N layers, and Direct2D will allocate an intermediate texture at most blend boundaries. Performance notes from Microsoft's own guidance: avoid primitive blends other than D2D1_PRIMITIVE_BLEND_SOURCE_OVER for the actual draw calls (non-SourceOver primitive blends also disable ClearType); use CanvasCommandList to record reusable subgraphs; set EffectBufferPrecision deliberately rather than letting it propagate from inputs, because where branches meet Direct2D takes the GREATEST precision of the inputs, so one 16bpc-float layer silently upgrades the whole downstream chain. Clamping is another sharp edge: many effects produce out-of-[0,1] values, and Win2D docs recommend inserting a ColorMatrixEffect with ClampOutput=true to force deterministic clamping, since effects without a ClampOutput property clamp inconsistently depending on precision.

- **Used by:** Win2D 'Effect precision and clamping' (microsoft.github.io/Win2D/WinUI3/html/EffectPrecision.htm); 'Improving the performance of Direct2D apps' (learn.microsoft.com/windows/win32/direct2d/improving-direct2d-performance); D2D1_PRIMITIVE_BLEND reference.
- **Relevance:** Concrete plan for Quill: build the graph once and mutate node properties rather than rebuilding per frame (Win2D effects are stateful objects and rebuilding thrashes the D2D effect registry). Combine with the above/below cache so the per-frame graph during an active stroke is only three nodes deep regardless of layer count. Rebuild the caches lazily on the first frame after layer selection changes.

### Tiled layer storage and dirty-rect propagation
Storing each layer as one full-canvas texture is simple but costs width×height×4 bytes per layer; a 4096×4096 document with 30 layers is 2 GB. The standard mitigation is tiling: the layer is a sparse dictionary of fixed tiles (64×64 or 256×256), with unallocated tiles meaning fully transparent, plus copy-on-write sharing so an untouched tile can be shared between an undo snapshot and the live layer. Krita's tiled data manager is the canonical open-source example. Compositing then walks only the tiles intersecting the dirty rect, and a tile that is transparent in the source layer is skipped entirely (source-over with αs = 0 is the identity). Dirty rects propagate up the layer tree: marking a tile dirty in a child marks the enclosing group dirty, and the composite only re-runs for the union of dirty regions. Undo interacts directly with this — the cheap undo representation for a paint stroke is the set of tiles the stroke touched, captured copy-on-write before the stroke commits, not a full-layer snapshot.

- **Used by:** Krita's KisTiledDataManager (tile + copy-on-write + shared tiles); the general dirty-region approach is described in Direct2D performance guidance and is standard across compositors — re-composite only regions modified since the last composite.
- **Relevance:** This is the decision that determines whether Quill's Art Mode can handle a realistic document. Even if v1 uses full-canvas CanvasRenderTargets for simplicity, put the layer pixel access behind an interface (GetTile/BeginPaint(dirtyRect)) so tiling can be introduced without touching the compositor or the undo system. Undo-by-touched-tiles should be designed in from the start; retrofitting it after shipping full-layer snapshots is painful.

### Recommendations
- Store every layer premultiplied (Win2D CanvasRenderTarget, CanvasAlphaMode.Premultiplied, DirectXPixelFormat.B8G8R8A8UIntNormalized). Convert to/from straight alpha only at PNG/file boundaries. This is non-negotiable — it is what makes the composite associative and what prevents dark-fringe artefacts on every scale and transform.
- Exploit premultiplied associativity with an above/below composite cache: keep two flattened buffers (everything above the active layer, everything below it) so the per-frame cost during a stroke is three images regardless of layer count. Invalidate on layer selection/reorder/property change. This is the single highest-leverage performance decision in the layer system.
- Default to gamma-space (sRGB-encoded, 8bpc) compositing to match artist expectation from Procreate/Photoshop, but make the pixel format a per-document property so a 16bpc-float linear-light mode can be added later as a setting rather than a rewrite. Never combine linear blending with 8bpc storage.
- Use Win2D's built-ins for almost everything: CompositeEffect (13 Porter-Duff modes), BlendEffect (26 blend modes, a superset of the Photoshop core set), OpacityEffect, AlphaMaskEffect, Transform2DEffect, CropEffect. Only hand-write shaders for spec-exact Hue and Saturation.
- Write exactly two custom blend shaders — Hue and Saturation — implementing the W3C SetLum/SetSat/ClipColor algorithm, because Direct2D implements these via an HSL round-trip whose saturation definition differs from the spec and from Photoshop. Use ComputeSharp.D2D1.WinUI to author them in C# rather than checking compiled .cso blobs into the repo. Add a visual-diff test for these two modes against reference images.
- Fix the node ordering as AlphaMask -> Transform2D -> Crop -> Opacity -> Blend/Composite. Layer opacity must multiply source alpha before blending, never lerp the blended result toward the backdrop — the two differ over partially transparent backdrops.
- Insert a CropEffect clamped to the canvas rect after every layer's transform and after each blend node. BlendEffect's output bounds are the union of its inputs, so an unclamped chain of transformed layers silently allocates oversized intermediates.
- Implement alpha lock as D2D1_COMPOSITE_MODE_SOURCE_ATOP on the stroke commit path and erasing as DESTINATION_OUT. Both are free — no separate code path, no extra buffer.
- Build 'composite a sub-run of layers into a temporary buffer' as the recursive core of the compositor. Clipping masks and groups are both instances of it. Ship isolated groups by default; defer Photoshop-style pass-through groups, which force the parent backdrop into the group input and complicate caching.
- Model layers as a discriminated union (PixelLayer | AdjustmentLayer | GroupLayer) from day one even if v1 only ships pixel layers. Adjustment layers read their backdrop, which a flat left-to-right fold cannot express — retrofitting this is a compositor rewrite. Cheap v1 adjustments: Curves via TableTransferEffect, Levels via LinearTransferEffect.
- Put layer pixel access behind a tile-oriented interface (GetTile / BeginPaint(dirtyRect)) even if v1 backs it with full-canvas render targets, and design undo as 'copy-on-write snapshot of touched tiles' rather than full-layer snapshots. A 4096x4096 document with 30 full-canvas layers is ~2 GB.
- Set CanvasDrawingSession.EffectBufferPrecision deliberately rather than letting it propagate — where graph branches meet, Direct2D takes the greatest precision of the inputs, so one high-precision layer silently upgrades the entire downstream chain. Consider Precision16Float for the composite chain even with 8bpc layer storage, to avoid banding in low-opacity airbrush work.

### Pitfalls
- Omitting the (1 - alpha_backdrop) term in the blend equation. The correct form is Cs' = (1 - ab) * Cs + ab * B(Cb, Cs) before source-over. Skipping it makes a Multiply layer turn transparent canvas areas black — the single most common blend-mode bug.
- Assuming Direct2D's Hue/Saturation/Color/Luminosity match Photoshop. They do not: Direct2D uses an RGB->HSL->RGB round trip, whereas W3C/PDF/Photoshop use SetLum/SetSat operating directly on RGB with Sat() = max - min (NOT HSL saturation). Hue and Saturation will visibly diverge for colours away from mid-lightness; Color and Luminosity should agree.
- Naive ColorDodge/ColorBurn division. Both formulas require explicit guard clauses (dodge: Cb==0 -> 0, Cs==1 -> 1; burn: Cb==1 -> 1, Cs==0 -> 0). Without them you get NaN/Inf and black or white speckle at the endpoints.
- Using plain sqrt() in SoftLight. The spec's D(Cb) is piecewise — the cubic ((16*Cb - 12)*Cb + 4)*Cb for Cb <= 0.25, sqrt above — specifically to keep the derivative continuous. Plain sqrt produces a visible discontinuity in dark gradients.
- Resampling or filtering straight-alpha data. GPUs cannot premultiply before the texture unit filters, so this is structural — every soft edge gets a dark halo on any scale, rotate, or mipmap. Premultiply first, always.
- Premultiplied 8bpc precision collapse at low alpha. At alpha = 8/255 only ~3 bits of colour survive. Repeated unpremultiply/premultiply round-trips (which non-separable blend modes and many effects do internally) compound this into visible banding. Raise intermediate precision, don't just hope.
- Assuming layer opacity and blend mode commute. Fading the blended result toward the backdrop is NOT the same as scaling source alpha; they agree only when the backdrop is fully opaque.
- Merge-down onto a partially transparent layer under a non-Normal blend mode is mathematically inexact and will subtly change appearance. Every app accepts this approximation — accept it knowingly, and snapshot both source layers for undo rather than just the result.
- Non-isolated (pass-through) groups cannot be flattened without changing appearance, because their children were blending with content outside the group. Isolate before flattening, or refuse.
- Forgetting that BlendEffect output bounds are the union of both inputs. Chains of blended, transformed layers grow the effective output rect without limit and cause non-obvious performance loss through oversized Direct2D intermediates.
- Relying on default clamping behaviour. Many Direct2D effects produce values outside [0,1] and clamp inconsistently depending on buffer precision; Win2D's own docs recommend inserting a ColorMatrixEffect with ClampOutput=true to force determinism. Reason about range in UNPREMULTIPLIED space even though effects emit premultiplied.
- Using a non-SourceOver primitive blend on the drawing session. Microsoft's performance guidance advises against it, and any non-SourceOver primitive blend also disables ClearType text rendering.
- Rebuilding the Win2D effect graph every frame. Win2D effects are stateful objects; mutate node properties on a persistent graph instead.
- Erasing to alpha = 0 permanently destroys the hue of those pixels under premultiplied storage. Any 'recolour then raise alpha' workflow will not work — plan for it or document it.

## brush-engine

### Dab/stamp model is the dominant architecture; a stroke is a resampling problem, not a geometry problem
A stroke is not drawn as a filled outline. The engine consumes a time-series of pointer events (x, y, pressure, tiltX/tiltY, timestamp) and converts each inter-event segment into N discrete 'dabs' (stamps) that are composited one after another. libmypaint's `mypaint_brush_stroke_to()` is the canonical API: it takes one new event, computes how many dabs are owed, and emits them via a `draw_dab(x, y, radius, r,g,b, opaque, hardness, alpha_eraser, aspect_ratio, angle, lock_alpha, colorize)` callback into an abstract surface. Everything expressive (pressure response, texture, scatter, smudge) is a per-dab parameter computation; the rasterizer itself only ever knows how to draw one soft ellipse. The alternative — vector/skeletal strokes (Hsu, Lee & Wiseman, 'Skeletal Strokes', UIST 1993) — deforms a reference picture ('flesh') along an arbitrary path ('spine'), preserving feature aspect ratios. It is resolution-independent and editable after the fact, but it cannot express per-dab randomness, wet-media pickup, or grain that accumulates, so no raster painting app uses it as the primary model.

- **Used by:** libmypaint (mypaint-brush.c `stroke_to`, `count_dabs_to`), Krita paintops, Photoshop brush engine, Procreate (Brush Studio's whole vocabulary is 'stamps'), Clip Studio Paint. Skeletal strokes: Adobe Illustrator art brushes, Expression/Creature House, Blender Grease Pencil. Ciallo (SIGGRAPH 2024, HKUST-GZ + Adobe) is the modern attempt to render vector strokes analytically on GPU while keeping raster-like texture.
- **Relevance:** Quill should build a dab pipeline, full stop. Design the core as `IDabSink.DrawDab(DabParams)` plus a `StrokeEngine` that owns event→dab conversion. Keep them separable so the dab sink can later be swapped (CPU tile rasterizer vs Win2D CanvasSpriteBatch vs custom pixel shader) without touching the dynamics code.

### Dab spacing is expressed as a fraction of brush diameter/radius, and is the single biggest quality lever
libmypaint's `count_dabs_to()` sums three independent spacing metrics into one 'dabs owed' count:
```c
res1 = dist / ACTUAL_RADIUS * DABS_PER_ACTUAL_RADIUS;  // scales with dynamic size
res2 = dist / base_radius    * DABS_PER_BASIC_RADIUS;   // scales with static size
res3 = dt   * DABS_PER_SECOND;                          // time-based (airbrush)
total = res1 + res2 + res3;
```
Dabs-per-radius R means dab centre spacing = radius/R. Photoshop/Procreate/CSP instead express `spacing` as a percentage of brush *diameter* (Photoshop default 25%; Krita default 0.1 i.e. 10%). Conversion: dabs_per_actual_radius ≈ 2 / (spacing_fraction_of_diameter). Krita additionally offers 'Auto Spacing', a quadratic law where spacing shrinks non-linearly as the tip grows, so huge brushes do not pay a linear dab-count cost while small brushes stay smooth. The dabs-per-second term is what makes an airbrush keep depositing paint while the stylus is stationary (Photoshop 'Airbrush/Build-up' toggle; Krita 'Airbrush' option; MyPaint `dabs_per_second`).

- **Used by:** libmypaint `count_dabs_to`; Krita brush tip 'Spacing' + 'Auto Spacing' (quadratic); Photoshop Brush Tip Shape → Spacing (% of diameter, default 25%); Procreate Stroke Path → Spacing.
- **Relevance:** Store spacing as a fraction of diameter (artist-facing, matches PS/Procreate/CSP mental model) and convert internally. Target ≤0.05–0.10 of diameter for hard round tips; clamp total dabs per event to avoid a pathological hang when radius→0. libmypaint clamps ACTUAL_RADIUS to [ACTUAL_RADIUS_MIN, ACTUAL_RADIUS_MAX] precisely to bound dab counts.

### Spacing artifacts: scalloping, corn-cob edges, and pressure-quantisation banding
Three distinct failure modes, each with a different cause. (1) **Scalloping / corn-cob**: with spacing ≥ ~0.5·diameter the union of circles has visible lobes on the stroke boundary. Cure is smaller spacing; the boundary error of a chain of circles of radius r spaced s apart is r − sqrt(r² − s²/4), so keeping s ≤ 0.2r puts the ripple under ~0.5% of radius, i.e. sub-pixel for most brushes. (2) **Density banding on speed change**: because spacing is distance-based, a fast flick emits fewer dabs per unit time; if flow is per-dab, the stroke gets *lighter* when fast. This is correct behaviour for a distance-spaced brush but is a bug if you mix in a dabs_per_second term without compensating. (3) **Overlap darkening / edge buildup**: consecutive dabs overlap by design, so naive source-over compositing at flow α makes the overlapped centre reach much higher alpha than α, and makes soft brush edges darken where the stroke passes over itself — this is the problem the stroke buffer solves (next finding). Ciallo (SIGGRAPH 2024) also calls out stamp-based rendering's texture-repetition artifact: a tiling grain stamped at fixed spacing produces a visible periodic pattern along the spine, and its memory/quality is resolution-locked.

- **Used by:** Documented as the motivation for Krita's 'Smooth lines'/super-sampled anti-aliasing paired with Auto Spacing; Ciallo §on stamp stroke limitations; libmypaint's `opaque_linearize` exists solely to cancel artifact (3).
- **Relevance:** Write a debug view that draws dab centres and dab count per stroke. Most 'the brush feels bad' complaints trace to spacing, not to the falloff curve.

### Interpolation and arc-length resampling of input points
libmypaint uses **linear** interpolation between consecutive events, with an explicit source comment that a nonlinear variant was too slow. The loop carries a fractional remainder so dab phase is continuous across events:
```c
float dabs_moved = STATE(PARTIAL_DABS);
float dabs_todo  = count_dabs_to(self, x, y, dtime);
while (dabs_moved + dabs_todo >= 1.0) {
  step_ddab = (dabs_moved > 0) ? (1.0 - dabs_moved) : 1.0;
  dabs_moved = 0;
  frac = step_ddab / dabs_todo;
  step_dx        = frac * (x - STATE(X));
  step_dy        = frac * (y - STATE(Y));
  step_dpressure = frac * (pressure - STATE(PRESSURE));
  /* ... advance state, draw one dab ... */
}
STATE(PARTIAL_DABS) = dabs_moved + dabs_todo;  // carry remainder
```
Carrying `PARTIAL_DABS` across events is what prevents a dab from being 'restarted' at every pointer sample, which would otherwise produce a dab cluster at each event position (a visible beading artifact at low sample rates). Higher-quality engines fit a **centripetal Catmull-Rom** spline through the last four samples (α=0.5 avoids the cusps and self-intersections that uniform Catmull-Rom produces on unevenly spaced input) and then walk it by arc length, since stylus sampling is time-uniform, not distance-uniform — humans slow down at corners, so raw samples bunch there. Arc length has no closed form for a cubic, so implementations either (a) subdivide the segment into k linear chunks and accumulate chord length, or (b) Newton-iterate on a precomputed length table. Pressure, tilt, and time are interpolated along the same parameter as position.

- **Used by:** libmypaint (linear + partial-dab carry); Procreate/CSP/Photoshop reconstruct with Catmull-Rom over the last four samples and place a stamp each time accumulated travel exceeds the spacing threshold, deriving stroke angle from local displacement.
- **Relevance:** Implement linear-with-carry first (it is ~40 lines and correct), then upgrade to centripetal Catmull-Rom over a 4-sample sliding window. WinUI 3 gives you `PointerPoint.GetIntermediatePoints()` — use it; without it you lose most of the pen's native report rate and the spline has nothing to work with. Never place dabs directly at event positions.

### Input → setting mapping: base value plus per-input response curves
MyPaint's model, which is the cleanest published one, is: every one of ~50 settings has a **base value** plus, for each of 9 **inputs**, an optional piecewise-linear **response curve** whose output is *added* to the base. Inputs are: Pressure, Fine Speed, Gross Speed, Random, Stroke, Direction, Declination (tilt from vertical), Ascension (tilt azimuth), Custom. Each curve is stored as (xmin, ymin, xmax, ymax) plus interior points, evaluated by `mypaint_mapping_calculate`. Crucially, several settings are in **log space**, so additive curves become multiplicative in linear space: `actual_radius = exp(radius_logarithmic)`, hence a curve on radius is a scale factor and a radius setting of 2.0 gives ≈7.4 px, 0.7 gives ≈2 px, 3.0 gives ≈20 px. `stroke_duration_logarithmic` likewise. Velocity inputs are not raw — they pass through a first-order exponential lowpass, documented on mypaint.app as `fac = exp(-dt/T1); out = fac*out + (1-fac)*in`, with per-setting slowness constants (`SPEED1_SLOWNESS`, `SPEED2_SLOWNESS`), because raw finite-difference velocity from a jittery digitizer is unusable. 'Stroke' is a ramp input that goes 0→1 over `stroke_duration` of travel, holds for `stroke_holdtime` (9.9 = infinite), then resets — this is how MyPaint does paint depletion and taper. Krita's equivalent is 'sensors' (Pressure, Tilt Direction, Drawing Angle, Fuzzy, Distance, Fade, Time, Perspective) each with an editable response curve, and each *multiplied* into the parameter rather than added. Procreate exposes the same idea as per-parameter min/max pairs plus a global Apple Pencil pressure/tilt curve.

- **Used by:** libmypaint `mypaint_mapping_calculate` + `update_states_and_setting_values`; Krita sensor system; Procreate Brush Studio Apple Pencil tab; Photoshop 'Shape Dynamics'/'Transfer' with Control = Pen Pressure/Tilt/Stylus Wheel and per-control Jitter + Minimum.
- **Relevance:** Model this as `BrushSetting { float Base; Dictionary<InputId, ResponseCurve> Curves; }` with a single evaluator. Do it once, generically, rather than hand-wiring pressure→size and pressure→opacity. Decide additive-in-log vs multiplicative early — additive-in-log (MyPaint) composes better when multiple inputs drive one setting. Tilt on Windows arrives as `PointerPointProperties.XTilt/YTilt` (degrees); convert to declination = acos(cos(xt)·cos(yt))-ish or the simpler sqrt(xt²+yt²), and ascension = atan2(yt, xt), so your curves match MyPaint semantics.

### OPACITY vs FLOW, and the stroke-buffer (wet layer) that stops dabs double-compositing
**Flow** is the alpha of an individual dab; it accumulates because dabs overlap, so passing over an area twice gets darker (spray-can behaviour). **Opacity** is the ceiling for the whole stroke; passing over an area twice inside one stroke gets no darker, and you must lift the pen and stroke again to build further. Krita states it exactly: Build-up mode 'will treat opacity as if it were the same as flow'; Wash mode 'will treat opacity as stroke transparency instead of dab-transparency'; and since 4.2 the two multiply rather than add. The implementation is the **stroke buffer / indirect painting device**: dabs are composited into a scratch surface (an 'indirect painting' temporary paint device in Krita, a per-stroke buffer in Photoshop), and that buffer is composited onto the destination layer **once** — either continuously with the buffer's own alpha capped, or once at stroke end. Three distinct cap policies exist and they look different:
  • **Build-up (direct)** — no buffer, each dab source-over onto the layer. Overlap darkens without limit up to 1.0.
  • **Wash** — buffer accumulates, and each pixel's final alpha is capped by the *stroke's current overall opacity at that moment*. Soft edges and internal grain still build up within the stroke.
  • **Soft** (Krita's newer mode) — 'each pixel is limited individually, preventing build-up within the stroke'; the cap is 'the opacity of that pixel in the stroke', i.e. a per-pixel MAX rather than a per-stroke scalar. Consequence: soft edges stay soft, textures do not compound. Krita's own docs note very soft brushes 'have a funky effect when you draw over the same area in a single stroke' and that this is intended.
Practically, 'Soft' is `dst_stroke = max(dst_stroke, dab)` in the buffer, whereas 'Wash' is `dst_stroke = min(over(dst_stroke, dab), strokeOpacity)`. Procreate's six Rendering modes (Light/Uniformed/Intense/Heavy Glaze, Uniform/Intense Blending) are points on this same axis — 'Glaze' modes are buffered/capped (a stroke lays one translucent coat), 'Blending' modes are direct build-up with wet-mix.

- **Used by:** Krita Build-up / Wash / Soft painting modes + indirect painting device; Photoshop Opacity vs Flow (+ Airbrush/Build-up toggle that adds time-based deposition); Procreate Rendering modes; Clip Studio's per-stroke vs per-dab opacity.
- **Relevance:** This is the highest-value thing to get right and the thing naive implementations always miss. Architecture: a `CanvasRenderTarget` sized to the stroke's dirty rect (grow it as the stroke moves), dabs drawn into it, composited to the layer each frame with `strokeOpacity`. For 'Soft'/max-alpha you need per-pixel MAX — Win2D's `CanvasBlend` enum offers SourceOver/Copy/Add/Min but not Max, so implement it with a small `PixelShaderEffect` (Win2D supports custom D2D1 pixel shaders) or with Min on the inverted alpha. Do NOT try to fake this by lowering per-dab alpha.

### opaque_linearize: the analytic alternative to a stroke buffer
MyPaint solves part of the same problem without a buffer, by pre-compensating the per-dab alpha so that the *converged* alpha of many overlapping dabs equals the requested opacity. If `dabs_per_pixel` dabs land on a given pixel and each has dab-alpha α_dab, the surviving transparency is β_dab^n, so to hit a target α you set:
```c
alpha = opaque;  beta = 1.0 - alpha;
beta_dab  = powf(beta, 1.0 / dabs_per_pixel);
alpha_dab = 1.0 - beta_dab;
```
The `opaque_linearize` setting interpolates between raw (0.0) and fully linearized (1.0, 'the opacity value above is for the final brush stroke'). `dabs_per_pixel` is derived from the spacing settings and the radius, so the compensation tracks size changes. Separately, `opaque_multiply` is the slot that pressure is conventionally mapped into — MyPaint documents it as 'responsible for stopping painting when there is zero pressure' — and `radius_by_random` applies an opacity correction alongside the size jitter so random-size dabs do not change perceived density. Limitation: linearization fixes *along-stroke* buildup for a straight pass; it does not fix a stroke crossing itself, which still needs the buffer.

- **Used by:** libmypaint (`OPAQUE`, `OPAQUE_MULTIPLY`, `OPAQUE_LINEARIZE` in mypaint-brush.c); exposed in Krita's MyPaint engine as Opaque / Opaque Multiply / Opaque Linearize; Drawpile's MyPaint brush support.
- **Relevance:** Cheap and worth implementing even if you also build the stroke buffer — it makes spacing changes not change apparent darkness, which decouples 'smoothness' from 'strength' in the UI. Note the pow() is per-dab; hoist it out of the inner pixel loop.

### Dab rasterization: hardness falloff as a two-segment linear function of squared radius
libmypaint's `draw_dab_pixels_BlendMode_Normal` evaluates coverage as a piecewise-linear function of the **normalized squared** radius rr (not of r — using rr avoids a per-pixel sqrt and gives a pleasing smoothstep-ish shape for free):
```c
float segment1_offset = (1.f)*(1.f-softness);
float segment1_slope  = -(1.0f/hardness - 1.0f)*(1.f-softness);
float segment2_offset =  hardness/(1.0f-hardness)*(1.f-softness);
float segment2_slope  = -hardness/(1.0f-hardness)*(1.f-softness);
// per pixel:
const float fac = rr <= hardness ? segment1_slope : segment2_slope;
float opa = rr <= hardness ? segment1_offset : segment2_offset;
opa += rr*fac;
```
So coverage is flat-ish inside rr<hardness then ramps to 0 at rr=1. hardness=1 is a hard disc (and MyPaint notes hardness=0 produces no output at all — guard it). Elliptical dabs come from rotating and scaling before computing rr:
```c
float angle_rad = angle/360*2*M_PI; cs=cos(angle_rad); sn=sin(angle_rad);
const float yyr = (yy*cs - xx*sn) * aspect_ratio;
const float xxr =  yy*sn + xx*cs;
const float rr  = (yyr*yyr + xxr*xxr) * one_over_radius2;
```
aspect_ratio must be ≥1.0 (1.0 = round); angle is 0–180 (180 wraps to horizontal). Krita instead offers three **mask generators** — Default (fastest, polynomial), Soft (user curve-controlled fade), Gaussian (slowest) — plus separate horizontal/vertical fade so you can make an ellipse with independent softness per axis.

- **Used by:** libmypaint mypaint-tiled-surface.c; Krita autobrush mask generators (Default/Soft/Gaussian, Circle/Square, Fade H/V, Density, Randomness); Photoshop round-brush Hardness slider.
- **Relevance:** If you rasterize dabs yourself in a compute/pixel shader, use exactly this rr-based two-segment form — it is cheap, matches artist expectations, and gives you MyPaint brush-file compatibility for free. If instead you stamp a pregenerated tip texture via Win2D, bake the falloff into a single-channel R8 texture at a few LODs and let the GPU do bilinear/aniso sampling.

### Anti-aliasing of dabs, especially small ones
Two problems. (1) The falloff already antialiases a soft dab, but a *hard* dab (hardness→1) produces a stair-stepped disc. libmypaint handles this with a 'pixel feather' concept: it converts hardness into a fadeout width in pixels, `current_fadeout_in_pixels = radius * (1.0 - hardness)`, and if that is under ~1 px it forces roughly a one-pixel feather while **adjusting both radius and hardness so the optical radius is preserved** — otherwise a 1px feather on a 2px brush would visibly fatten it. Drawpile documents the exposed control: 'Pixel feather — 0.0 disable (for very strong erasers and pixel brushes), 1.0 blur one pixel (good value)'. (2) For radius under ~3 px, per-pixel point sampling of the falloff aliases badly; libmypaint switches to an **analytic area-coverage** path that evaluates visibility at the nearest and farthest points of the pixel square within the dab and blends by the delta, i.e. approximate box-filter integration rather than a point sample. Krita's equivalent knobs are 'Precision' (1–5, quality of mask rendering; recommends 5 for inking, 1 for big fills, with Auto Precision tying it to size) and 'Smooth lines' super-smooth AA. Pixel-art brushes deliberately disable all of this (Krita's Sharpness option thresholds the mask; MyPaint pixel feather 0).

- **Used by:** libmypaint tiled surface; Krita Precision / Auto Precision / Smooth lines / Sharpness; Drawpile's MyPaint settings UI.
- **Relevance:** Small-dab aliasing is what makes a 2px inking brush look amateurish. Budget for the analytic path, or supersample the dab 2–4× when radius < 4 px. And expose a 'no AA' path for pixel brushes — thresholding a soft mask is not the same as rendering a hard one.

### Brush tips: procedural masks vs predefined bitmap tips, and the tip-rotation problem
Three tip families. (a) **Procedural/autobrush** — circle or square with a mask generator and fade parameters; infinitely scalable, tiny memory, and the falloff formula above computes it. (b) **Predefined bitmap tips** — an 8-bit alpha mask (or RGB) stamped repeatedly. Krita reads GIMP GBR/GIH and Photoshop ABR and offers five interpretations: Alpha Mask, Color Image, Lightness Map, Gradient Map. (c) **Image-pipe / multi-tip** (GIH) — a set of tips cycled by index, angle, pressure, or random, which is how 'natural' bristle and foliage brushes get variety. Rotation semantics matter enormously: Krita's Rotation option is driven by sensors, with **Drawing Angle** (aligns the tip's horizontal axis to the stroke tangent → calligraphy/ribbon) and **Tilt Direction** (aligns to the physical pen azimuth → chisel/marker) being the two that behave differently from the rest; Krita's docs recommend a 90° base angle with Tilt Direction for natural results. Procreate's equivalents are Shape→Rotation (−100% inverse to +100% follows stroke) and Azimuth (tilt-driven). Note that a bitmap tip stamped at spacing s with a fixed orientation reads as an obviously repeated texture — this is why 'Randomize' initial rotation and per-dab jitter exist.

- **Used by:** Krita brush tips (autobrush, predefined GBR/GIH/ABR, text tips); GIMP .gih image pipes; Photoshop ABR + Brush Tip Shape Angle/Roundness + Shape Dynamics Angle Jitter with Control=Direction/Initial Direction; Procreate Shape source + Rotation/Azimuth.
- **Relevance:** Support (a) from day one and (b) soon after — artists judge a paint app by whether they can import their brush packs. ABR parsing is nontrivial and version-fragmented; GBR is trivial and a good first target. For rotation, store a `RotationMode { Fixed, FollowStroke, TiltAzimuth, Random }` rather than only a numeric sensor — the semantics differ, not just the value.

### Scatter, jitter, and count — decorrelating the dab lattice
Regular spacing plus a fixed tip is a lattice, and lattices are visible. Engines break it four ways. (1) **Positional jitter**: libmypaint `offset_by_random` displaces each dab by a *Gaussian* (not uniform) sample scaled by base radius — `x += rand_gauss(rng) * amp * base_radius` — which is why MyPaint's scatter looks organic rather than boxy. (2) **Directional scatter**: Krita's Scatter separates the axis along the stroke from the axis perpendicular to it, and notes the perpendicular one dominates perceptually. (3) **Count/density**: Photoshop Scattering has a Count (dabs per spacing position) and Count Jitter; Procreate's Shape→Count stamps the shape up to 16× per point; Krita autobrush has Density and Randomness (0–100%). (4) **Per-dab parameter jitter**: size (`radius_by_random`, with the opacity correction noted earlier), angle, hue/sat/lum (Procreate Color Dynamics has both per-stamp and per-stroke variants; Krita's Source = Uniform Random / Total Random). Separately, `offset_by_speed` displaces the dab along the smoothed velocity vector — `x += NORM_DX_SLOW * offset_by_speed * 0.1 / view_zoom` — with sign controlling lead vs lag: '>0 draws where the pointer moves to, <0 draws where the pointer comes from', plus an `offset_by_speed_filter` controlling how fast it recovers when the cursor stops.

- **Used by:** libmypaint OFFSET_BY_RANDOM / OFFSET_BY_SPEED / RADIUS_BY_RANDOM; Krita Scatter + Density + Randomness; Photoshop Scattering (Scatter, Both Axes, Count, Count Jitter); Procreate Stroke Path Jitter + Shape Scatter/Count/Randomized.
- **Relevance:** Use a deterministic, seeded RNG keyed on stroke id + dab index. This is not a nicety: undo/redo, replay, and any future 'reopen the document and re-render' path will produce a *different picture* with an unseeded RNG. libmypaint carries its RNG in the brush state for exactly this reason.

### Grain / texture maps and the canvas-anchored vs stamp-anchored distinction
A grain is a second texture multiplied into the dab's alpha (or into its colour). The critical design decision is the texture's **coordinate frame**: anchored to the *canvas* (the stroke reveals a static paper texture underneath — correct for paper tooth, charcoal, pastel) or anchored to the *stamp* (the grain travels with each dab — correct for a textured bristle). Procreate names these exactly: Grain→Movement = **Texturized** ('static texture beneath strokes') vs **Moving** ('dragging effect with adjustable movement intensity'), plus Scale, Zoom, Rotation, Depth (0→max contrast), Depth Jitter, Offset Jitter, and a Blend Mode. Krita's Texture option has Pattern Scale (1.0 = 100%), Brightness/Contrast, a **Neutral Point** (added 4.4; 'adjust the gray value that is considered neutral' — values above 0.5 darken, below lighten; matters for Lightness Map and Gradient Map modes), Horizontal/Vertical Offset with a random-per-stroke option, a **Texturing Mode** (Multiply, Subtract, Lightness Map, Gradient Map, Height variants, Overlay/Dodge/Burn/Linear), and a **Cutoff + Cutoff Policy** (Disabled / Pattern / Brush) that clips the grayscale range so a texture can be made to bite only in its darkest or lightest band. Multiply vs Subtract differ meaningfully: Multiply scales alpha (grain fades proportionally, never fully breaks up), Subtract offsets it (grain punches holes at low flow — the dry-brush look). Strength is itself sensor-drivable, so pressure can bury or reveal the tooth.

- **Used by:** Krita Texture option; Procreate Grain tab; Photoshop Texture (Scale, Brightness/Contrast, Texture Each Tip, Mode, Depth, Minimum Depth, Depth Jitter) — note 'Texture Each Tip' is precisely the stamp-anchored toggle; Clip Studio's paper texture layers.
- **Relevance:** Implement canvas-anchored first (compute texture UV from canvas coordinates in the dab shader) — it is what makes pencil and charcoal read correctly and it costs nothing extra. Subtract-mode grain plus pressure→strength is the single cheapest way to get a convincing pencil. Watch out: canvas-anchored grain must be sampled in *document* space, not screen space, or the texture will crawl when the user zooms or pans mid-stroke.

### Stabilization / smoothing of the input path, and why it is separate from interpolation
Distinct from spline interpolation (which fills gaps) — stabilization *lags and averages* the pointer to remove hand tremor, and it changes where the stroke goes. Common algorithms: (a) **exponential/lowpass** — libmypaint's `slow_tracking` and `slow_tracking_per_dab` ('0 disables it, higher values remove more jitter'), applied to the position state, using the same first-order filter as velocity: `fac = exp(-dt/T1); out = fac*out + (1-fac)*in`; (b) **weighted moving average** over the last N samples (Krita's Basic/Weighted smoothing); (c) **pulled-string / rope**: the drawn point trails the cursor by a fixed distance and is pulled toward it, which produces the very clean inking feel of Procreate's **StreamLine** and CSP's stabilizer; (d) **fitted-curve / delayed re-fit**, where the last few segments are refitted as more samples arrive. All of them introduce end-of-stroke lag, so engines must 'catch up' to the final pointer position when the pen lifts or strokes will end short. Related: libmypaint's `stroke_threshold` sets the minimum pressure that counts as a stroke start, and it is documented that the MyPaint engine needs no minimum pressure to begin drawing.

- **Used by:** libmypaint SLOW_TRACKING / SLOW_TRACKING_PER_DAB; Krita's smoothing modes (None, Basic, Weighted, Stabilizer with distance/delay/finish-line); Procreate StreamLine; Clip Studio stabilizer; Photoshop Smoothing with Pulled String Mode / Stroke Catch-up / Adjust for Zoom.
- **Relevance:** Make stabilization a pipeline stage between raw events and the spline, with a per-brush strength. Implement catch-up on pen-up or users will complain that lines end early. 'Adjust for Zoom' matters: stabilization strength should be in document units, not screen pixels, or zooming changes how the brush feels.

### Smudge / wet-mix as a per-dab colour source
Smudge is not a separate tool — it is the dab's colour becoming a function of what is already on the canvas. MyPaint keeps a smudge colour in brush state: `smudge` blends brush colour with the sampled canvas colour ('0.0 does not use the smudge color, 0.5 mixes, 1.0 uses only the smudge color'); `smudge_length` is the update rate of that stored colour ('0.0 immediately updates, 1.0 never changes'), i.e. a one-pole filter that gives the brush a memory and therefore a drag/streak length; `smudge_radius_log` scales the pickup sampling radius relative to the dab. Procreate's Wet Mix is a richer reservoir model: **Dilution** (water:paint ratio → transparency), **Charge** (initial paint load, depletes over stroke length), **Attack** (adhesion to canvas), **Pull** (strength of dragging existing pigment), **Grade** (texture chunkiness). Corel Painter's patented model (US 8462173, 8654143, 8599213) formalizes it as a **reservoir buffer** (paint loaded on the brush) plus a **pickup buffer** (paint lifted from canvas), with independent brush flow rate and pickup flow rate, mixing the two before deposition. Krita's Color Smudge engine adds Smudge Length, Smudge Radius, Color Rate (how much fresh pigment is added) and a Overlay/dulling-vs-smearing mode choice.

- **Used by:** libmypaint SMUDGE / SMUDGE_LENGTH / SMUDGE_RADIUS_LOG; Krita Color Smudge Brush Engine; Procreate Wet Mix; Corel Painter reservoir+pickup patents.
- **Relevance:** Defer this, but do not architect it out. The requirement it imposes is that the dab pipeline can *read back* the destination surface — which means your stroke buffer cannot be the only thing dabs see, and it means you cannot batch dabs freely on the GPU (a smudging dab depends on the result of the previous one). Reserve a 'serialized dabs' path.

### Per-dab flags that are not colour or shape: eraser, lock-alpha, colorize
libmypaint's `draw_dab` carries three extra scalars beyond geometry and colour: `alpha_eraser` (the dab removes alpha instead of adding it — an eraser is the *same brush* with this flag, which is why MyPaint erasers keep all their texture and dynamics), `lock_alpha` (the dab may change colour but not alpha, i.e. paint only where paint already exists — 'preserve transparency' / alpha lock), and `colorize` (the dab replaces hue/sat while preserving luminance). Making these dab parameters rather than separate tools is a significant architectural simplification: one rasterizer, one dynamics system, N behaviours. Krita expresses the equivalent via blend modes on the paintop plus Preserve Alpha; Photoshop via Lock Transparent Pixels and the Eraser sharing brush presets.

- **Used by:** libmypaint `MyPaintSurface::draw_dab` signature and `ERASER`/`LOCK_ALPHA`/`COLORIZE` settings; Krita Preserve Alpha; Photoshop Lock Transparent Pixels.
- **Relevance:** Adopt this shape for `DabParams` verbatim. It means Quill's eraser is free and behaves like a real brush, which is a noticeable quality signal to artists.

### Recommendations
- Architect as three decoupled stages: (1) InputPipeline — raw PointerPoint + GetIntermediatePoints → stabilization → centripetal Catmull-Rom → arc-length resampler; (2) BrushDynamics — the base-value + per-input response-curve evaluator producing a DabParams per dab; (3) DabSink — the rasterizer. Every real engine has this split and it is what lets you change the renderer later without rewriting brush behaviour.
- Implement the partial-dab carry (libmypaint's PARTIAL_DABS) on day one. It is the difference between a smooth stroke and a beaded one, it is ~5 lines, and it is invisible in screenshots so it is easy to skip and hard to debug later.
- Build the stroke buffer before you build a second brush. Concretely: a Win2D CanvasRenderTarget covering the stroke's growing dirty rect, dabs composited into it at flow alpha, the buffer composited to the target layer once per frame at stroke opacity, and flattened on pen-up. Ship all three cap policies (Build-up / Wash / Soft) — they are cheap once the buffer exists and they are the vocabulary artists coming from Krita/Photoshop/Procreate already have.
- Express spacing as a fraction of brush DIAMETER in the UI (matching Photoshop/Procreate/CSP) while computing internally in libmypaint's dabs-per-radius form; provide the dabs_per_second term separately so airbrush/build-up works when the stylus is stationary. Default around 0.05–0.10 of diameter.
- Use the rr-based two-segment falloff (segment1/segment2 offsets and slopes from mypaint-tiled-surface.c) as the procedural tip. It avoids a per-pixel sqrt, matches artist expectations for a Hardness slider, and gives you .myb file compatibility nearly for free — which is a large, high-quality, permissively licensed brush library you can import instead of authoring.
- Implement opaque_linearize (beta_dab = beta^(1/dabs_per_pixel)) even alongside the stroke buffer. It decouples the smoothness slider from the strength slider, which is the difference between a brush editor that feels tuned and one that feels like it fights you.
- Handle small-dab anti-aliasing explicitly: force ~1px feather below a threshold while compensating radius+hardness to preserve optical size, and use analytic pixel-area coverage (or 2–4x supersampling) below ~4px radius. Provide an AA-off path for pixel brushes and hard erasers.
- Seed all per-dab randomness deterministically from (strokeId, dabIndex) and store the seed with the stroke. Without this, undo/redo and any re-render path silently changes the artwork.
- Do dab stamping on the GPU via Win2D CanvasSpriteBatch (batched, per-sprite tint and transform) with a pregenerated single-channel tip atlas, and reserve a serialized non-batched path for smudge/wet-mix brushes that must read back the destination. Verify CanvasSpriteBatch's per-sprite rotation support against your Windows App SDK 1.8 target before committing.
- Sample canvas-anchored grain in DOCUMENT space, not screen space, so texture does not crawl when the user zooms or pans mid-stroke. Ship Multiply and Subtract texturing modes at minimum — Subtract is what produces the dry-brush/pencil break-up.
- Give the eraser, alpha-lock, and colorize behaviours as DabParams flags rather than separate tools, following libmypaint's draw_dab signature.
- Prototype-test with a debug overlay showing dab centres, dab count, and effective spacing. Almost every 'this brush feels wrong' report resolves to spacing or to missing stroke-buffer capping, not to the falloff curve.

### Pitfalls
- Compositing every dab directly onto the layer with source-over at the brush's opacity. This looks fine on a straight fast stroke and falls apart the moment the stroke crosses itself or slows down — soft edges darken into ridges and the stroke reaches full opacity where you did not ask for it. This is the #1 thing that makes a homegrown brush engine feel wrong, and no amount of falloff tuning fixes it.
- Placing dabs at raw pointer event positions instead of resampling. WinUI delivers pointer events at an irregular rate; you get dab clusters at sample points and gaps between them. Related: forgetting PointerPoint.GetIntermediatePoints(), which throws away most of the pen's native report rate.
- Not carrying the fractional dab remainder across pointer events — restarts the dab phase at every sample and produces periodic beading correlated with the event rate rather than with distance.
- Using uniform (non-centripetal) Catmull-Rom on stylus input. Because humans slow at corners, samples bunch there, and uniform parameterization produces cusps, loops, and overshoot exactly at the corners where accuracy matters most. Use alpha=0.5 (centripetal).
- Uniform-random positional jitter instead of Gaussian. Uniform jitter reads as a visible square/box cloud; libmypaint uses rand_gauss for a reason.
- Confusing stabilization with interpolation. Smoothing that lags the pointer must implement catch-up on pen-up, or every stroke ends short of where the user lifted — a bug users describe as 'my lines are too short' and that is very hard to trace back.
- Unseeded RNG for per-dab scatter/size/hue jitter. The stroke re-renders differently after undo, after a document reload, or after any tile eviction, and the artwork silently changes.
- Setting hardness to exactly 0 or exactly 1 without guarding. MyPaint documents that hardness=0 produces no output at all; hardness=1 with no pixel feather aliases badly. The falloff's segment2 coefficients divide by (1-hardness).
- Adding a 1px anti-aliasing feather without compensating radius and hardness. On a 2px brush this visibly fattens the stroke, so small brushes end up thicker than the size slider claims — libmypaint explicitly adjusts both to preserve optical radius.
- Sampling canvas grain in screen space. The texture crawls under the stroke when the user zooms or pans, which looks broken and is often discovered only after the feature ships.
- Assuming aspect_ratio can be < 1.0 for elliptical dabs. libmypaint requires >= 1.0 and expresses the other orientation via angle; allowing <1 silently inverts the ellipse.
- Forgetting that radius is stored logarithmically in MyPaint-derived models (actual_radius = exp(radius_log)). Linear interpolation of a log radius is a multiplicative ramp — correct and desirable — but mixing the two spaces produces size dynamics that feel wrong at one end of the pressure range.
- Batching dabs on the GPU for a smudge/wet-mix brush. Each smudge dab depends on the composited result of the previous one; batching them produces a stroke that picks up only the pre-stroke canvas and the streak/drag behaviour disappears.
- Deriving velocity from raw finite differences without a lowpass filter. Digitizer jitter makes raw dv/dt useless for driving size or opacity; MyPaint runs every speed input through fac = exp(-dt/T1) with tunable slowness before it reaches a response curve.
- Treating spacing quality and spacing performance as the same knob. Halving spacing doubles dab count and therefore fill rate; Krita's Auto Spacing exists specifically because a linear spacing law makes very large brushes unusably slow while small brushes stay coarse.

## pen-input

### The PointerPoint / PointerPointProperties surface actually available to WinUI 3 (Microsoft.UI.Input), and what each field means
In a WinUI 3 desktop app the pen data reaches you as Microsoft.UI.Xaml.Input.PointerRoutedEventArgs on PointerPressed/Moved/Released. Call e.GetCurrentPoint(relativeToUIElement) -> Microsoft.UI.Input.PointerPoint. PointerPoint gives Position (DIPs, relative to the element you passed; float precision, sub-pixel — do NOT round), RawPosition (unpredicted/uncorrected position; Position may be host-adjusted), Timestamp (ulong microseconds since boot, NOT DateTime — this is your dt source and it is far more reliable than Stopwatch on the UI thread), PointerId, IsInContact, PointerDeviceType (Mouse/Pen/Touch) and PointerDevice (Windows.Devices.Input.PointerDevice, which exposes SupportedUsages — the HID usage page/usage IDs the digitizer actually reports, plus MaxContacts and PhysicalDeviceRect/ScreenRect).
PointerPoint.Properties (PointerPointProperties) carries: Pressure (float normalized 0..1; per MS docs 'default 0.5' — hardware that cannot report pressure returns 0.5 while a button/contact is active and 0 otherwise); XTilt/YTilt (float DEGREES, -90..+90, the angle of the pen from vertical projected onto the X and Y planes — note these are two independent plane angles, not azimuth/altitude, so you must convert if your brush engine wants azimuth/altitude); Twist (float degrees 0..359.9, barrel/axial rotation of the pen about its own axis); Orientation (float degrees, the touch/contact ellipse orientation — different from Twist, and it is the field most commonly confused with barrel rotation); ContactRect and ContactRectRaw (Rect, contact ellipse bounding box in DIPs — meaningful for touch, usually degenerate for pen); IsBarrelButtonPressed, IsEraser, IsInverted (pen flipped to eraser end), IsInRange (hover — pen detected but not touching), IsPrimary, TouchConfidence (false = the digitizer itself believes this contact is a palm), PointerUpdateKind, and IsLeftButtonPressed (for pen this is true when the tip is in contact).
Critical mapping detail: PointerRoutedEventArgs.GetCurrentPoint returns coordinates relative to the element, but if you have a pan/zoom transform on your art canvas you must invert it yourself; use RawPosition + your own transform for maximum fidelity rather than letting XAML round-trip it.
Also critical: XTilt/YTilt/Orientation are documented as populated for touch too (contact ellipse derived), so branch on PointerDeviceType before trusting them.

- **Used by:** Microsoft Learn 'Handle pointer input' (learn.microsoft.com/windows/apps/develop/input/handle-pointer-input) — the QueryPointer sample explicitly reads Pressure, Orientation, XTilt, YTilt, IsBarrelButtonPressed only inside `if (ptrPt.IsInContact)` and only for PointerDeviceType.Pen. PointerPointProperties.Pressure docs (learn.microsoft.com/uwp/api/windows.ui.input.pointerpointproperties.pressure) state the 0..1 range with default 0.5. Avalonia mirrors the same contract (docs.avaloniaui.net/api/avalonia.input/PointerPointProperties).
- **Relevance:** Quill's Art Mode input layer should normalize into its own `PenSample` struct { Vector2 PosCanvas; float Pressure; float TiltX, TiltY; float Twist; ulong TimestampUs; PenSampleFlags Flags } captured at the raw event boundary, with a per-device `PenCapabilities` record resolved once per PointerDevice. Never let brush code read PointerPoint directly — the brush engine should consume normalized, already-validated samples so that Wacom-vs-Surface differences are handled in exactly one place.

### Hardware reality: which of pressure / tilt / twist are actually populated, and how to detect it at runtime
Pressure: essentially universal on active pens (Surface Slim Pen 2 = 4096 levels / MPP 2.6; Wacom Pro Pen 2 = 8192; Huion PW517 = 8192; XP-Pen = 8192). But the value you get is quantized to those levels then normalized to 0..1, so at 1024-level hardware the pressure axis has visible stair-stepping — this is a real cause of 'banded' brush width.
Tilt: Surface Slim Pen 2 reports tilt (~77 degrees range) on recent Surface hardware; older Surface Pen models and many Surface Go/older digitizers do NOT. Wacom Pro Pen / Pro Pen 2 report altitude+azimuth. Huion documents tilt support as a per-model feature (support.huion.com 'Graphic Input Device Tilt Support (Windows)'). XP-Pen reports tilt through the Windows Ink path but has known-broken wintab32.dll behavior.
Twist: this is the big one. Twist is reported essentially only by rotation-capable pens — the Wacom Art Pen (Wacom's own dev support says 'twist data is only available with Art pens'). Surface Slim Pen 2 does NOT report barrel rotation. Assume Twist == 0 always and treat rotation-driven brushes as an opt-in feature.
Detection: do NOT feature-detect by 'is the value nonzero' on a single sample — a pen held perfectly vertical legitimately reports tilt 0. Two robust approaches: (a) query PointerDevice.SupportedUsages and look for the HID Digitizer usage page (0x0D) usages X Tilt (0x3D), Y Tilt (0x3E), Twist (0x41), Tip Pressure (0x30); this is the authoritative capability answer. (b) as a fallback, run a short observation window: if a field is bit-identical across N>50 samples spanning real movement, mark it unsupported and latch that per device for the session.
Another wrinkle: WinTab vs Windows Ink. Wacom/Huion/XP-Pen drivers expose a 'Use Windows Ink' toggle. When OFF, the driver serves WinTab and the Windows pointer stack may get degraded data (pressure flattened, tilt absent, or worse — the pen behaves as a mouse). Quill only sees the Windows Ink path, so a user with Windows Ink disabled will report 'pressure doesn't work' and it is not a Quill bug. Detect the symptom (pressure pinned at exactly 0.5 for a pen-type pointer) and surface an actionable hint.

- **Used by:** Wacom Developer Support 'Windows Ink' and 'Wintab' articles (developer-support.wacom.com); Huion support article on tilt support; Microsoft pen protocol (MPP 2.6) spec summary via Wikipedia; Blender's Windows tablet API rework thread (blenderartists.org 'Windows Tablet changes (Testers Needed: Wacom, Huion, XP-Pen)') which documents exactly these cross-vendor divergences as encountered by a real app.
- **Relevance:** Ship a `PenCapabilities` probe + a visible 'Pen diagnostics' panel (live pressure bar, tilt crosshair, twist dial, reported device name, sample rate). This is cheap and turns an entire class of unfixable support reports into self-service. Every brush parameter bound to tilt or twist must declare a fallback: tilt-unsupported -> use velocity or a fixed angle; twist-unsupported -> use stroke direction as the pseudo-rotation (this is what real engines do for flat/chisel nibs).

### GetIntermediatePoints and why event-rate-only sampling produces faceted strokes
Pen digitizers report at 120–360 Hz (ChromeOS styluses reach 400 Hz; Wacom Pro tablets ~200 Hz; Surface ~120–240 Hz). Your UI thread delivers PointerMoved at best once per compositor frame (60–120 Hz), and under load far less. Windows COALESCES: if a pointer message is still queued when a newer one arrives, the older one is dropped and replaced. So handling only GetCurrentPoint on PointerMoved throws away 50–75% of the pen's samples, and worse, the ones you keep are irregularly spaced. Fast strokes then become long straight chords between widely-spaced samples — the classic 'faceted' / polygonal fast stroke.
The fix is PointerRoutedEventArgs.GetIntermediatePoints(relativeTo), which returns the full backlog: per the WinRT docs it retrieves 'position and state information for the specified pointer, from the last pointer event up to and including the current pointer event', and 'the most recent pointer information retrieved by this method is equivalent to that retrieved by GetCurrentPoint'. Every intermediate PointerPoint is a full PointerPoint — it carries its own Pressure, XTilt, YTilt, Twist and Timestamp, so you lose nothing by using it.
Two hard gotchas: (1) ORDER. The collection is returned MOST-RECENT-FIRST (index 0 is the newest, equal to GetCurrentPoint). You must iterate in reverse to append in temporal order. Getting this backwards produces a stroke that zig-zags backwards on every event and is a very common bug. Verify empirically against Timestamp rather than trusting any doc — assert monotonic timestamps in a debug build. (2) In some WinUI 3 preview builds GetIntermediatePoints returned Microsoft.UI.Input.Experimental.ExpPointerPoint rather than PointerPoint (microsoft-ui-xaml issue #3674); on Windows App SDK 1.8 it returns IList<PointerPoint>, but pin this with a compile-time-checked helper.
Separately: PointerPressed only ever gives you one point, and PointerReleased gives you the final backlog — you must drain intermediate points on Released too, or every stroke loses its last few milliseconds (which is exactly where the pressure taper lives, so strokes end with an ugly blunt tip).

- **Used by:** MicrosoftDocs/winrt-api PointerPoint.GetIntermediatePoints reference; Microsoft Learn 'Handle pointer input' which explicitly recommends GetCurrentPoint/GetIntermediatePoints for extended data; AvaloniaUI issue #7338 'Pointer events are lost if the UI thread is slow' documents the Windows coalescing behavior and why inking needs the intermediate-point path.
- **Relevance:** This is the single highest-leverage correctness item in the whole input layer, and it costs about ten lines. Quill's ArtSurface pointer handler must be: on Pressed -> begin stroke with GetCurrentPoint; on Moved -> `var pts = e.GetIntermediatePoints(canvasRoot); for (int i = pts.Count - 1; i >= 0; i--) Ingest(pts[i]);`; on Released -> drain intermediates then close. Never call GetCurrentPoint in the Moved handler at all (it is already pts[0]).

### Latency budget: where the milliseconds come from and which ones you can actually remove
End-to-end pen-to-photon on a stock Windows app is typically 50–100 ms. Decomposed: digitizer scan + HID report (~4–8 ms at 120–240 Hz), driver + Windows input stack promotion to pointer messages (~2–5 ms), UI-thread message dispatch + your handler (0 ms to tens of ms, entirely dependent on how loaded the UI thread is), your rendering work, then DWM compose + swap + display scanout + panel response (typically 16–33 ms, and this is the biggest fixed block).
What you can remove:
1. Get off the UI thread. SwapChainPanel.CreateCoreIndependentInputSource(CoreInputDeviceTypes.Pen | Touch) must be called FROM a non-UI thread; it returns an InputPointerSource whose pointer events are delivered on that thread, bypassing XAML layout/dispatch entirely. Requirement: you must have already set a swap chain on the SwapChainPanel and rendered from it at least once (that render is what makes hit-testing possible). This is exactly the architecture DirectInk uses — MS describes it as 'input flows directly to a high priority background thread and will never be blocked by UI/app thread activities.'
2. Present with low latency. Use a flip-model swap chain with DXGI_SWAP_CHAIN_FLAG_FRAME_LATENCY_WAITABLE_OBJECT, set MaximumFrameLatency = 1, and wait on the frame-latency waitable handle before rendering. Prefer 2 buffers. Consider DXGI_PRESENT_DO_NOT_WAIT / partial presents (DirtyRects) so you only push the changed region.
3. Prediction. Microsoft's own low-latency-ink patents (US9633466 'Low latency ink rendering pipeline', US10338725/US10719168 'Wet ink predictor') state prediction buys '15 ms or more'. Android's motion prediction library does the same with a Kalman filter over direction, velocity, pressure and time.
4. Front-buffer / direct-to-screen rendering for the wet segment (Android's low-latency graphics library writes directly to the front buffer for the small changed region, then resumes double-buffered rendering when the stylus lifts). On Windows this is approximated by a small dedicated wet-ink swap chain with dirty-rect presents.
What you must NOT do: any per-sample allocation, any per-sample XAML property write, any synchronous file/undo work in the input path. A single GC pause is worth more latency than everything above combined.

- **Used by:** Microsoft Learn SwapChainPanel.CreateCoreIndependentInputSource docs; MSDN Magazine 'Digital Ink — Ink Interaction in Windows 10' on the DirectInk high-priority background thread + prediction + Bezier wet rendering; US patents 9633466 / 10338725 / 10719168; Android Developers 'Stylus Low Latency' (medium.com/androiddevelopers) for front-buffered rendering and the Kalman predictor.
- **Relevance:** Quill is unpackaged WinUI 3 + Win2D. The pragmatic staging is: v1 = UI-thread input + Win2D CanvasSwapChainPanel with MaximumFrameLatency=1 (already a large win over CanvasControl/CanvasImageSource, which are tied to the XAML render pass); v2 = move to CreateCoreIndependentInputSource on a dedicated input+render thread. Note Win2D's CanvasSwapChain wraps a DXGI swap chain and MS docs explicitly say it 'can be updated independently of the XAML UI renderer, which can provide smoother and lower latency animation' — that is the intended vehicle.

### Stabilization algorithm family, precisely specified, with the real tradeoffs
Treat stabilization as a chain of separable stages, not one algorithm.
(A) DEADZONE / JITTER GATE. Reject a new sample if |p_new - p_last| < eps (eps in canvas units, typically 0.3–1.0 px at 100% zoom, and it MUST be scaled by zoom or it becomes useless when zoomed in). Cheap, kills digitizer quantization noise at slow speeds, costs nothing in lag, but produces stair-stepping if eps is too large. Krita exposes exactly this as Stabilizer 'Delay' — 'determines the size of the dead zone around the cursor. This can be used to create sharp corners with more control.'
(B) SIMPLE MOVING AVERAGE over the last N samples. out_i = mean(p_{i-N+1..i}). Linear-phase, so lag is exactly (N-1)/2 samples — predictable. Rounds corners proportionally to N. This is Krita's 'Stabilizer' ('averages all inputs from the tablet... draws a circle around your cursor and the line will be a bit behind') and Lazy Nezumi's 'Moving Average' ('continuously averaging your previous pen positions', ideal for curves with rounded corners; higher Amount = smoother but more brush lag).
(C) EXPONENTIAL MOVING AVERAGE. out_i = a*p_i + (1-a)*out_{i-1}, a in (0,1]. One float of state, no buffer. Lag ~ (1-a)/a samples. Lazy Nezumi's EMA mode 'gives more importance to recent samples' so it straightens out faster on catch-up — better for 'mostly straight with tight curves'. THE TRAP: a fixed `a` is wrong because sample dt is not constant. Make it dt-aware: a = 1 - exp(-dt / tau). Without this, your smoothing strength silently changes with device report rate and CPU load.
(D) ONE EURO FILTER (Casiez/Roussel/Vogel, CHI 2012) — the correct default. It is (C) with an adaptive cutoff. Per sample: T_e = dt; alpha(f_c) = 1/(1 + tau/T_e) where tau = 1/(2*pi*f_c); estimate raw derivative dx_i = (x_i - x_hat_{i-1}) / T_e; smooth the derivative with a fixed cutoff f_Cd (default 1.0 Hz) to get dx_hat; then f_c = f_Cmin + beta*|dx_hat|; finally x_hat_i = alpha(f_c)*x_i + (1-alpha(f_c))*x_hat_{i-1}. Tuning rule from the paper: slow-speed jitter -> lower f_Cmin; high-speed lag -> raise beta. This directly encodes the perceptual fact that humans notice jitter at low speed and lag at high speed, and it is why one-euro beats fixed EMA/SMA at equal jitter reduction. Run one filter per axis (x, y) and separate filters for pressure and tilt.
(E) PULLED-STRING / ROPE (Lazy Nezumi 'Pulled String', Photoshop 'Smoothing' + 'Pulled String Mode', Clip Studio's stabilizer at high values). Geometric constraint, not a filter: maintain anchor A (the drawn point) and cursor C (raw pen). If |C - A| > L, move A along the direction (C - A) so that |C - A| == L exactly: A += (C - A) * (1 - L/|C - A|). Otherwise A does not move at all. Properties: ZERO jitter below the string length (the brush is literally stationary), and — the key advantage — it produces genuinely sharp corners, because when you reverse direction the anchor stays put until the string re-extends on the new heading. Lazy Nezumi: 'the brush will only move when the string is fully extended, which makes it easy to draw sharp corners.' Cost: constant positional lag of L, and the anchor path is C1-discontinuous (it's a sequence of straight pulls), so you usually feed the anchor path into a light EMA/spline afterwards.
(F) MASS-SPRING / WEIGHTED (Lazy Nezumi 'Weighted' script mode). Give the brush mass m, apply force F = k*(C - A) toward the cursor, integrate with drag d: v = (v + F/m*dt) * d; A += v*dt. Produces organic overshoot on direction changes (calligraphic feel) and, notably, Lazy Nezumi derives OUTPUT PRESSURE from brush speed with a maxPressureSpeed threshold — a neat trick for giving mouse/no-pressure users a pressure-like taper.
(G) STROKE-END / CATCH-UP behavior. All lagging filters leave the drawn point behind the true pen at lift-off. Three options, all shipped by real apps: leave it (line ends short — Krita's default when Finish Line is off), animate the remaining points to completion (Krita 'Finish Line' = 'ensures that the line will be finished'; Lazy Nezumi 'Catch Up' = 'brush repositions when you pause and at stroke completion'), or interpolate toward the last known cursor by a user-controlled fraction (Krita Weighted 'Stroke Ending' = 'controls how much the line will attempt to reach the last known position of the cursor after the stylus is lifted'). Whichever you pick, the pressure filter must be flushed in the same way or the stroke ends with a fat blunt tip instead of a taper.
(H) SMOOTH THE OTHER CHANNELS TOO. Krita: 'Smooth Pressure' and 'Stabilize Sensors' (smooths pressure, speed and tilt). Pressure is noisier than position on most digitizers; unsmoothed pressure on a size-mapped brush is a very visible defect.
TRADEOFF SUMMARY: every one of these buys smoothness with lag, and lag is the thing users describe as 'the pen feels disconnected'. Corner preservation is orthogonal and only (A) and (E) give it to you. The standard production recipe is deadzone -> one-euro (or pulled-string for high stabilization settings) -> arc-length resample -> spline.

- **Used by:** Casiez, Roussel & Vogel, '1€ Filter: A Simple Speed-based Low-pass Filter for Noisy Input in Interactive Systems', CHI 2012 (direction.bordeaux.inria.fr/~roussel/publications/2012-CHI-one-euro-filter.pdf); equations restated at jaantollander.com/post/noise-filtering-using-one-euro-filter. Lazy Nezumi Pro smoothing docs (lazynezumi.com/smoothing) for Pulled String / Moving Average / EMA / Weighted + Catch Up. Krita Freehand Brush Tool manual (docs.krita.org/en/reference_manual/tools/freehand_brush.html) for No Smoothing / Basic / Weighted (Distance, Stroke Ending, Smooth Pressure, Scalable Distance) / Stabilizer (Sample Count at Max/Min Speed, Delay dead zone, Finish Line, Stabilize Sensors). Photoshop and Clip Studio Paint ship the same pulled-string mechanism under different names.
- **Relevance:** Recommend Quill implement a `IStrokeFilter` pipeline of small stateful stages that each take and return a PenSample, configured per-tool: technical-pen tool = deadzone + one-euro(low beta); inking/lineart tool = deadzone + pulled-string(L exposed as a 0–100 'Stabilization' slider mapping to 0–40 px) + light EMA; sketch tool = deadzone only. Expose exactly ONE user-facing 'Stabilization' number plus an advanced expander, not seven knobs. Every stage must be dt-aware and zoom-aware (Krita's 'Scalable Distance' exists precisely because distances that aren't scaled by zoom behave wrongly).

### Prediction (negative lag) and why it must be strictly speculative
Prediction extrapolates 1–3 samples (~10–30 ms) ahead of the last real point so the wet stroke visually reaches the pen tip. Methods, in increasing order of quality: (1) linear extrapolation p + v*dt_pred — trivial, but overshoots badly on curves and direction reversals; (2) quadratic/constant-acceleration p + v*dt + 0.5*a*dt^2 — better on smooth arcs, much worse on reversals; (3) Kalman filter over a constant-acceleration state [pos, vel, accel] per axis, which is what Android's androidx.input motion-prediction library uses ('based on direction, velocity, pressure and time to predict where the next point will be'), and it also predicts pressure so the predicted segment has the right width; (4) curvature-aware prediction that projects along the fitted spline's tangent rather than the raw velocity vector, which is what keeps prediction from flying off the outside of a tight curve.
The non-negotiable rule, stated bluntly by the Android docs: 'Predicted motion events are artificial and inaccurate by definition. Use the predicted event to reduce the perceived latency, but ensure the predicted data is replaced with the real data once it is received.' So predicted points must live ONLY in the wet/preview buffer and must never be appended to the stroke's committed point list.
Artifacts to expect and damp: overshoot 'whiskers' at direction reversals and at pen-lift (the predictor keeps flying after the pen stops). Standard mitigations: clamp the prediction distance as a function of |velocity| AND estimated curvature (predict less when turning hard), fade prediction to zero over the last few samples when speed drops below a threshold, and drop prediction entirely on PointerReleased. Microsoft's 'Wet ink predictor' patents cover exactly this class of confidence-gated prediction.

- **Used by:** Android Developers 'Stylus Low Latency' (androidx motion prediction, Kalman); Microsoft patents US10338725 / US10719168 'Wet ink predictor' and US9633466 'Low latency ink rendering pipeline' (claims ~15 ms saving); Microsoft's DirectInk stack ('uses a high-priority background thread and input prediction').
- **Relevance:** Prediction is a v2 feature, not v1. Ship the stabilizer + intermediate points + low-latency swap chain first and measure; if perceived lag is still the top complaint, add a constant-acceleration Kalman predictor with curvature clamping, wired only into the wet layer. Make it a toggle — some artists hate the overshoot more than they hate the lag.

### Live 'wet' stroke rendering without committing, on WinUI 3 + Win2D
IMPORTANT CONSTRAINT: the UWP inking stack's custom-drying path (InkPresenter.ActivateCustomDrying + InkSynchronizer.BeginDry/EndDry, the classic wet/dry pattern) is NOT available to Quill — InkCanvas/InkPresenter/InkSynchronizer are not supported in WinUI 3 desktop (microsoft-ui-xaml issue #6742, and CommunityToolkit issue #4105 where InfiniteCanvas crashes on WinUI 3 for this exact reason). Quill must implement wet/dry itself. That is fine — the pattern is simple and it is what Photoshop, Procreate and Krita do internally anyway.
THE TWO-LAYER MODEL. Keep two GPU surfaces: (1) COMMITTED — a CanvasRenderTarget holding all finished strokes for the active paint layer; (2) WET — a second CanvasRenderTarget (or a command list) holding only the in-progress stroke. Each frame you composite committed + wet + the rest of the layer stack. On pen-up you draw the wet target into the committed target once, clear wet, and push an undo entry. The wet stroke therefore never touches the committed pixels until it is finalized, which is what makes 'undo the whole stroke' and 'change opacity mid-stroke' trivially correct.
WHY A SEPARATE WET TARGET AND NOT JUST 'DRAW THE STROKE EVERY FRAME': for anything with per-stamp alpha accumulation (airbrush, soft round with flow < 1), redrawing the whole stroke each frame is both O(n^2) over the stroke and produces the WRONG result — a stroke that crosses itself must not double-darken within a single stroke for most brush models. Keeping the wet layer and stamping only the NEW segment into it each frame is both faster and semantically correct. This is the 'stroke buffer at full stroke opacity, composited at the layer/tool opacity' model used by Photoshop and Krita.
INCREMENTAL STAMPING. Maintain a cursor `distanceConsumed` along the stabilized+resampled path. Each frame, walk the newly-arrived arc length and emit stamps at fixed spacing (spacing = brushDiameter * spacingRatio, typically 0.05–0.25), interpolating pressure/tilt/twist at each stamp. Only the bounding box of the new stamps is dirty — track it and use it as the invalidation rect and as the swap chain's DirtyRects.
RESAMPLING/SPLINE. Before stamping, fit the sample path with a CENTRIPETAL Catmull-Rom spline (alpha = 0.5, knots t_{i+1} = t_i + |P_{i+1} - P_i|^alpha). Centripetal is the right choice specifically because, per the standard result, loops/self-intersections and cusps cannot occur within a segment and the curve follows the control points more tightly — uniform (alpha=0) Catmull-Rom overshoots and forms cusps exactly where a pen sample cluster is irregularly spaced, which is constantly. Then walk the spline by arc length (build a coarse arc-length-to-parameter table and lerp) so stamp spacing is uniform in screen space regardless of pen speed. Note you need points i-1..i+2 to evaluate segment i..i+1, so the spline can only be evaluated one sample behind the newest — budget that one-sample latency, or use a one-sided/extrapolated tangent for the leading segment.
WIN2D SPECIFICS. Use CanvasSwapChainPanel + CanvasSwapChain rather than CanvasControl: MS docs note CanvasSwapChain 'can be updated independently of the XAML UI renderer, which can provide smoother and lower latency animation'. Set MaximumFrameLatency = 1. Allocate the committed target once and reuse it — CanvasRenderTarget allocation is expensive and will hitch. For very large canvases use CanvasVirtualControl/CanvasVirtualImageSource-style tiling (Win2D supports rendering these from non-UI threads) so you only keep resident the tiles near the viewport.

- **Used by:** Microsoft Learn 'Using Win2D without built-in controls' and the Win2D CanvasSwapChain reference (microsoft.github.io/Win2D/WinUI3); microsoft-ui-xaml issue #6742 and discussion #7426 confirming no InkCanvas/InkSynchronizer in WinUI 3 desktop; the UWP custom-drying pattern (Mike Taulty, 'Windows 10, UWP, InkCanvas, Win2D and Custom Drying') as the reference architecture to reimplement; centripetal Catmull-Rom properties per the standard formulation (Yuksel et al. / Wikipedia 'Centripetal Catmull-Rom spline').
- **Relevance:** Concrete architecture for Quill: `ArtStroke` (immutable list of PenSample + brush settings) -> `StrokeRasterizer` (stabilize -> centripetal Catmull-Rom -> arc-length resample -> stamp) -> writes into `WetTarget` incrementally -> `CompositeRenderer` draws Committed + Wet + prediction tail into the CanvasSwapChain each vsync. On pen-up: DrawImage(WetTarget) into Committed, clear Wet, push undo. Undo of an in-progress stroke = just clear Wet, zero cost. This also gives you free stroke-level features later (stroke smoothing after the fact, re-render at different resolution for export) because the sample list is retained.

### Pressure response curves and per-device calibration
Raw normalized pressure is not perceptually useful directly. Two separable transforms:
1. DEVICE CALIBRATION (normalize the hardware): map raw 0..1 through a user-set [minP, maxP] window, r' = clamp((r - minP) / (maxP - minP), 0, 1). minP is the 'click threshold' — the force below which nothing is drawn (Krita exposes exactly this: 'the click pressure threshold, located below the curve, controls how hard you need to press before Krita registers a stroke'). maxP lets users who cannot comfortably reach full force still reach 100%. David Revoy's standard calibration procedure — white canvas, black, linear curve, draw naturally, then set the endpoints to the range you actually produced — should be shipped as an in-app wizard.
2. RESPONSE CURVE (the feel): apply a user-editable transfer curve, r'' = f(r'). Simplest useful parameterization is a single gamma, r'' = r'^g: g > 1 = 'hard' (need more force for thick lines, more control in the thin range); g < 1 = 'soft'. Better UX is an editable spline curve like Krita's/Photoshop's; per Krita's docs an S-curve 'makes light pressures more sensitive while requiring extra force to reach 100%', an inverse-S 'gives a wider midtone range but makes it harder to get delicate thin lines'.
3. PER-PARAMETER MAPPING (do NOT reuse one curve for everything). Real engines map pressure differently per target: brush RADIUS follows roughly a logarithmic/perceptual curve, while stamp OPACITY is commonly modulated by something like p^2.5 — the high exponent on opacity is what produces the characteristic translucency build-up of real media. Size and opacity must be independently bindable to pressure/tilt/velocity.
4. QUANTIZATION. On 1024-level hardware, r has 1024 distinct values; after a steep curve the low end has very few distinct radii and you get visible width banding. Mitigate by smoothing pressure temporally (its own one-euro filter) and by interpolating pressure per-stamp along the resampled path rather than per-sample.
5. NO-PRESSURE FALLBACK. Mouse and non-pressure pens report exactly 0.5. Detect that and substitute velocity-derived pressure (fast = thin, slow = thick — this is Lazy Nezumi's 'Weighted' mode behavior, output pressure from brush speed with a maxPressureSpeed threshold) so mouse users still get expressive strokes.

- **Used by:** Krita Freehand Brush / Tablet Settings pressure curve and click threshold (docs.krita.org); David Revoy, 'Calibrating stylus pressure' (davidrevoy.com/article182); Lazy Nezumi 'Weighted' mode speed-to-pressure (lazynezumi.com/smoothing); the log-radius / p^2.5-opacity convention as documented in painting-engine literature.
- **Relevance:** Store the curve per input DEVICE (keyed on PointerDevice identity) not globally, because a user with both a Surface and a Wacom needs two calibrations. Persist alongside Quill's notebook settings. Ship three presets (Soft / Linear / Hard) plus the calibration wizard; the editable curve widget is a nice-to-have, the presets + min/max window covers ~90% of complaints.

### Palm rejection and touch/pen arbitration
Layered defense, cheapest first:
1. TRUST THE PLATFORM FIRST. Windows already does a lot: PointerCanceled fires on touch pointers 'when a pen is detected within range of the input surface' (per the Learn docs), and PointerPointProperties.TouchConfidence is false when the digitizer itself classifies a contact as a palm. Honor both — drop any contact with TouchConfidence == false, and treat PointerCanceled as 'discard this stroke entirely', not 'end this stroke'. This alone handles most Surface-class hardware.
2. PEN-IN-RANGE SUPPRESSION. The pen reports IsInRange during hover, before contact. Maintain a `penProximityUntil` timestamp: whenever any pen pointer event arrives (hover or contact), set penProximityUntil = now + ~500 ms and reject ALL touch-type drawing input until then. This is the single most effective app-level heuristic and it covers the common failure where the palm lands a few ms before the tip.
3. MODE SEPARATION (the real fix, and what users actually want). In Art Mode, touch should never paint. Bind: pen = draw; touch = pan/zoom/rotate the canvas (two-finger), single-finger = pan; mouse = draw. Then palm contacts are at worst a spurious pan, not a ruined painting. Offer 'Touch draws' as an explicit off-by-default toggle for touch-only devices, and when it is on, apply steps 1/2/4 aggressively.
4. GEOMETRIC/TEMPORAL HEURISTICS, only if you must support touch drawing. Use ContactRect: reject contacts whose bounding box exceeds a threshold (the literature uses ~25 mm as a 'large object' cutoff — convert via PointerDevice.PhysicalDeviceRect/ScreenRect to get real mm, do not guess from DIPs). Combine with: reject non-primary contacts during an active pen stroke; reject contacts that appear within a radius of, and shortly before/after, a pen contact; and use a short latency window (~30–60 ms) before committing a touch stroke so a subsequent pen-down can retroactively cancel it. The state-of-the-art academic approach (Schwarz et al., 'Probabilistic Palm Rejection Using Spatiotemporal Touch Features and Iterative Classification') classifies each contact from features that evolve over a short window — touch area, velocity, distance to other contacts — and ITERATIVELY RE-CLASSIFIES as more evidence arrives, which is why the retroactive-cancel window matters.
5. HANDEDNESS. Windows exposes a left/right-handed setting; the palm is below-left of the tip for right-handers. If you implement geometric rejection, bias the rejection region accordingly.

- **Used by:** Microsoft Learn 'Handle pointer input' (PointerCanceled semantics: 'Touch pointers are canceled when a pen is detected within range'; TouchConfidence); Schwarz, Xiao, Mankoff, Hudson & Harrison, 'Probabilistic Palm Rejection Using Spatiotemporal Touch Features and Iterative Classification' (CHI 2014); linux-surface/iptsd issue #173 documenting practical palm-rejection heuristics on Surface hardware; large-object thresholds from touch-controller palm-rejection patents (e.g. US9710104).
- **Relevance:** Quill should default Art Mode to pen-draws / touch-navigates, honor TouchConfidence and PointerCanceled as hard discards, and implement the pen-proximity suppression window. Treat PointerCanceled specially in the wet/dry model: it should CLEAR the wet target and push nothing to undo — which is another argument for the two-layer architecture, since cancellation becomes free.

### Robust pointer lifecycle: the events that actually end a stroke
PointerPressed and PointerReleased do not reliably pair — the Learn docs state this outright and warn that your app 'should listen for and handle any event that might conclude a pointer down (PointerExited, PointerCanceled, and PointerCaptureLost)'. PointerCaptureLost can fire INSTEAD of PointerReleased. Causes of an unclosed stroke in the wild: pen leaves the digitizer's active area mid-stroke, display/DPI/monitor configuration change, session lock, another element steals capture, or the contact goes unreported for >100 ms.
Required handling: on PointerPressed, call CapturePointer (only succeeds when IsInContact is true) so that drawing continues correctly when the pen crosses outside the canvas element's bounds. Then implement a single `EndStroke(reason)` invoked from Released (commit), CaptureLost (commit — the user probably did finish), Canceled (discard), and a watchdog timer (commit after ~500 ms of silence with no in-contact samples). Track active strokes in a Dictionary<uint /*PointerId*/, StrokeState> because multi-pen (Surface Hub supports two) and stray touch contacts will otherwise corrupt a single global 'current stroke'.
Also: PointerMoved fires during HOVER for pens (IsInRange true, IsInContact false). Gate all sample ingestion on IsInContact or you will draw while hovering. Hover events are still valuable — use them to drive a brush-size cursor preview and to prime the palm-rejection proximity window.

- **Used by:** Microsoft Learn 'Handle pointer input' — explicit notes on Pressed/Released not occurring in pairs, PointerCaptureLost firing instead of PointerReleased, the >100 ms no-contact cancellation rule, and CapturePointer requiring IsInContact.
- **Relevance:** This is boring and it is where shipped drawing apps leak 'my stroke never ended and now everything I do is one giant stroke' bugs. Quill's ArtSurface should have exactly one EndStroke path with a reason enum, a per-PointerId stroke dictionary, and a watchdog. Write this on day one; retrofitting it after the brush engine exists is painful.

### Recommendations
- Build a dedicated input layer that terminates at a normalized `PenSample` struct { Vector2 PosCanvas; float Pressure; float TiltX, TiltY; float Twist; ulong TimestampUs; PenSampleFlags Flags }. Brush/render code must never see PointerPoint. All device quirk handling lives in this one place.
- Day-one, ten-line, highest-ROI fix: drain GetIntermediatePoints on PointerMoved AND PointerReleased, iterating the list in REVERSE (index 0 is the newest, equal to GetCurrentPoint). Do not additionally call GetCurrentPoint in Moved. Assert monotonically increasing PointerPoint.Timestamp in debug builds to catch ordering regressions.
- Use PointerPoint.Timestamp (microseconds) as the sole time source for all filters. Every filter must be dt-aware (alpha = 1 - exp(-dt/tau)), never a fixed per-sample coefficient, or smoothing strength will silently vary with device report rate and CPU load.
- Adopt the one-euro filter (Casiez et al., CHI 2012) as the default stabilizer: filter x, y, pressure and tilt independently. Start at f_Cmin ~ 1.0 Hz, beta ~ 0.007, f_Cd = 1.0 Hz for a pen at 120-240 Hz, then tune on real hardware. Expose one user-facing 'Stabilization' slider.
- Add a pulled-string / rope stabilizer as the high-stabilization mode (A += (C-A)*(1 - L/|C-A|) only when |C-A| > L), because it is the only mechanism in the family that preserves genuinely sharp corners. Map the Stabilization slider's upper range to string length L in canvas pixels, and scale L by zoom.
- Structure stabilization as an ordered chain of small stateful stages — deadzone -> one-euro or pulled-string -> centripetal Catmull-Rom (alpha=0.5) -> arc-length resample -> stamp at fixed spacing. Keep each stage independently testable and independently disableable per tool.
- Implement the wet/dry two-layer model yourself: a persistent committed CanvasRenderTarget plus a wet CanvasRenderTarget for the in-progress stroke, stamped INCREMENTALLY (only new arc length each frame) and merged on pen-up. InkPresenter/InkSynchronizer custom drying does not exist in WinUI 3 desktop, so there is no shortcut — but this model also gives you free stroke-cancel, correct within-stroke alpha accumulation, and cheap undo.
- Render through CanvasSwapChainPanel/CanvasSwapChain with MaximumFrameLatency = 1 and dirty-rect presents, not CanvasControl. This decouples the paint surface from the XAML render pass and is the largest single latency win available without rearchitecting threading.
- Plan (but defer) moving pointer input off the UI thread via SwapChainPanel.CreateCoreIndependentInputSource — it must be called from a non-UI thread and requires that a swap chain has already been set and rendered from at least once. This is the DirectInk architecture; treat it as v2 once the brush engine is stable.
- Ship a per-device capability probe (prefer PointerDevice.SupportedUsages / HID digitizer usages 0x30 pressure, 0x3D/0x3E tilt, 0x41 twist over value-sniffing) plus a visible Pen Diagnostics panel showing live pressure, tilt, twist, device name and measured sample rate. Give every tilt/twist-driven brush parameter an explicit declared fallback.
- Ship pressure calibration per input device: a min/max force window (min = click threshold) plus a response curve, with Soft/Linear/Hard presets and a David-Revoy-style 'draw naturally, we'll set your range' wizard. Map pressure to radius and to opacity through SEPARATE curves — a single shared curve gives the wrong feel for one of them.
- Default Art Mode to pen-draws / touch-navigates. Honor PointerPointProperties.TouchConfidence == false and PointerCanceled as hard DISCARDS (clear the wet layer, push nothing to undo), and suppress touch drawing for ~500 ms after any pen event including hover.
- Write a single EndStroke(reason) path invoked from PointerReleased, PointerCaptureLost, PointerCanceled, PointerExited and a ~500 ms silence watchdog, with a Dictionary<uint PointerId, StrokeState> for concurrent pointers. Call CapturePointer in PointerPressed. Do this before the brush engine exists.
- Instrument latency from the start: log PointerPoint.Timestamp at ingest vs. QPC at present, and keep a debug HUD showing measured samples/sec, dropped-coalesced estimate, and pen-to-present ms. You cannot tune stabilization or justify prediction without this number.
- Treat prediction as v2 and gate it behind a toggle. If added, use a constant-acceleration Kalman filter over position and pressure, clamp prediction distance by both speed and curvature, fade to zero as speed drops, kill it entirely on pen-up, and keep predicted points strictly in the wet layer — never in the committed sample list.

### Pitfalls
- GetIntermediatePoints returns points MOST-RECENT-FIRST. Iterating forward and appending produces a stroke that jumps backward on every event. It looks like 'jitter' or 'the stabilizer is broken' and people spend days tuning filters to fix an ordering bug. Assert on Timestamp monotonicity.
- Calling GetCurrentPoint AND then appending all of GetIntermediatePoints duplicates the newest sample every event — it biases every filter and creates zero-length segments that make normalized direction vectors NaN.
- Forgetting to drain intermediate points in PointerReleased silently truncates the last ~10-20 ms of every stroke. That is exactly where the pressure taper lives, so strokes end blunt instead of tapered, and it will be misdiagnosed as a brush-engine problem.
- Pressure == 0.5 exactly is the documented sentinel for 'hardware cannot report pressure'. If you do not special-case it, mice and non-pressure pens produce dead uniform mid-weight strokes and users report 'pressure is broken'. Conversely, do not treat a genuine 0.5 mid-press reading as a failure — latch the capability per device, not per sample.
- Twist/barrel rotation is essentially never populated. Only rotation-capable pens (Wacom Art Pen) report it; Surface Slim Pen 2 does not. Any brush whose behavior depends on Twist will appear completely broken to ~95% of users. Default to stroke-direction-derived rotation.
- XTilt/YTilt are two independent plane angles in degrees, not azimuth/altitude. Feeding them directly into a brush that expects azimuth/altitude gives subtly wrong nib orientation that looks 'almost right' and is very hard to spot. Also, they are populated for TOUCH from the contact ellipse — branch on PointerDeviceType.
- Confusing Properties.Orientation (contact-ellipse orientation) with Properties.Twist (barrel rotation). They are different fields with different meanings and the naming actively invites the mistake.
- Fixed-coefficient EMA/SMA without dt. Sample intervals are irregular (120-360 Hz hardware, coalescing, UI-thread jitter), so a fixed alpha means your stabilization strength changes with machine load. The stroke feels different on a busy machine and you will never reproduce the bug report.
- Uniform (alpha=0) Catmull-Rom for stroke interpolation. It overshoots and forms cusps and self-intersections exactly where pen samples are irregularly spaced — i.e. constantly. Use centripetal (alpha=0.5), whose defining property is that cusps and self-intersections cannot occur within a segment.
- Stamping at fixed PARAMETER increments along a spline instead of fixed ARC LENGTH. Segment arc lengths differ, so stamp density varies with pen speed and you get visible density banding — dark clumps where the pen was slow, gaps where it was fast.
- Not scaling deadzone / string length / stabilizer distance by zoom level. At 800% zoom a 1 px deadzone eats all detail; at 12% it does nothing. Krita ships a 'Scalable Distance' option specifically because of this.
- Smoothing position but not pressure. Pressure is noisier than position on most digitizers, so a size-mapped brush gets a smooth centerline with a visibly wobbling width. Krita exposes 'Smooth Pressure' / 'Stabilize Sensors' for exactly this reason.
- Not flushing the filter state at pen-up. Every lagging filter leaves the drawn point behind the true pen; if you also do not flush the PRESSURE filter, the stroke ends at whatever mid-pressure the filter was holding, producing a blunt fat tip. Position catch-up and pressure catch-up must be handled together.
- Committing predicted points to the stroke's real sample list. Android's docs are explicit that predicted events are 'artificial and inaccurate by definition' and must be replaced by real data. Committed prediction bakes overshoot whiskers into the saved artwork permanently.
- Redrawing the entire in-progress stroke from scratch every frame into the wet layer. It is O(n^2) over the stroke, and for any brush with per-stamp alpha < 1 it also produces the WRONG image (double-darkening where the stroke crosses itself within one stroke).
- Assuming PointerPressed/PointerReleased pair up. The docs explicitly warn they do not. PointerCaptureLost can fire instead of PointerReleased; contacts unreported for >100 ms are cancelled; a monitor/DPI change cancels pointers. Without a unified EndStroke path you get permanently-open strokes.
- Ingesting samples during pen HOVER. PointerMoved fires while IsInRange is true and IsInContact is false. Gate on IsInContact or the app paints while the pen is in the air.
- Planning around InkCanvas / InkPresenter.ActivateCustomDrying / InkSynchronizer. They are UWP-only; InkCanvas is not supported in WinUI 3 desktop (microsoft-ui-xaml #6742), which is also why CommunityToolkit's InfiniteCanvas crashes there. Any tutorial built on custom drying is a dead end for Quill.
- Blaming Quill for a driver-mode problem. Wacom/Huion/XP-Pen drivers have a 'Use Windows Ink' toggle; with it off, the driver serves WinTab and the Windows pointer stack delivers degraded or mouse-like data. Detect the signature (pen-type pointer with pressure pinned at 0.5) and tell the user, rather than trying to work around it.
- Doing allocation, undo-stack work, or any I/O in the pointer handler. A single gen-2 GC pause dwarfs every latency optimization above. Pool sample buffers and defer all bookkeeping to pen-up.
- Rounding pointer positions to integers. Position is sub-pixel float; rounding introduces exactly the quantization jitter the stabilizer is supposed to remove, and it makes slow strokes stair-step.

## canvas-architecture

### Tiled canvas storage: 64px is the universal CPU tile size, and every serious raster paint app uses it
Krita stores pixel data in 64x64 tiles managed by KisTiledDataManager (libs/image/tiles/), with a hash table keyed by (col,row,dataManager) and copy-on-write sharing; unmodified/absent tiles are backed by a shared default tile, and tiles can be swapped to disk or simply destroyed if reconstructible. MyPaint's TiledSurface uses 64x64 tiles as NumPy (64,64,4) uint16 arrays and 'allocates memory just for the tiles that you paint on'. Drawpile/drawdance stores each layer as a grid of 64x64 tiles where 'each tile consumes a fixed 16 KB of memory, except for completely blank tiles, which can be represented by a null pointer' (64*64*4 = 16384 B exactly), plus persistent/transient data structures (Clojure-style) so canvas_diff.cpp can dirty-check two canvas states cheaply by pointer comparison of tile/layer nodes. The payoff is threefold: (1) sparse allocation — a typical artwork layer covers 20-40% of the canvas, so effective per-layer cost drops to ~0.2-0.4x dense; (2) O(1) structural sharing makes undo snapshots and layer duplication nearly free; (3) dirty tracking is a set of tile indices, not a pixel diff.

- **Used by:** Krita (KisTiledDataManager, 64x64, community.kde.org/Krita/Tile_Data_Format), MyPaint (lib/tiledsurface.py + libmypaint mypaint-tiled-surface.c, 64x64 uint16), Drawpile/drawdance (64x64, 16 KB/tile, docs.drawpile.net/help/development/architecture.html), Photoshop (tiled, user-configurable 128/1024 tile size)
- **Relevance:** Quill should make the tile the *storage, undo, and serialization* unit — but 64x64 is the wrong unit for Direct2D/GPU work (see the D2D 64 KB minimum-bitmap finding). Recommend 128x128 RGBA8 tiles (exactly 64 KB, the D2D-recommended minimum allocation) as the single unified tile size: 2480x3508 (A4@300) = 20x28 = 560 tiles/layer; 4961x7016 (A4@600) = 39x55 = 2145 tiles/layer. Store tiles as byte[] in a Dictionary<TileKey,Tile> with a shared immutable 'blank' sentinel, refcounted + copy-on-write.

### Memory math: the real numbers for A4/Letter at 150/300/600 DPI, RGBA8 vs RGBA16F, x layers
Pixel dimensions: A4 (8.268x11.693 in) = 1240x1754 (2.18 Mpx) @150, 2480x3508 (8.70 Mpx) @300, 4961x7016 (34.81 Mpx) @600. Letter (8.5x11 in) = 1275x1650 (2.10 Mpx) @150, 2550x3300 (8.42 Mpx) @300, 5100x6600 (33.66 Mpx) @600. RGBA8 = 4 B/px, RGBA16F = 8 B/px. Dense per-layer cost, A4: @150 = 8.7 MB / 17.4 MB (8/16F); @300 = 34.8 MB / 69.6 MB; @600 = 139.2 MB / 278.5 MB. Letter@300 = 33.7 / 67.3 MB; Letter@600 = 134.6 / 269.3 MB. Multiply by layer count and add the fixed overhead of the render architecture: belowCache + aboveCache + stroke scratch + final composite = 4 extra canvas-sized surfaces. So A4@300 RGBA8 with 20 dense layers = 20*34.8 + 4*34.8 = 835 MB. A4@600 RGBA8 with 20 dense layers = 3.34 GB — not viable. Sparse tiling at a realistic 30% tile occupancy cuts the *layer* term to ~0.3x (caches stay dense): A4@600, 20 layers = 20*41.8 + 4*139.2 = 1.39 GB — viable on a discrete GPU, marginal on integrated. Budget assumption: an unpackaged desktop app on a typical 16 GB laptop with integrated graphics (shared memory) should target ≤1.0-1.5 GB of surface memory; a 6-8 GB discrete GPU can take 2-3 GB.

- **Used by:** Procreate publishes no formula but states plainly that 'the maximum layer limit for a canvas is determined by two main factors — your iPad model's RAM and the pixel dimensions of your canvas' and ships an interactive calculator; layer limits tripled on some iPads in Procreate 5.2 purely from memory-management changes (procreate.com/insight/2021/layer-limits). This is the industry-standard answer: layer count is a derived quantity, not a constant.
- **Relevance:** Quill should do the same: compute maxLayers = floor((memoryBudget - fixedOverhead) / estimatedLayerCost) and surface it in the new-document dialog. Concrete recommendation: default document = A4@300 (2480x3508) RGBA8, soft layer cap 24, hard cap from budget. Offer 150 DPI (8.7 MB/layer, 60+ layers trivially) as the 'sketch' preset and 600 DPI as an expert preset with a loud layer warning. Do NOT make RGBA16F the default — it doubles both memory and compositing bandwidth for a benefit most users won't see in an 8-bit sRGB display pipeline; expose it as a per-document 'high precision' flag mapping to DirectXPixelFormat.R16G16B16A16Float / CanvasBufferPrecision.Precision16Float.

### One giant surface per layer is legal and much simpler in Win2D — the 16384 texture cap, not the API, is the wall
Direct3D feature level 11_0 guarantees D3D11_REQ_TEXTURE2D_U_OR_V_DIMENSION = 16384, so a single 2D texture may be up to 16384x16384 texels; ID2D1RenderTarget::GetMaximumBitmapSize returns exactly this device limit (WARP/software returns 16 megapixels). A4@600 (4961x7016) and even 8K square canvases fit comfortably in one texture. Direct2D/DXGI handle residency, and the driver already stores textures swizzled/tiled internally for cache locality — the Direct2D performance guide notes bitmaps 'might be swizzled' and that this is hidden from you. So the argument for app-level tiling on Windows is NOT 'the GPU can't hold it'. It is: (a) N dense layers blow the memory budget, (b) canvases beyond 16384 px are impossible without tiling, (c) tiles give you cheap undo/serialization/dirty granularity, (d) tiles let you evict cold layers to CPU. Conversely the argument against per-tile GPU bitmaps is explicit in the same doc: 'Video cards typically have a minimum memory allocation size... We recommend that you keep most bitmaps to at least 64 KB and limit the number of bitmaps that are smaller than 4 KB.' A 64x64 RGBA8 tile is 16 KB — under that bar — and a full A4@300 composite from 64px GPU tiles would be 2145 DrawImage calls per layer per frame.

- **Used by:** Microsoft Direct2D performance guidance (learn.microsoft.com/windows/win32/direct2d/improving-direct2d-performance), D3D11 resource limits (learn.microsoft.com/windows/win32/direct3d11/overviews-direct3d-11-resources-limits), ID2D1RenderTarget::GetMaximumBitmapSize
- **Relevance:** Adopt a two-level (hybrid) model rather than picking a side. Level 1 = CPU-authoritative sparse tile store, 128x128 RGBA8, the source of truth for undo, save, and device-lost recovery. Level 2 = GPU working set: each *visible/hot* layer gets one full-canvas CanvasRenderTarget (34.8 MB at A4@300), created lazily from tiles and evicted back to tiles under memory pressure. For documents where width or height > 8192, switch level 2 to 512x512 GPU cache blocks (1 MB each) covering only the viewport plus a margin, so GPU cost becomes O(viewport) instead of O(canvas).

### The three-surface composite: belowCache / activeLayer / aboveCache turns O(layers) per frame into O(1)
Naive compositing redraws every layer every frame. At A4@300 with 20 layers that is 20 * 8.70 Mpx = 174 Mpx of blended fill per frame; on integrated graphics doing D2D SourceOver at a realistic ~10-20 Gpx/s effective, that is 9-17 ms — over budget at 60 Hz, catastrophic at 120 Hz. The standard fix is to collapse the stack into three images: belowCache = flatten(layers strictly below active), activeLayer, aboveCache = flatten(layers strictly above active). Painting only ever touches activeLayer, so per frame you draw exactly 3 images: 3 * (viewport pixels). At a 2560x1440 viewport that is 11.1 Mpx -> ~0.6-1.1 ms integrated, ~0.15 ms discrete, and — critically — independent of both layer count and canvas size. The caches are rebuilt only on layer-selection change, layer reorder, visibility/opacity/blend-mode change, or a non-active layer edit; a full rebuild of belowCache at A4@300 with 15 layers is 15*8.7 = 130 Mpx ≈ 7-13 ms, i.e. one dropped frame on selection change, which is acceptable and can be hidden with an async rebuild. Krita's equivalent machinery is the 'projection': KisBaseRectsWalker walks the layer stack to compute the change/need rects and KisAsyncMerger regenerates only the affected sub-rects of the projection on a worker thread; Krita's own tracker notes the missing API gap ('KisImage::regenerateFinalRect(KisNodeSP node, const QRect &rc)') for preparing a change rect on a layer's final projection.

- **Used by:** Krita (KisAsyncMerger + KisBaseRectsWalker projection system, phabricator.kde.org/T17561), and the general pattern in every layered compositor; browsers do the same with dirty-region-limited layer recomposition
- **Relevance:** Build the caches in *canvas space* at full canvas resolution, not view space. View-space caches are cheaper per pixel but are invalidated by every pan and zoom — fatal for a pen app where the user pans constantly. Canvas-space caches cost 2 extra canvas surfaces (2 * 34.8 MB at A4@300 = 70 MB, cheap) and survive pan/zoom/rotate entirely. Blend/opacity on the active layer must be applied at present time (DrawImage with opacity + CanvasComposite, or a BlendEffect) rather than baked into a cache. Layers with non-Normal blend modes above the active layer cannot be flattened into a simple aboveCache without the below content — handle by flattening the whole above-stack against a transparent backdrop only when all above layers are Normal/SourceOver, otherwise fall back to per-layer compositing for the above group (usually few layers).

### The in-progress stroke needs its own scratch surface — for correctness first, performance second
A brush stroke is thousands of overlapping dabs. If each dab is composited SourceOver directly into the layer at brush opacity, overlaps within a single stroke accumulate and the stroke darkens where it self-intersects, and slow strokes look darker than fast ones. The fix used by every real engine: rasterize dabs into a scratch surface using an accumulate/alpha-darken rule (max or interpolated-alpha rather than plain over), then composite the finished scratch into the layer exactly once at the brush's opacity. Krita calls this 'indirect painting' with a temporary target paint device on the layer (and exposes it to users as Wash/Build-up/Soft modes); MyPaint composites 'all tiles touched by the stroke' but clips the update region 'to the bounding box of the new dabs'; Drawpile calls it a sublayer and had an explicit issue about switching indirect/wash mode to interpolated alpha-darken (drawpile#1446). Secondary benefits: cancelling or undoing an in-progress stroke is a scratch clear, not a layer restore; eraser and blend-mode strokes act on the stroke as a unit; and the layer's own undo snapshot is taken once at stroke end from a single known bbox.

- **Used by:** Krita (indirect painting / temporary target, exposed as Wash and Soft painting modes), MyPaint (stroke-scoped dab compositing with bbox-clipped update region), Drawpile (sublayer with alpha-darken), Photoshop/Clip Studio (equivalent 'wet' stroke buffer), Microsoft's own inking stack ('the wet ink canvas is where ink is rendered as it's drawn, but is not dried... when the stroke is completed, the stroke is processed on the UI thread and rendered dry')
- **Relevance:** Allocate one canvas-sized RGBA8 scratch CanvasRenderTarget (34.8 MB at A4@300), pooled and reused across strokes, and track a strokeDirtyRect. Never clear the whole scratch — clear only strokeDirtyRect via a drawing session with PushAxisAlignedClip + CanvasBlend.Copy of transparent (a full A4@600 clear is 34.8 Mpx of fill, several ms). Dab stamping into the scratch should use CanvasSpriteBatch (ds.CreateSpriteBatch) which batches many small quads into few GPU draw calls — this is the single highest-leverage Win2D API for a dab-stamping brush engine. Present the layer as DrawImage(layerRT) then DrawImage(scratchRT, opacity: brushOpacity) so the user sees the stroke live without it being committed.

### Dirty-rectangle invalidation: the algorithm, the coalescing heuristic, and the tile-alignment rule
Per-dab dirty rect = dab center ± (radius * maxPressureScale) inflated by ceil(antialias margin) + any per-brush effect kernel radius (a blur/scatter brush expands this substantially — Direct2D effects expand the required *source* rect by the kernel radius, so a 20px Gaussian needs ~60px of margin at 3 sigma). Union the per-dab rects into a per-frame dirty rect and separately into a per-stroke bbox. Maintain the invalidation set as a small list of rects with a coalescing heuristic: merge rect A into rect B when area(A ∪ B) <= k * (area(A) + area(B)) with k ~1.2-1.4, otherwise keep them separate; cap the list at ~8-16 rects and collapse to the bounding box beyond that. Snap every rect outward to the storage tile grid (128px) before using it to invalidate tiles/caches, so you never do partial-tile uploads or half-invalid cache state. Transform to view space by mapping the four corners through the view matrix and taking the bounding box, then inflate by 2px to cover bilinear filter taps. MyPaint's rule is the minimal version of this: 'all tiles touched by the stroke are composited completely, but the update region is clipped to the bounding box of the new dabs'.

- **Used by:** MyPaint (bbox-clipped update region over fully-composited touched tiles), Krita (KisBaseRectsWalker change-rect/need-rect propagation through the layer stack — note that the need-rect *grows* as it walks through blur-like masks, exactly the effect-kernel expansion problem), Win2D CanvasVirtualControl (RegionsInvalidated delivers a collection of invalidated rects; Invalidate(Rect) marks a sub-region)
- **Relevance:** Keep two independent dirty concepts and don't conflate them: (1) *content* dirty rects in canvas space, which invalidate layer RTs, belowCache/aboveCache, and the tile store; (2) *presentation* dirty rects in view space. With a flip-model swap chain the back buffer contents are undefined after Present, so the presentation path must redraw the full viewport each frame anyway — at 11 Mpx that is ~1 ms and not worth optimizing. Spend all the dirty-rect effort on (1).

### GPU readback (GetPixelBytes) is the performance cliff, and it also dictates the undo design
CanvasBitmap/CanvasRenderTarget.GetPixelBytes maps the surface for CPU read, which forces a Flush, a full pipeline stall waiting for the GPU to drain, a copy into a CPU-accessible staging resource, and then a memcpy. Win2D issue #101 and the D2D guidance both flag this: readback is 'well known for being a slow operation', and the Win2D team notes that GetPixelBytes/SetPixelBytes on rectangular *subregions* are significantly slower than the full-image variants. Realistic cost for a full A4@300 RGBA8 surface (34.8 MB) is 4-8 ms of transfer over PCIe staging plus 3-10 ms of stall — call it 10-15 ms, i.e. one to two dropped frames, every time. Anything in the painting path that needs CPU pixels is therefore an architectural hazard: color picker / eyedropper, flood fill, CPU-side smudge/blend brushes, per-stroke undo snapshots, autosave, and thumbnail generation. The counterpart is that GPU-to-GPU copies are cheap: CanvasBitmap.CopyPixelsFromBitmap(source, destX, destY, srcX, srcY, w, h) is a driver blit with no CPU sync, and Direct2D's own guidance is that CopyFrom* is 'much less expensive' than re-rendering because render targets are stored in a less-optimal swizzle than pure textures.

- **Used by:** Win2D issue #101 'Best way to copy raw pixels' and issue #453; Microsoft Direct2D performance guide section 'Copying bitmaps'; Win2D added IBuffer overloads of GetPixelBytes/SetPixelBytes/CreateFromBytes specifically to reduce allocation churn around readback
- **Relevance:** Never call GetPixelBytes on the render thread during a stroke. Design undo as GPU-resident tile snapshots: at stroke start, for every 128px tile the stroke's bbox will touch, CopyPixelsFromBitmap the tile out of the layer RT into a pooled 'undo' CanvasRenderTarget atlas in VRAM. Cost is a few hundred KB of GPU-to-GPU blit, sub-0.1 ms. Then, on a background thread with the undo stack under a lock, spill the oldest undo generations from VRAM to the CPU tile store via GetPixelBytes — off the critical path, batched, one large readback rather than many small ones. Eyedropper: read from the *composite* at a 1x1 rect only, or better, keep a low-res CPU mirror of the composite updated lazily. Flood fill: it must run on the CPU, so run it against the CPU tile store (another reason the tile store is authoritative) and upload the result.

### Win2D control choice: CanvasSwapChainPanel is the right presentation surface for a pen-first paint app
Microsoft documents the layering explicitly: CanvasControl wraps CanvasImageSource, CanvasVirtualControl wraps CanvasVirtualImageSource, CanvasAnimatedControl wraps CanvasSwapChainPanel + CanvasSwapChain. (a) CanvasControl/CanvasImageSource is tied to the XAML render tick and — critically — 'whenever a drawing session is created on a CanvasImageSource, the CanvasImageSource is cleared', so it can never be used for incremental accumulation. (b) CanvasVirtualControl virtualizes *its own presentation surface*, not your document: the OS tracks which regions of the control's surface are valid and raises RegionsInvalidated for invalid ones. It is designed for 'displaying images that are much larger than the screen (for example in a ScrollViewer)'. For a paint canvas with continuous zoom/rotate you own the transform anyway, so the virtualization mostly buys you a second cached copy of your pixels you didn't want; it also does not auto-invalidate on resize (you must call Invalidate() from SizeChanged). (c) CanvasSwapChainPanel is 'a relatively thin wrapper around CanvasSwapChain', the app decides Present timing, and content 'can be re-drawn with a low latency, as it is not tied to the XAML framework refresh timer'. Microsoft's inking guidance quantifies this: routing input directly to a swap chain rather than through the XAML object tree gave 'up to an 80% reduction in end-to-end latency' on ink tests that averaged 100+ ms. Costs: CanvasSwapChain 'has heavier resource costs than CanvasImageSource' and MS advises no more than one or two swap chains on screen; XAML transforms and opacity do not apply to it; you must ResizeBuffers on SizeChanged and set DPI yourself; and device-lost is NOT auto-handled the way it is inside the controls.

- **Used by:** Microsoft Win2D 'Using Win2D without built-in controls' (learn.microsoft.com/windows/apps/develop/win2d/using-win2d-without-built-in-controls); Windows 8.1 XAML/DirectX interop blog on wet-ink SwapChainPanel latency; Microsoft's own coloring-book WinUI sample
- **Relevance:** Use exactly one CanvasSwapChainPanel for the art surface, with all UI (toolbars, layer panel, color picker) as normal XAML siblings. Render on a dedicated render thread, not the UI thread. Pair it with SwapChainPanel.CreateCoreIndependentInputSource(InputPointerSourceDeviceKinds.Pen | Mouse | Touch) so pen events arrive on an independent input thread at full report rate (240 Hz on Surface hardware) rather than being throttled to the UI thread's 60 Hz pump. If schedule pressure forces an MVP shortcut, CanvasControl is an acceptable *temporary* stand-in (it auto-handles device lost and DPI) — but write the renderer against an ISurfacePresenter abstraction from day one so the swap is a one-file change, because retrofitting a threaded swap chain later is a rewrite.

### CanvasRenderTarget, CanvasCommandList, and effect graphs: which one to reach for
CanvasRenderTarget is a real GPU bitmap you can draw into repeatedly; its contents persist across drawing sessions. It is the correct type for layers, caches, and the stroke scratch. CanvasCommandList records *vector* drawing commands and 'does not have a DPI, because it contains vector drawing instructions rather than a bitmap image. DPI only comes into play during the rasterization process' — it is resolution-independent and replayable, which makes it attractive for a stroke you might want to re-rasterize at a new zoom, but its memory and replay cost grow linearly with the number of recorded dabs, so it is unsuitable as the accumulation target for a long stroke. Effect graphs (CanvasEffect subclasses like BlendEffect with its 28 blend modes, CompositeEffect for Porter-Duff, GaussianBlurEffect) are Direct2D effect graphs: each node is a shader pass writing an intermediate the size of the requested output rect, and D2D internally tiles the evaluation. Two properties matter enormously: CacheOutput=true pins a node's rendered output so downstream frames don't re-evaluate the subgraph, and BufferPrecision selects the intermediate format (Precision8UIntNormalized vs Precision16Float) which directly doubles or halves intermediate bandwidth. The Direct2D perf guide's core caching advice — 'Cache content if you render the same content frame over frame' via full-scene caching to a color bitmap, A8 opacity masks + FillOpacityMask, or geometry realizations — is precisely the belowCache/aboveCache pattern, and it also warns that resource creation/deletion 'are expensive operations because they require lots of overhead for communicating with the video card', so pool and reuse render targets rather than allocating per stroke.

- **Used by:** Win2D API docs for CanvasCommandList / CanvasRenderTarget; Microsoft 'Improving the performance of Direct2D apps' (caching, atlasing, resource reuse, D2D1_DEVICE_CONTEXT_OPTIONS_ENABLE_MULTI_THREADED_OPTIMIZATIONS for path geometry)
- **Relevance:** Layers, caches, scratch = CanvasRenderTarget, all pooled by size in a free-list (a 34.8 MB allocation is not something to do per stroke). Blend layers with CanvasDrawingSession.DrawImage(bitmap, destRect, srcRect, opacity, interpolation, CanvasComposite.SourceOver) for Normal mode — this is the intermediate-free fast path. Only instantiate a BlendEffect when the layer's blend mode is non-Normal, and set CacheOutput on the belowCache/aboveCache nodes. Use CanvasCommandList only for the vector overlay layer (selection marching ants, guides, shape tool previews), never for accumulated brush dabs.

### DPI: canvas surfaces must be pinned at 96 DPI, only the presentation surface follows the display
Win2D's rule is pixels = dips * dpi / 96; integer-typed APIs are pixels, float-typed APIs are DIPs. Every bitmap-bearing resource carries a DPI: CanvasBitmap, CanvasRenderTarget, CanvasSwapChain, the controls, CanvasImageSource. The controls 'automatically use the same DPI as the display', and when the display DPI changes they 'raise the CreateResources event and pass a CanvasCreateResourcesReason of DpiChanged. Apps should respond to this event by recreating any resources (such as rendertargets) that depend on the DPI of the control.' There are two constructor overloads that make this a trap: CanvasRenderTarget(ICanvasResourceCreatorWithDpi, width, height) inherits the control's DPI, while CanvasRenderTarget(ICanvasResourceCreator device, width, height, dpi) takes it explicitly. If you use the first form for your layers, a 2480x3508 layer created on a 150%-scaling monitor is actually 3720x5262 pixels, and dragging the window to a 100% monitor fires DpiChanged and asks you to recreate — i.e. resample — the user's artwork. Separately, when a bitmap of a different DPI is used as an effect source, Win2D silently inserts a DpiCompensationEffect to rescale it.

- **Used by:** Win2D 'DPI and DIPs' (learn.microsoft.com/windows/apps/develop/win2d/dpi-and-dips)
- **Relevance:** Create every canvas-space surface (layer RTs, belowCache, aboveCache, scratch, undo atlas) with the explicit-DPI overload passing 96f, so 1 DIP == 1 pixel == 1 canvas pixel and the document is completely decoupled from display scaling. Handle display DPI only at the presentation boundary: the swap chain's DPI and the view matrix. On DpiChanged, recreate/resize *only* the swap chain and any view-space overlays — never touch document surfaces. Also set CanvasDrawingSession.Units = CanvasUnits.Pixels when drawing into canvas surfaces to remove any residual DIP arithmetic from the brush engine.

### Device lost will wipe every pixel in VRAM — so the CPU tile store is a correctness requirement, not an optimization
Device lost occurs on 'GPU hardware malfunction, driver bugs, driver software updates, or switching the app from one GPU to another'; a lost device is unusable and 'the app must create a new device and then recreate all its graphics resources.' Driver updates alone make this a routine event on Windows, not an exotic one. The Win2D controls auto-recover by recreating their CanvasDevice and raising CreateResources with reason NewDevice — but they only auto-catch exceptions thrown from CreateResources, Update, Draw, or RegionsInvalidated handlers. A paint app draws from pointer-input handlers and a background render thread, which are explicitly outside that protection, so you must wrap those in the documented pattern: catch (Exception e) when (canvasDevice.IsDeviceLost(e.HResult)) { canvasDevice.RaiseDeviceLost(); }. RaiseDeviceLost notifies any controls sharing the device to run their recovery path; if the device came from somewhere other than a control, subscribe to CanvasDevice.DeviceLost. Recovery recreates the *objects* — nothing recreates the *contents*. If the artwork exists only as CanvasRenderTargets, a driver update destroys the user's work. Test procedure is documented: disable the display adapter in Device Manager, which forces Windows onto WARP and triggers device lost in all running apps.

- **Used by:** Win2D 'Handling device lost' (learn.microsoft.com/windows/apps/develop/win2d/handling-device-lost); CanvasDevice.DeviceLost / IsDeviceLost / RaiseDeviceLost; Win2D issues #194 (DeviceLost on CanvasVirtualControl) and #750 (Win2D graphics erased after GPU device re-enabled)
- **Relevance:** Make the CPU sparse tile store the single source of truth and treat all GPU surfaces as caches that can be regenerated from it. Layer RT contents must be written back to tiles at every commit point (stroke end, tool change, idle) — via the batched background readback path, not synchronously. On device lost: dispose all Win2D resources, recreate the CanvasDevice, reallocate layer RTs, and repopulate them from tiles with SetPixelBytes; the user sees a brief hitch and loses at most the in-flight stroke. Add a periodic disk autosave of dirty tiles as a second line of defence. Wrap every drawing entry point — input handler, render loop, thumbnail generator, export — in the IsDeviceLost/RaiseDeviceLost guard, and add a debug-menu 'simulate device lost' that calls RaiseDeviceLost directly so this path is exercised in normal development rather than discovered in the field.

### Realistic per-frame budget and what canvas size / layer count Quill can actually support
Frame budget: 8.3 ms at 120 Hz, 16.7 ms at 60 Hz; target ≤4 ms GPU for the paint path to leave headroom for XAML UI on the same device. Breakdown at A4@300 (2480x3508), 2560x1440 viewport, 100% zoom, with the three-surface architecture: (1) dab stamping into scratch — a 30px brush moving 500 px in a frame at 0.25 spacing is ~67 dabs covering ~15,000 px² with ~4x overdraw = 0.06 Mpx of fill, well under 0.1 ms, and via CanvasSpriteBatch it is a handful of draw calls not 67; (2) scratch→present composite over the dirty rect, negligible; (3) full-viewport present of belowCache + activeLayer(+scratch) + aboveCache = 3-4 * 3.7 Mpx = 11-15 Mpx of blended fill = ~0.6-1.5 ms on integrated (Iris Xe class), ~0.15-0.3 ms discrete. Total well inside 4 ms, and — the key property — invariant to canvas size and layer count. The costs that are NOT invariant and must be moved off the critical path: cache rebuild on layer-selection change (15 layers * 8.70 Mpx = 130 Mpx ≈ 7-13 ms integrated, one dropped frame — do it async with the old cache still presented), layer RT allocation (34.8 MB, 5-20 ms — pool it), and any readback (10-15 ms — background thread only). Memory-derived layer ceilings assuming a 1.2 GB surface budget and 4 canvas-sized surfaces of fixed overhead, sparse tiling at 30% occupancy for cold layers and dense RTs for the ~8 hot (recently-touched) layers: A4@150 → 100+ layers; A4@300 → ~40 layers comfortable, 24 as a conservative default cap; A4@600 → ~16-20 layers; A4@600 RGBA16F → ~8 layers.

- **Used by:** Derived from the D2D fill-rate model in 'Improving the performance of Direct2D apps' (the fill-rate-bound vs vertex-bound diagnostic: shrink the render target and see if perf scales), combined with the Procreate model that layer limits are a function of RAM and canvas pixel count
- **Relevance:** Ship A4@300 RGBA8 as the default document with a 24-layer soft cap, and gate 600 DPI and RGBA16F behind explicit expert choices that show the computed layer ceiling before creation. Instrument from day one: a debug HUD showing surface memory in use, layer RT count, cache-rebuild count per second, dirty-rect area per frame, and GPU frame time. The single number to watch is cache rebuilds per second — if it is above ~1 during normal painting, the below/above cache invalidation logic is wrong and every other optimization is moot.

### Recommendations
- Two-level storage: a CPU-authoritative sparse tile store (128x128 RGBA8 = exactly 64 KB, meeting Direct2D's recommended 64 KB minimum allocation, with a shared blank-tile sentinel and refcounted copy-on-write) as the source of truth, plus a GPU working set of full-canvas CanvasRenderTargets for hot layers, evictable back to tiles. Switch the GPU tier to 512x512 viewport-covering blocks only when canvas width or height exceeds 8192.
- Three-surface compositing in canvas space: belowCache, activeLayer (+ scratch), aboveCache. Rebuild caches only on selection/reorder/visibility/opacity/blend change or non-active-layer edits, and rebuild asynchronously while still presenting the stale cache. This makes per-frame cost independent of layer count and canvas size.
- Dedicated stroke scratch CanvasRenderTarget with alpha-darken/max dab accumulation, composited into the layer once at stroke end at brush opacity. This is a correctness requirement (prevents self-overlap darkening within a stroke), not just a performance one. Clear only the tracked stroke bbox, never the whole surface.
- Present through a single CanvasSwapChainPanel on a dedicated render thread, with SwapChainPanel.CreateCoreIndependentInputSource for full-rate pen input off the UI thread. Write the renderer behind an ISurfacePresenter interface so a CanvasControl MVP can be swapped for the swap chain without a rewrite.
- Create every document-space surface with the explicit-DPI CanvasRenderTarget overload passing 96f, and set CanvasDrawingSession.Units = Pixels when drawing into them. Only the swap chain and view-space overlays follow display DPI. This makes the document immune to monitor-scaling changes.
- Zero GPU readback on the render thread. Undo = GPU-to-GPU CopyPixelsFromBitmap of the touched 128px tiles into a pooled VRAM undo atlas at stroke start; spill old generations to the CPU tile store on a background thread with batched large GetPixelBytes calls. Flood fill and eyedropper read the CPU tile store, not the GPU.
- Use DrawImage + CanvasComposite.SourceOver as the compositing fast path for Normal-mode layers; instantiate BlendEffect only for non-Normal modes, and set CacheOutput on cache nodes. Use CanvasSpriteBatch for dab stamping. Pool and reuse all render targets — a 34.8 MB allocation must never happen mid-stroke.
- Treat device lost as expected. Wrap every drawing entry point (input handler, render loop, export, thumbnails) in catch(e) when (device.IsDeviceLost(e.HResult)) { device.RaiseDeviceLost(); }, repopulate layer RTs from the CPU tile store on recovery, add periodic dirty-tile disk autosave, and ship a debug 'simulate device lost' command so the path is exercised continuously.
- Default document A4@300 (2480x3508) RGBA8 premultiplied with a 24-layer soft cap. Compute and display maxLayers = floor((budget - 4 * canvasSurfaceBytes) / perLayerBytes) in the new-document dialog, Procreate-style. Gate 600 DPI and RGBA16F behind expert presets with the computed ceiling shown before creation.
- Maintain a mip pyramid (or use ScaleEffect with anisotropic/HighQualityCubic sampling) for the composite when zoom < 100%, otherwise a 35 Mpx canvas shown at 25% will shimmer and alias badly under default linear sampling.
- Instrument from day one with a debug HUD: surface memory in use, live layer RT count, cache rebuilds per second, dirty-rect area per frame, GPU frame time, readback count. Cache rebuilds per second during normal painting is the canary — anything above ~1 means the invalidation logic is broken.

### Pitfalls
- Using 64x64 tiles as GPU-resident Direct2D bitmaps. 64x64 RGBA8 = 16 KB, below Microsoft's explicit 'keep most bitmaps to at least 64 KB' guidance, and a full A4@300 layer would be 2145 DrawImage calls. 64x64 is right for CPU storage (Krita, MyPaint, Drawpile all use it) and wrong for GPU compositing granularity.
- Creating layer render targets with the DPI-inheriting overload (ICanvasResourceCreatorWithDpi). On a 150%-scaling monitor a '2480x3508' layer is silently 3720x5262 real pixels, and moving the window to a 100% monitor fires CreateResources(DpiChanged) asking you to recreate — i.e. resample — the artwork.
- Using CanvasImageSource for incremental painting. 'Whenever a drawing session is created on a CanvasImageSource, the CanvasImageSource is cleared' — accumulation is impossible by design. Same trap indirectly applies to CanvasControl.
- Assuming CanvasVirtualControl virtualizes your document. It virtualizes its own presentation surface; your document still needs independent storage, and you end up paying for a second cached copy of pixels. It also does not auto-invalidate on resize — you must call Invalidate() from SizeChanged or the control shows stale content.
- Any GetPixelBytes call in the stroke path. A full A4@300 readback is ~10-15 ms including the pipeline stall. Subregion GetPixelBytes/SetPixelBytes are documented by the Win2D team as significantly slower than whole-image variants, so 'just read a small rect' is not the escape hatch it looks like.
- Storing the artwork only in VRAM. A routine GPU driver update triggers device lost, all CanvasRenderTargets become invalid, and nothing in Win2D recreates their contents. The user loses the drawing.
- Relying on the Win2D controls' automatic device-lost recovery while drawing from pointer handlers or a background thread. The controls only auto-catch exceptions from CreateResources, Update, Draw, and RegionsInvalidated — exactly the handlers a paint app does the least work in.
- Chaining N BlendEffects to composite N layers. Each effect node materializes a full-size intermediate, so a 20-layer graph is a 20x fill-rate multiplier plus 20 intermediate allocations. Without CacheOutput, the entire subgraph re-evaluates every frame.
- Forgetting that effect graphs expand the required source rect. A Gaussian blur mask or scatter brush needs ~3 sigma of extra margin on every dirty rect; Krita hits the same thing as need-rect growth in KisBaseRectsWalker. Under-inflating produces visible seams at dirty-rect boundaries that only appear on some brushes.
- Not snapping dirty rects outward to the tile grid before invalidating tiles or caches. Produces partial-tile uploads, half-valid cache state, and 1px seam artifacts that are extremely hard to reproduce.
- Compositing each dab SourceOver directly into the layer. Looks correct on fast strokes and visibly darkens on slow or self-overlapping strokes — the classic symptom that the scratch/indirect-painting surface is missing. Drawpile filed this as a real bug (#1446) and had to move to interpolated alpha-darken.
- Expecting a flip-model swap chain's back buffer to retain the previous frame. Contents are undefined after Present, so incremental accumulation directly on the back buffer silently produces garbage on some drivers and works on others. Redraw the full viewport each frame (~1 ms at 2560x1440 with three surfaces).
- Clearing the full-canvas scratch surface at every stroke start. At A4@600 that is 34.8 Mpx of fill per stroke — several milliseconds of stutter at exactly the moment the user expects the app to be most responsive.
- Allocating render targets per stroke or per layer-selection change. Direct2D guidance is explicit that 'resource creation and deletion on hardware are expensive operations'; a 34.8 MB allocation is 5-20 ms. Pool by size.
- Ignoring premultiplied alpha in the dab shader. Direct2D's fastest path is B8G8R8A8_UNORM premultiplied; feeding straight-alpha colors produces dark fringes on every antialiased dab edge that will be misdiagnosed as a brush-engine bug.
- Mixing sRGB-space and linear-space blending between the DrawImage compositing path and the effect-graph path. The two paths will produce visibly different results for the same blend mode, and the discrepancy only shows up on mid-tones.
- Choosing RGBA16F as the default 'because it's better'. It doubles memory (69.6 vs 34.8 MB per A4@300 layer) and halves effective compositing fill rate, for a benefit invisible in an 8-bit sRGB display pipeline. It is a per-document expert option, not a default.

## color-management

### sRGB is an encoding, not a linear intensity — the exact piecewise transfer function
sRGB values are perceptually-spaced code words, not light. Decode (sRGB→linear): L = S/12.92 if S <= 0.04045, else L = ((S+0.055)/1.055)^2.4. Encode (linear→sRGB): S = 12.92*L if L <= 0.0031308, else S = 1.055*L^(1/2.4) - 0.055. The linear segment near black exists so the derivative stays finite at 0 (a pure power law has infinite slope at 0, which breaks quantization and derivatives). Effective exponent averaged over the range is ~2.2, but using pow(x,2.2) instead of the piecewise curve introduces errors up to ~1.5/255 near black — visible in dark gradients. The reason the encoding exists at all: with 8 bits, a linear allocation spends ~128 of 256 codes on the top quarter of perceived luminance and starves the shadows, where the eye's Weber-fraction sensitivity (~1% relative) is sharpest. Gamma encoding redistributes codes so each step is roughly a constant *perceptual* increment.

- **Used by:** IEC 61966-2-1 sRGB spec; John Novak's 'What every coder should know about gamma' (blog.johnnovak.net) documents the piecewise formula and the precision-allocation argument; GIMP's precision docs state that 8-bit *linear* encoding produces 'horribly posterized shadows'.
- **Relevance:** Quill must decide, explicitly and in one place, what the numbers in a layer buffer MEAN. Today InkSurface.cs uses the default CanvasRenderTarget ctor, which is B8G8R8A8UNorm + Premultiplied + sRGB-encoded values — i.e. every blend Quill currently does is mathematically wrong-but-conventional. Art Mode needs a documented invariant: 'layer pixels are linear-light, premultiplied, Rec.709/sRGB primaries, D65.' Every entry/exit point (color picker, file load, PNG export, eyedropper readout) applies the transform above.

### Why blending, antialiasing and gradients must happen in linear light — the dark-fringe/midpoint-darkening mechanism
Physical light superposition is additive: two half-covered subpixels of white and black produce 0.5 of the light. If you average the *encoded* values instead — (0 + 255)/2 = 128 — and the display then applies ~2.2 gamma, you emit 0.5^2.2 ≈ 0.22 of full luminance, not 0.5. The midpoint is ~2.3x too dark. Correct: decode both to linear (0.0 and 1.0), average to 0.5, re-encode to 188/255. This is the entire mechanism behind: (a) dark halos around bright objects when you blur; (b) the muddy dark band in a blue→yellow gradient interpolated in sRGB; (c) soft-brush edges in gamma-incorrect paint programs showing 'weird darkish transition bands' (Novak's phrasing); (d) antialiased edges reading as too heavy/dark. It is worst where the two endpoints are far apart in luminance, and vanishes when they are equal — which is why the bug hides on flat-colored art and screams on airbrush work over a dark ground. Note the artifact direction: sRGB-space interpolation is always DARKER than correct, never lighter, because the encode curve is concave.

- **Used by:** Krita and Pixelmator are cited as the drawing programs that got gamma-correctness right; Affinity Photo exposes an explicit 'linear light blending' compositing toggle; Unity's Linear vs Gamma color space docs describe the same failure; NVIDIA GPU Gems 3 ch.24 'The Importance of Being Linear'; Pixar RenderMan compositing docs describe dark fringes when merging non-linearized elements.
- **Relevance:** This is the single highest-leverage correctness decision in Art Mode. Soft round brushes, airbrush, smudge, blur, gaussian glow, layer opacity, and the eraser all reduce to weighted averages of two colors. Get the working space linear and all of them are right at once; leave it sRGB and every one of them is individually, subtly wrong in a way artists describe as 'the colors go muddy' without being able to name it.

### Gamma-correct alpha compositing and the premultiplication-order trap
Porter-Duff source-over is Cout = Cs*As + Cd*Ad*(1-As), Aout = As + Ad*(1-As); premultiplied form is simply Cout' = Cs' + Cd'*(1-As). The correctness requirement is that C be LINEAR light. The classic bug is doing the premultiply on sRGB-encoded values: srgb(C)*A is not srgb(C*A), so the stored premultiplied value is meaningless and unpremultiplying it (divide by A) at low alpha amplifies the encoding error enormously — this is the source of dark halos on composited PNGs with soft edges. Correct order is always: decode to linear → multiply by alpha → blend → (only at the very end) encode. Alpha itself is NEVER gamma-encoded; it is a coverage/geometry term and stays linear. A second trap: at A=0 the premultiplied RGB is all-zero, so any 'average the pixels' operation (eyedropper radius, blur, downscale) that ignores alpha weighting drags results toward black. Correct averaging is: average premultiplied RGB and alpha independently, then unpremultiply the result.

- **Used by:** Direct2D's own docs state 'Colors are always treated as straight alpha by Direct2D drawing commands and brushes, regardless of the destination format' — D2D does the premultiply for you into whatever the target format is, so the format determines whether that premultiply is linear-correct. Chris Lomont's 'Correct alpha and gamma for image processing' documents the ordering bug in detail.
- **Relevance:** Quill's layer stack, eraser, opacity slider, clipping masks and flatten-on-export all run this equation thousands of times per stroke. Fixing the working space to linear makes D2D's built-in premultiply correct for free. Also: alpha-weighted averaging must be used in the eyedropper and in any mip/thumbnail generation, or transparent regions bleed black into sampled colors.

### Bit depth: where banding appears, and why 8-bit linear is unusable while 8-bit sRGB is merely marginal
The eye resolves roughly a 1% relative luminance difference (Weber). 8-bit sRGB gives midtone steps around 0.5-1% relative — just barely below threshold, which is why 8-bit sRGB gradients band on large smooth areas but usually pass on textured art. 8-bit LINEAR is catastrophic: the step from code 1 to 2 is a 100% relative change, so the darkest ~30 codes are separated by visible jumps — posterized shadows. Matching 8-bit sRGB's perceptual step spacing with a linear encoding requires ~12 bits; comfortable headroom for repeated editing needs 14-16. 16-bit unsigned normalized linear (65536 uniform steps) is sufficient: worst-case relative step at code 1 is still 100%, but code 1 is 1/65536 of full white, far below the display's black floor, so it is irrelevant in practice. 16-bit half float (IEEE 754 binary16: 1 sign, 5 exponent, 10 stored mantissa bits) is better still because precision is RELATIVE: ~11 bits of mantissa, i.e. ~0.05% relative error at every magnitude, from 6.1e-5 (smallest normal) down to 6e-8 (subnormal). Krita's docs note this works out to roughly 10-11 bits of usable precision per channel for scene-referred use, and that linear working spaces need 16 bits minimum. The other axis is ACCUMULATION error, discussed separately, which is the painting-specific reason 8 bits fails even when a single blend would look fine.

- **Used by:** Krita bit-depth docs (8-bit int, 16-bit int, 16-bit float, 32-bit float, with the explicit warning that 32-bit float 'will quickly take up your RAM'); GIMP 2.10/3.0 precision docs; Krita's scene-linear painting guide states linear color 'requires a bit depth of at the least 16bit to work correctly'; SMPTE ST 2084 PQ was designed because even gamma-2.4 would need 15+ bits for HDR.
- **Relevance:** Determines the layer buffer format. It also determines memory: an FP16 RGBA layer is 8 bytes/px, so a 4096x4096 layer is 128 MB vs 64 MB for 8-bit. This forces tiled layer storage (see recommendations) rather than one monolithic CanvasRenderTarget per layer.

### Accumulation error: the painting-specific argument that kills 8-bit, independent of banding
A digital brush stroke is not one composite — it is a dense train of overlapping low-alpha stamps. With spacing at 5-10% of brush diameter and flow at 2-5%, a single stroke performs 50-500 source-over operations on the same destination pixel. In an 8-bit destination, each operation rounds to 1/255. The increment for one dab is delta = A*(Csrc - Cdst). When A=0.02 and (Csrc - Cdst) is 20/255, delta = 0.4/255 — which rounds to 0 (round-to-nearest) or 0 (truncation). The stroke STALLS: repeated painting never converges to the source color, it asymptotes early and then quantizes into visible steps. Artists experience this as 'low-flow airbrush won't build up' and 'soft gradients get stair-steppy the more I work them'. With FP16 the per-dab increment is representable at any magnitude (relative precision ~5e-4), so buildup converges smoothly. This effect compounds with the linear-space requirement: in linear space the dark end has even smaller absolute deltas, so 8-bit linear is doubly broken.

- **Used by:** Krita, Photoshop and Procreate all offer/default to higher-than-8-bit documents for painting workflows for this reason; Krita's docs frame 16-bit as the floor for linear work. The mechanism is the standard fixed-point accumulation-error argument from image compositing.
- **Relevance:** This is the argument to give the user for why Art Mode documents are heavier than Notes documents. It is also the reason a 'cheap' path (8-bit sRGB target with a _SRGB DXGI view, which gets you linear blending for free in the ROP) is NOT sufficient: the ROP blends in high precision but STORES back to 8 bits, so accumulation error remains.

### What Direct2D/Win2D actually give you — formats, buffer precision, and where D2D is and isn't color-space aware
Direct2D's blend is color-space AGNOSTIC: it blends the numbers stored in the target. So the pixel format alone decides whether blending is linear. Three viable configurations: (1) DXGI_FORMAT_B8G8R8A8_UNORM (Quill's current default) — values are sRGB-encoded, blending is gamma-space and wrong. (2) DXGI_FORMAT_B8G8R8A8_UNORM_SRGB — the D3D ROP decodes sRGB→linear on read, blends linear at high internal precision, encodes back on write. Free linear blending, 4 bytes/px, but still 8-bit storage (accumulation error). (3) DXGI_FORMAT_R16G16B16A16_FLOAT — you store linear values yourself; blending is linear and storage is FP16. Microsoft's 'Supported Pixel Formats and Alpha Modes' doc confirms ID2D1DeviceContext (Windows 8+) supports B8G8R8A8_UNORM_SRGB, R8G8B8A8_UNORM_SRGB, R16G16B16A16_UNORM, R16G16B16A16_FLOAT and R32G32B32A32_FLOAT, gated on ID2D1DeviceContext::IsDxgiFormatSupported (Win2D: CanvasDevice.IsPixelFormatSupported(DirectXPixelFormat.R16G16B16A16Float)). Separately, EFFECT graphs have their own intermediate-texture precision: D2D1_BUFFER_PRECISION_{8BPC_UNORM, 8BPC_UNORM_SRGB, 16BPC_UNORM, 16BPC_FLOAT, 32BPC_FLOAT}, set via ID2D1DeviceContext::SetRenderingControls or D2D1_PROPERTY_PRECISION per effect; precision propagates through the graph taking the max at joins, and defaults are inferred from input bitmap formats. Values outside [0,1] survive only at FLOAT precision; at UNORM precision D2D clamps, and on Windows 10+ shader linking means the clamp may or may not occur depending on graph topology — so clamping behaviour is NOT guaranteed unless you force it (ColorMatrix with ClampOutput=TRUE) or force float precision. D2D is color-space aware in exactly two places: gradient stop interpolation (D2D1_GAMMA_1_0 vs D2D1_GAMMA_2_2) and the D2D1ColorManagement effect (which needs D2D1_COLORMANAGEMENT_QUALITY_BEST for float/DXGI color space support). Win2D surfaces color space as CanvasColorSpace { Custom=0, Srgb=1, ScRgb=2 }. FP16 + linear + sRGB primaries IS scRGB.

- **Used by:** Microsoft Learn Direct2D 'Supported Pixel Formats and Alpha Modes' and 'Precision and numerical clipping in effect graphs'; Win2D CanvasRenderTarget has a 5-arg overload (ICanvasResourceCreator, width, height, dpi, DirectXPixelFormat, CanvasAlphaMode); the D2DAdvancedColorImages sample in microsoft/Windows-universal-samples.
- **Relevance:** Quill already uses CanvasRenderTarget in InkSurface.cs with the 4-arg overload (implicit B8G8R8A8UNorm/Premultiplied). Art Mode should use the 5-arg overload with R16G16B16A16Float + Premultiplied, and set rendering controls to 16BPC_FLOAT so effect intermediates don't silently quantize back to 8-bit. Also: D2D1_GAMMA_1_0 must be set on every gradient brush in Art Mode, and existing Notes-mode gradients keep 2_2 for visual parity.

### Windows display color management: what an unmanaged app actually gets
Historically (and still, on any machine without Auto Color Management enabled): the app writes 8-bit values, DWM composites them without any transform, and they hit the panel raw. The values are IMPLICITLY interpreted as sRGB, but nothing enforces it — on a wide-gamut panel in native mode, sRGB-intended content renders oversaturated because the panel's primaries are wider than sRGB. Windows 11 22H2+ Auto Color Management (ACM) changes this: DWM composites in FP16 (removing the historical 8-bit-per-channel windowed-app bottleneck), and unmanaged/legacy SDR apps are mapped consistently INTO sRGB and then correctly transformed to the display's actual gamut. So with ACM on, an unmanaged app gets accurate-but-sRGB-clamped output. The catch, well documented in the calibration community: ACM derives the display profile from EDID rather than a user-installed colorimeter-generated ICC profile, and it causes even color-aware apps to ignore applied ICC profiles unless the app is individually opted into 'legacy color management'. ACM is off by default on most hardware and cannot be assumed. To go beyond sRGB an app must opt into advanced color explicitly — an FP16 swap chain with DXGI_COLOR_SPACE_RGB_FULL_G10_NONE_P709 (scRGB), and it must scale SDR content by AdvancedColorInfo.SdrWhiteLevelInNits/80 because scRGB defines 1.0 = 80 nits.

- **Used by:** Microsoft DirectX Developer Blog, 'Advancing the State of Color Management in Windows' (devblogs.microsoft.com/directx/auto-color-management/); Microsoft Learn 'Use DirectX with Advanced Color on high/standard dynamic range displays'; DisplayCAL and PC Monitors forum threads documenting the ICC-profile-bypass behaviour; a Chromium bug (328856550) tracking wide-gamut images being clamped to sRGB under ACM.
- **Relevance:** Practical conclusion: Quill Art Mode should target sRGB output and NOT attempt display calibration or ICC round-tripping in v1. Document 'colors are sRGB' and be honest about it. The genuinely useful, low-cost win is an optional soft-proof toggle later. If Quill ever wants an HDR/wide-gamut mode, the FP16-linear working space chosen here is exactly the prerequisite — that decision is free now and expensive to retrofit.

### Why HSV/HSL sliders feel wrong to artists — and what Oklab fixes
HSV/HSL are trivial algebraic reparameterisations of the RGB cube (max/min of the channels). They have no relationship to human vision. Three concrete failures: (1) LIGHTNESS IS A LIE — at fixed S and L, sweeping H produces large perceived-brightness swings; HSL(60,70%,50%) yellow reads far brighter than HSL(240,70%,50%) blue. An artist building a value structure cannot trust the slider. (2) HUE IS NOT CONSTANT — holding H fixed and changing S or V visibly shifts hue, worst in the blues and purples, where increasing saturation pulls blue toward purple. (3) SATURATION IS NOT UNIFORM — equal S steps produce wildly unequal chroma steps across hues, because the RGB cube's distance to the gamut boundary varies enormously by hue. Oklab fixes this with a perceptually-derived pipeline: from linear sRGB, matrix M1 to an LMS-like cone space (rows [0.8189330101, 0.3618667424, -0.1288597137], [0.0329845436, 0.9293118715, 0.0361456387], [0.0482003018, 0.2643662691, 0.6338517070]), then a cube-root nonlinearity per channel, then matrix M2 to Lab (rows [0.2104542553, 0.7936177850, -0.0040720468], [1.9779984951, -2.4285922050, 0.4505937099], [0.0259040371, 0.7827717662, -0.8086757660]). Ottosson reports RMS errors of 0.20 for lightness (vs CIELAB's 1.70), 0.81 chroma (vs 1.84), 0.49 hue — i.e. it beats CIELAB substantially on all three and is competitive with IPT. Cylindrical form OKLCH = (L, C=sqrt(a^2+b^2), h=atan2(b,a)). Cost: ~2 matrix multiplies + 3 cube roots each way — trivially fast, fully differentiable except at C=0.

- **Used by:** Björn Ottosson, 'A perceptual color space for image processing' (bottosson.github.io/posts/oklab/); adopted into CSS Color 4 as oklab()/oklch(); used by Evil Martians' design system tooling, Photoshop's newer perceptual gradient options, and many modern palette tools.
- **Relevance:** Quill's Art Mode picker should be built on Oklab, not HSV. The concrete artist-visible payoff: a 'value ladder' or shade ramp generated by varying L alone actually reads as an even ladder; a hue wheel at fixed L reads as one consistent value; and the 'lighten/darken' modifier keys behave predictably. Keep an HSV readout for compatibility with tutorials and hex codes, but drive the UI from OKLCH.

### Okhsv/Okhsl — the actual picker geometry, because raw OKLCH has a ragged gamut
OKLCH is perceptually good but its sRGB-representable region is an irregular blob: for each hue there is a 'cusp' (the point of maximum chroma) at a different L, so a naive square L-vs-C picker is mostly out-of-gamut dead space, and dragging along an edge silently clips. Ottosson's Okhsl/Okhsv solve this by remapping the gamut into a clean cylinder. Shared prerequisite: a corrected lightness Lr with a 'toe' so that Lr better matches CIE L* and Lr(1)=1: Lr = (k3*L - k1 + sqrt((k3*L - k1)^2 + 4*k2*k3*L))/2 with k1=0.206, k2=0.03, k3=(1+k1)/(1+k2). OKHSV: (a) find the sRGB cusp for the hue via the gamut-clipping approximation (a fitted polynomial in the a/b direction that predicts max saturation, avoiding an iterative gamut search); (b) remap the two-segment gamut triangle onto a unit square so the cusp lands at s=1,v=1; (c) rescale v to remove residual curvature so the space is an exact cylinder; (d) tweak low-s scaling for uniformity. OKHSL instead defines three chroma curves — C0 (hue-independent, the perceptual low-chroma behaviour), Cmid (fitted mid-gamut), Cmax (the gamut boundary) — and interpolates so dC/ds = C0 at s=0, C = Cmid at s=0.8, C = Cmax at s=1.0. This confines gamut irregularity to the extreme edge and keeps the interior perceptually smooth. Guarantees: hue is perceptually orthogonal to the other two axes in both; geometry is a simple cylinder so a conventional wheel+square or wheel+triangle UI works; neither is fully perceptually uniform (that is mathematically impossible on a cylinder over a non-cylindrical gamut).

- **Used by:** Björn Ottosson, 'Two new color spaces for color picking — Okhsv and Okhsl' (bottosson.github.io/posts/colorpicker/) with reference C++/JS implementations in the public domain; the companion gamut-clipping post supplies the cusp approximation. Adopted by numerous web pickers and design tools.
- **Relevance:** This is the concrete, implementable answer for Quill's picker widget. Recommendation: Okhsl for the default wheel+square picker (its interior smoothness is what makes casual dragging feel good), with an OKLCH numeric readout for precision work, and Okhsv available for users who want Photoshop-like 'pure hue at top-right' behaviour. All three are ~100 lines of math with public-domain reference code.

### Gamut clipping — what to do when the picked/computed color isn't representable
Harmony generation, lighten/darken, and OKLCH numeric entry all readily produce colors outside sRGB. Naive per-channel clamp to [0,1] shifts hue badly (clipping the blue channel of a purple turns it magenta) and flattens gradients. The principled approach, from Ottosson's gamut-clipping work, is to project toward a hue-preserving anchor in Oklab: hold hue h fixed, then reduce chroma C along a line toward a target lightness L0, binary-searching (or using the fitted cusp approximation to jump directly) for the boundary. Choices for L0 give different character: L0 = L (preserve lightness exactly, chroma-only reduction — best for value-structure work); L0 = 0.5 (project toward mid-grey — smoother for very saturated extremes); L0 = the cusp lightness (maximum chroma retention). The cusp approximation makes this O(1) instead of an iterative search per pixel.

- **Used by:** bottosson.github.io/posts/gamutclipping/; CSS Color 4's gamut mapping algorithm uses a closely related OKLCH chroma-reduction-with-deltaE-check procedure.
- **Relevance:** Needed the moment the picker is OKLCH-based. Also needed for harmony rules (rotating hue by 180 degrees at high chroma frequently exits sRGB) and for any 'HDR-ish' brush that produces values above 1.0 in the FP16 buffer — those must be gamut-mapped, not clamped, at display time.

### Color harmony rules, and the RYB-vs-perceptual-wheel problem
The standard set is: complementary (h+180), split-complementary (h+150, h+210), triadic (h+120, h+240), tetradic/square (h+90,180,270), analogous (h±15 to ±45), and monochromatic (fix h and C, vary L). Implementation is a rotation in a hue coordinate — but WHICH hue coordinate decides whether the result matches artist expectation. The traditional artist's wheel is RYB (Itten): red's complement is green, blue's is orange, yellow's is purple. In RGB/HSV hue degrees, red(0) + 180 = cyan, not green; yellow(60) + 180 = blue, not purple. So a naive HSV complement contradicts every color-theory book an artist has read. Two fixes: (a) apply a nonlinear RYB→RGB hue warp before rotating (the standard approach is a cubic-interpolated lookup over the 8 RYB cube corners, per Gossett & Chen's 'Paint Inspired Color Mixing'); or (b) rotate in OKLCH hue, which is perceptually uniform but still a light-mixing wheel — its complements are cyan-ish, not green-ish. The honest resolution is to offer a wheel-model toggle. Independently, harmony sets should be generated with L and C held constant in Oklab so the family reads as a coherent value/saturation group — this is what makes generated palettes look designed rather than random, and it is precisely what HSV cannot do.

- **Used by:** Sessions College color calculator, Adobe Color, Figma's color wheel all expose these rule sets; Itten's color theory is the RYB source; Gossett & Chen (2004) is the standard RYB interpolation reference.
- **Relevance:** A low-cost, high-perceived-value feature: given the current foreground color, offer a strip of harmony swatches. Doing it in OKLCH with L/C held fixed is what makes it actually useful. Ship the RYB-wheel toggle — the mismatch with art-school training is a real complaint driver.

### Pigment mixing is a different problem from linear blending, and linear light does NOT solve it
Linear-light blending is correct for LIGHT superposition (two projectors, two glass sheets). Paint is subtractive: pigments absorb and scatter, and the mixture's reflectance is not a linear combination of the components' reflectances. This is why blue + yellow gives green with real paint but grey-ish with any RGB lerp, linear or not. Kubelka-Munk theory (Kubelka & Munk, 1931, 'Ein Beitrag zur Optik der Farbanstriche') models each pigment by absorption K and scattering S per wavelength; mixtures combine K and S linearly (weighted by concentration), and reflectance is recovered via R = 1 + K/S - sqrt((K/S)^2 + 2K/S). Mixbox (Secret Weapons, the EbSynth authors) makes this practical: RGB in, RGB out, by fitting a latent representation of four base pigments plus a residual, so the whole model is a small lookup + polynomial rather than a full spectral integration. spectral.js (Ronald van Wijnen) is an open JS implementation of the same idea.

- **Used by:** Mixbox ships in Rebelle 5 Pro as 'Rebelle Pigments'; Rebelle's watercolor/oil engines; Aseprite community extensions; scrtwpns.com/mixbox with a published SIGGRAPH-adjacent paper and reference implementations (note: Mixbox has a non-commercial/commercial licensing split — check before shipping).
- **Relevance:** A genuine differentiator, and orthogonal to the working-space decision. Suggested architecture: make the brush's color-combination function pluggable — 'Light' (standard linear source-over, the default and the fast path) vs 'Pigment' (Kubelka-Munk-style). Only the smudge/mixing brushes and a 'pigment mix' blend mode need the second path, so the cost is contained. Important: the pigment path expects sRGB-ish reflectance inputs, so it needs its own encode/decode around the linear buffer — do not feed it raw linear values.

### Eyedropper design: sample source, radius semantics, and the zoom-dependence bug
Three independent axes. (1) SOURCE: 'Current Layer' reads the active layer's own buffer; 'All Layers'/'Merged' reads the composited result. Photoshop additionally offers 'Current & Below' and 'no adjustments' variants. These give genuinely different answers whenever layer opacity, blend modes, or adjustment layers are involved — an artist picking from a 40%-opacity layer expects the merged appearance, not the layer's stored color. (2) RADIUS: point, 3x3, 5x5, 11x11, 31x31, 51x51, 101x101 average. Averaging is essential on textured/noisy work where a point sample lands on an outlier pixel. (3) The trap, explicitly documented in Photoshop guidance: Photoshop's sample size is defined in SCREEN pixels, so zooming changes the sampled color. This is widely regarded as a footgun. Correct implementation: define radius in DOCUMENT pixels so the result is zoom-invariant. Two further correctness requirements: the average must be computed in LINEAR light (averaging sRGB values reproduces the midpoint-darkening error, so a 51x51 average over a checkerboard reads too dark), and it must be alpha-weighted (sum premultiplied RGB, sum alpha, divide) so transparent pixels don't drag the result to black.

- **Used by:** Photoshop (sample size dropdown, Sample menu with Current Layer / Current & Below / All Layers / All Layers No Adjustments); Krita's color picker has an explicit 'sample radius' and 'sample all layers' pair plus a 'blend' percentage that mixes the picked color with the current one; Procreate's press-and-hold picker samples merged with a small radius.
- **Relevance:** Concrete spec for Quill: default to merged-composite sampling with a 1px point sample; expose radius in document pixels (1/3/5/9/17/33); read from the FP16 linear composite; alpha-weighted linear average; convert once to OKLCH for the picker UI. Add Krita's 'blend' slider — it is a small feature artists love. Pen-first bonus: barrel-button or long-press to pick, which suits Quill's stylus-first positioning.

### Palette storage: interchange formats and what to use internally
GPL (GIMP Palette) — plain text, human-editable/diffable: line 1 'GIMP Palette', then 'Name: x', 'Columns: n', a '#' comment line, then one entry per line as 'R G B<TAB>name' with 0-255 integers. sRGB only, 8-bit only, no groups. ASE (Adobe Swatch Exchange) — big-endian binary: magic 'ASEF', version as 2x int16 (1,0), block count int32; then blocks each with type int16 (0x0001 = color entry, 0xC001 = group start, 0xC002 = group end), length int32, a UTF-16BE null-terminated name preceded by its uint16 length, a 4-byte color model tag ('RGB ', 'CMYK', 'LAB ', 'Gray'), the components as FLOAT32 (3 for RGB/LAB, 4 for CMYK, 1 for Gray), and a color type int16 (0=global, 1=spot, 2=normal). Note ASE is float and supports LAB — the only common interchange format that can carry out-of-8-bit precision and a device-independent space. It has no formal public spec; implementations are reverse-engineered. ACO (Adobe Color) — binary, uint16 components, supports RGB/HSB/CMYK/Lab/Grayscale, versions 1 and 2 (v2 adds names). ACT is a bare 768-byte RGB table. GIMP added .ase support in 2.10.36 (Nov 2023).

- **Used by:** selapa.net/swatches/colors/fileformats.php is the de facto community spec reference; behreajj/AsepriteSwatchExchange and dagronf/ColorPaletteCodable are working implementations to check behaviour against; GIMP developer standards page for GPL.
- **Relevance:** Internally, store palettes as OKLCH floats plus the source hex, in Quill's own JSON — never as 8-bit sRGB triples, or every palette silently round-trips through quantization and you lose the ability to re-derive ramps. For interchange, import GPL + ASE + ACO (all easy readers) and export GPL (trivial) and ASE (write RGB float blocks, big-endian). ASE's float encoding is the right export target precisely because it doesn't quantize.

### The text/UI-antialiasing exception to 'always blend linear'
Font outlines and hinting were designed against gamma-INCORRECT rasterizers. Switching glyph compositing to physically-correct linear blending makes text look noticeably thinner and lighter, because a 50% coverage pixel that used to emit 128/255 (perceptually ~22% luminance, i.e. too dark, i.e. accidentally 'bolder') now correctly emits 188/255. The industry does not use gamma 1.0 here: Photoshop antialiases text at an empirical gamma around 1.42 as a compromise; Adobe's and macOS's font engines apply 'stem darkening' (slightly emboldening glyphs at small sizes) to compensate. Direct2D's own behaviour is relevant too: setting any alpha mode other than D2D1_ALPHA_MODE_IGNORE automatically downgrades text AA from ClearType to grayscale, and ClearType onto a transparent surface gives unpredictable results.

- **Used by:** FreeType's 'text rendering general' doc on stem darkening; whynothugo.nl on gamma correction and font blending in linear space; Direct2D's 'ClearType and Alpha Modes' section; Unity forum threads on UI font rendering degrading under Linear color space.
- **Relevance:** Do not composite Quill's UI chrome or on-canvas text annotations through the Art Mode linear path expecting it to look better — it will look thinner. Keep the WinUI/XAML UI layer entirely outside the linear working space (it renders to the normal sRGB swapchain), and if Art Mode gains a text tool, rasterize glyph coverage separately and consider a gamma ~1.4 compromise on the coverage mask rather than pure linear.

### Recommendations
- PRIMARY RECOMMENDATION — layer and composite buffers: DirectXPixelFormat.R16G16B16A16Float, CanvasAlphaMode.Premultiplied, with the documented invariant that stored values are LINEAR LIGHT with sRGB/Rec.709 primaries and D65 white — i.e. scRGB / Win2D's CanvasColorSpace.ScRgb. Use the 5-arg CanvasRenderTarget(ICanvasResourceCreator, w, h, dpi, DirectXPixelFormat, CanvasAlphaMode) overload; Quill's existing InkSurface.cs uses the 4-arg overload and silently gets B8G8R8A8UNorm/sRGB. Gate on CanvasDevice.IsPixelFormatSupported(DirectXPixelFormat.R16G16B16A16Float) at startup.
- TRADEOFFS, PLAINLY. Cost of FP16-linear: (1) 2x memory — 8 bytes/px, so a 4096x4096 layer is 128 MB vs 64 MB, and a 10-layer document is 1.3 GB if stored monolithically. (2) 2x GPU bandwidth on every composite, which on integrated graphics at high canvas resolution is a real framerate cost. (3) FP16 render targets are near-universal on modern hardware but not guaranteed on very old feature-level-9 GPUs. (4) Half floats compress poorly with generic LZ compressors. Benefits: correct soft-brush edges and gradients with no dark fringing; no accumulation stalling on low-flow buildup; no shadow posterization; headroom above 1.0 for glow/dodge and a free path to HDR later. For a painting app the benefits are load-bearing, not cosmetic — this is the right trade.
- MITIGATE THE MEMORY COST WITH TILING, not with a lower bit depth. Store each layer as a sparse grid of 256x256 FP16 tiles, allocating only touched tiles. Blank canvas costs nothing; typical artwork touches a fraction of the full extent. This is the standard approach (Krita, Photoshop, MyPaint all tile) and it is what makes FP16 affordable. It also gives you free dirty-region tracking for undo and for incremental compositing.
- EXPLICITLY REJECT the two tempting cheaper options, and record why. (a) B8G8R8A8_UNORM_SRGB gives linear blending for free in the D3D ROP at 4 bytes/px — but it still STORES 8 bits, so low-flow brush buildup stalls and bands (see the accumulation-error finding). Good enough for Notes mode, not for painting. (b) R16G16B16A16_UNORM is the same 8 bytes/px as FP16 with uniform rather than relative precision and no above-1.0 headroom — strictly worse for the same cost. FP16 wins.
- SET EFFECT PRECISION EXPLICITLY. Call the Win2D/D2D equivalent of ID2D1DeviceContext::SetRenderingControls with bufferPrecision = D2D1_BUFFER_PRECISION_16BPC_FLOAT for the Art Mode device context, guarded by IsBufferPrecisionSupported. Without this, effect-graph intermediates are inferred from input formats and can silently drop to 8-bit UNORM, quantizing (and clamping) mid-graph. Where you deliberately WANT clamping, force it with a pass-through ColorMatrix effect with ClampOutput=TRUE — on Windows 10+ shader linking makes implicit clamping unreliable.
- SET D2D1_GAMMA_1_0 ON EVERY GRADIENT BRUSH IN ART MODE. Direct2D's gradient stop interpolation defaults to 2.2 (gamma-space) — this is one of only two places D2D is color-space aware, and it will silently produce the dark-band artifact in the gradient tool even though the render target is linear.
- PICKER: build on Oklab. Ship Okhsl as the default wheel+square geometry (its interior smoothness makes dragging feel right), expose OKLCH numeric fields for precision work, offer Okhsv as an alternative for Photoshop-trained users, and keep an HSV/hex readout purely for compatibility with tutorials and web colors. Ottosson's reference implementations are public domain, roughly 100 lines each — use them rather than reimplementing. Include the Lr toe function (k1=0.206, k2=0.03, k3=(1+k1)/(1+k2)); it is what makes the lightness axis actually behave.
- GAMUT-MAP, DON'T CLAMP. Implement hue-preserving chroma reduction in Oklab using the fitted cusp approximation, with a selectable anchor (L0 = L to preserve lightness — the right default for painting). Apply it at three points: OKLCH numeric entry, harmony generation, and display-time conversion of any FP16 values that exceed sRGB.
- EYEDROPPER SPEC: sample from the merged FP16 linear composite by default (with a Current-Layer option); define sample radius in DOCUMENT pixels, not screen pixels, so results are zoom-invariant — Photoshop's screen-pixel behaviour is a documented footgun, don't copy it; average in linear light and alpha-weighted (sum premultiplied RGB and alpha separately, then unpremultiply); offer radii 1/3/5/9/17/33; add Krita's 'blend with current color' percentage slider. Bind it to barrel button / long-press for Quill's stylus-first positioning.
- PALETTES: store internally as OKLCH floats plus the originating hex in Quill's own JSON — never as 8-bit sRGB triples, or you lose the ability to regenerate ramps without accumulating quantization. Import GPL, ASE and ACO; export GPL (trivial text) and ASE (big-endian, float32 RGB blocks) — ASE is the only common interchange format that carries float precision and can express LAB.
- HARMONY: generate complementary / split-complementary / triadic / tetradic / analogous / monochromatic by rotating hue in OKLCH while HOLDING L AND C FIXED — that constancy is what makes generated sets look designed. Ship a wheel-model toggle between the perceptual (OKLCH) wheel and an RYB-warped artist's wheel, because 'red's complement is green' is what art training says and a naive perceptual complement gives cyan.
- OUTPUT/EXPORT: convert to sRGB using the exact piecewise transfer function (not pow(x, 1/2.2)) at the last step only. Default PNG export to 8-bit sRGB; offer 16-bit PNG for users who will re-edit. For the native .quill Art document, persist raw FP16 tiles — and byte-split the halves (all high bytes, then all low bytes) before running zstd/LZ4, which materially improves the compression ratio on float data.
- DISPLAY COLOR MANAGEMENT: target sRGB output and say so in the UI. Do not attempt ICC round-tripping or display calibration in v1 — under Windows 11 Auto Color Management, applied ICC profiles are bypassed in favour of EDID-derived ones anyway unless the app is individually opted into legacy color management, so the effort largely does not pay off. The FP16-linear working space chosen above is the entire prerequisite for a future HDR/wide-gamut mode (FP16 scRGB swap chain, DXGI_COLOR_SPACE_RGB_FULL_G10_NONE_P709, SDR content scaled by SdrWhiteLevelInNits/80), so that door stays open at zero present cost.
- MAKE THE COLOR-COMBINATION FUNCTION PLUGGABLE so a Kubelka-Munk / Mixbox-style pigment mode can be added to smudge and mixing brushes without touching the compositor. Default to 'Light' (standard linear source-over, fast path). Check Mixbox's licensing before shipping commercially; spectral.js is a more permissive reference for the same theory.
- KEEP THE WINUI/XAML UI LAYER OUT OF THE LINEAR PATH. It renders to the normal sRGB swapchain. If Art Mode gains a text tool, rasterize glyph coverage separately and consider a gamma ~1.4 compromise on the coverage mask (Photoshop's approach) rather than pure linear — physically-correct linear blending makes text look thin because fonts were hinted against gamma-incorrect rasterizers.

### Pitfalls
- PREMULTIPLYING BEFORE LINEARIZING. srgb(C)*A != srgb(C*A). Storing premultiplied sRGB-encoded values and later unpremultiplying (divide by A) amplifies the encoding error catastrophically at low alpha — this is the classic source of dark halos on soft-edged composited images. Order is always: decode → premultiply → blend → encode.
- GAMMA-ENCODING THE ALPHA CHANNEL. Alpha is coverage/geometry, never light. It stays linear always. Applying a transfer function to it is a surprisingly common bug in hand-rolled converters.
- USING pow(x, 2.2) INSTEAD OF THE PIECEWISE sRGB CURVE. Error reaches roughly 1.5/255 near black, which is exactly where a painting app's shadow gradients live. Use the piecewise form with the 0.0031308/0.04045 breakpoints, or an accurate LUT.
- ASSUMING 8-BIT IS FINE BECAUSE A SINGLE BLEND LOOKS RIGHT. The failure mode in a paint app is accumulation across 50-500 low-alpha dabs per stroke, not one composite. A soft airbrush at 2% flow will visibly stall and stair-step in 8-bit while looking perfect in an A/B test of a single stamp. Test with low-flow buildup, not with hard-edged shapes.
- STORING LINEAR VALUES IN 8 BITS. This is strictly worse than sRGB-encoded 8-bit — posterized shadows, immediately and obviously. If you cannot afford 16 bits, keep the sRGB encoding and accept gamma-space blending; do not split the difference.
- ASSUMING DIRECT2D KNOWS ABOUT COLOR SPACES. It does not — it blends whatever numbers are in the target. The pixel format is the entire mechanism. The only two places D2D is color-space aware are gradient stop interpolation (D2D1_GAMMA) and the D2D1ColorManagement effect. Everything else is on you.
- LETTING EFFECT-GRAPH INTERMEDIATES SILENTLY QUANTIZE. Direct2D infers intermediate buffer precision from input formats unless you set it. Worse, on Windows 10+ shader linking means intermediates may or may not exist depending on graph topology, so clamping behaviour differs between machines and Windows versions. Set precision explicitly and force clamps where you need determinism.
- EXPECTING CLAMPING TO [0,1] TO HAPPEN. It happens at UNORM precision and not at FLOAT precision, and shader linking can eliminate the intermediate that would have clamped. Many built-in effects (ColorMatrix, ArithmeticComposite, Convolve, Lighting, Exposure, Composite in Plus mode, Saturation, Sepia, cubic-interpolation transforms) emit out-of-range values in UNPREMULTIPLIED space even from in-range inputs — and the range must be checked in unpremultiplied space, not premultiplied.
- AVERAGING PIXELS WITHOUT ALPHA WEIGHTING. Transparent pixels have premultiplied RGB of zero, so any unweighted average (eyedropper radius, blur, thumbnail downscale, mipmaps) drags results toward black at partially-transparent edges. Sum premultiplied RGB and alpha independently, then unpremultiply.
- EYEDROPPER SAMPLE RADIUS IN SCREEN PIXELS. Photoshop does this and it means the sampled color changes when you zoom. It looks harmless and users find it maddening. Define radius in document pixels.
- SWITCHING GLYPH AND UI COMPOSITING TO LINEAR AND CALLING IT A FIX. Text will look thinner and lighter, and users will report it as a regression, because fonts were hinted against gamma-incorrect rasterizers. Also note Direct2D silently downgrades ClearType to grayscale AA whenever the alpha mode is not IGNORE.
- EXPECTING LINEAR BLENDING TO MAKE BLUE + YELLOW = GREEN. It will not, and it should not — linear blending is correct for light superposition, not pigment. That is a separate subtractive/Kubelka-Munk problem. Conflating the two leads to 'fixing' the working space and then being disappointed that mixing still doesn't feel like paint.
- ASSUMING HSV HUE ROTATION GIVES ARTIST-EXPECTED COMPLEMENTS. In HSV, red+180 is cyan and yellow+180 is blue — contradicting every color-theory text, which says green and purple. Either warp through RYB or clearly label the wheel model.
- BUILDING A NAIVE OKLCH SQUARE PICKER. Most of an L-vs-C rectangle is outside sRGB for any given hue, and the in-gamut region's shape changes as you move the hue slider. Users will drag into dead space and get silent clipping. Use Okhsl/Okhsv, which remap the gamut into a proper cylinder.
- PER-CHANNEL CLAMPING FOR OUT-OF-GAMUT COLORS. It shifts hue visibly — clipping a purple's blue channel turns it magenta. Use hue-preserving chroma reduction in Oklab.
- ASSUMING A CALIBRATED MONITOR MEANS CALIBRATED OUTPUT ON WINDOWS 11. Auto Color Management bypasses user-installed ICC profiles in favour of EDID-derived ones and causes even color-aware apps to ignore applied profiles unless individually opted into legacy color management. Do not build features that assume the user's ICC profile is being honored.
- QUANTIZING PALETTES TO 8-BIT sRGB INTERNALLY. Every save/load round-trip loses precision and you lose the ability to regenerate perceptual ramps from stored swatches. Store float OKLCH; quantize only at export.

## natural-media

### Curtis et al. three-layer watercolor model (the canonical algorithm)
Three coupled 2D grids over the paper: (1) a shallow-water layer holding velocity u,v on a staggered MAC grid, pressure p, and a binary wet-area mask M; (2) a pigment-deposition layer holding, per pigment k, g_k = pigment suspended in the water and d_k = pigment deposited on the paper; (3) a capillary layer holding water saturation s and per-cell capacity c derived from paper fibre. One tick = MoveWater -> MovePigment -> TransferPigment -> SimulateCapillaryFlow.

MoveWater has four sub-steps. (a) UpdateVelocities: first bias by paper slope, u -= dh/dx, v -= dh/dy (h = paper height field), then an explicit Euler step of the 2D Navier-Stokes momentum equation, which the WPI/Curtis discretisation writes as u_{i+.5,j} += dt*(A - mu*B + p_i - p_{i+1} - kappa*u_{i+.5,j}), where A is the advective term (u^2_{ij} - u^2_{i+1,j} + u_{i+.5,j-x}v_{i+.5,j-x} - u_{i+.5,j+x}v_{i+.5,j+x}), B is the 5-point Laplacian stencil (u_{i+1.5,j}+u_{i-.5,j}+u_{i+.5,j+x}+u_{i+.5,j-x}-4u_{i+.5,j}), mu is viscosity and kappa is drag. (b) RelaxDivergence: Jacobi/SOR iterations, delta = -xi*(div u); p += delta; u_{i+.5,j} += delta; u_{i-.5,j} -= delta; repeat until max|delta| < tau (typically 10-50 iterations). (c) FlowOutward: p -= eta * (1 - GaussianBlur(M, large sigma)). (d) Enforce u=0 on every face outside M so water cannot leave the wet region.

MovePigment advects g_k by explicit upwind flux (each cell gives max(0,u)*g to the downstream neighbour), which conserves pigment mass exactly.

Critically, step (c) FlowOutward is what produces edge darkening — it is not emergent from the fluid solve. Blurring the wet mask and subtracting it from pressure creates a pressure gradient pointing at the perimeter of the wash, so the velocity field continuously carries pigment from the centre to the rim, where TransferPigment then deposits it. This is the mechanism behind the classic watercolor dark rim.

- **Used by:** Curtis, Anderson, Seims, Fleischer & Salesin, 'Computer-Generated Watercolor', SIGGRAPH 1997 (https://grail.cs.washington.edu/projects/watercolor/), patented as US6198489B1. Reimplemented in the WPI course notes with the explicit discretisation (https://davis.wpi.edu/~matt/courses/watercolor/fluid.html) and in andrewdotwang's GPU reimplementation (https://andrewdotwang.github.io/WatercolorRendering/). It is the direct ancestor of Rebelle's wet engine and of every 'real watercolor' claim in commercial paint software.
- **Relevance:** This is the reference algorithm Quill should implement if it wants a true wet engine. Every step is a gather-only stencil over a fixed grid, so it maps exactly onto Win2D PixelShaderEffect ping-pong render targets — no compute shader needed. The expensive part is RelaxDivergence (10-50 full-screen passes). The FlowOutward blur can be delegated to Win2D's built-in hardware GaussianBlurEffect for free. Budget roughly 20-50 full-screen passes per sim tick.

### Capillary layer: backruns, blooms and the wet-mask expansion rule
SimulateCapillaryFlow runs on the saturation field s and capacity field c (c is sampled from the paper height/fibre texture, so rougher paper holds more water). Three phases per tick: (1) absorption — for every cell where M=1, s += max(0, min(alpha, c - s)), draining the shallow layer into the paper; (2) diffusion — for each cell i with s_i > epsilon, for each 4-neighbour j with s_i > s_j and s_j > delta, transfer ds = max(0, min(s_i - s_j, c_j - s_j)/4), s_i -= ds, s_j += ds. The s_j > delta condition is what makes the spread ragged and fibre-like rather than a smooth diffusion — water only wicks into paper that is already slightly damp; (3) re-wetting — for every cell, if s_i > sigma then set M_i = 1.

Phase (3) is the entire backrun/bloom mechanism. Expanding M lets the shallow-water solver operate in territory it previously could not reach; water there rushes outward, re-mobilises already-deposited pigment d_k back into suspension g_k, and carries it to a new perimeter where FlowOutward deposits it as a hard cauliflower edge. Drop clean water into a drying wash and you get exactly the real-world backrun.

Drybrush is the complementary case: where s is low and paper height h is high, the wet mask never covers the peaks, so pigment only lands in the valleys and you get broken, grainy coverage.

- **Used by:** Curtis et al. 1997 (all four functions MoveWater/MovePigment/TransferPigment/SimulateCapillaryFlow). Rebelle exposes this layer directly as user controls: 'Absorbency' (0-10, how fast paper drinks the wash), 'Diffusion Speed' (1-10), and 'Re-wet' (1-10, 'if set high, the new paint rewets the paint below faster and creates strong watercolor edges') — see https://escapemotions.com/products/rebelle/manual/7/starting-painting/water/
- **Relevance:** Backruns are the single effect users point to as proof a watercolor engine is 'real'. They cost almost nothing extra (2-3 more pixel shader passes on a single-channel texture) but only exist if Quill implements the wet-mask expansion rule and the de-deposition path in TransferPigment. Implementations that skip the capillary layer produce washes that look plausible but flat and lifeless.

### TransferPigment: deposition/lifting, granulation, staining
Two-way exchange between suspended pigment g_k and deposited pigment d_k, per pigment per cell per tick:
  delta_down = g_k * (1 - h*gamma_k) * rho_k
  delta_up   = d_k * (1 + (h-1)*gamma_k) * rho_k / omega_k
  d_k += delta_down - delta_up;  g_k += delta_up - delta_down
with clamping so neither goes negative and d_k stays within capacity. h is the normalised paper height at that cell; gamma_k is the pigment's granulation coefficient; rho_k is density (settling rate); omega_k is staining power (resistance to being lifted back off).

The h*gamma coupling is the whole trick: a high-granulation pigment (gamma near 1) deposits preferentially where h is low — the valleys of the paper — and lifts preferentially from the peaks. Over a wash this produces the mottled, speckled texture of real granulating pigments like ultramarine or cerulean. Set gamma=0 and you get a perfectly flat staining pigment like phthalo blue. Set omega high and the pigment refuses to lift, so backruns pass over it without disturbing it; set omega low and it re-mobilises easily.

This means the *identity* of a pigment in a physically-based engine is not a colour — it is the tuple (K spectrum, S spectrum, gamma, rho, omega). Real paint ranges (granulating earth pigments vs staining modern organics) fall right out of it.

- **Used by:** Curtis et al. 1997. Rebelle ships named real pigments (cadmium red, phthalo blue, titanium white...) with distinct granulation behaviour — 'Rebelle is the first software with real physical color mixing based on traditional pigments in a full RGB color gamut' (https://escapemotions.com/products/rebelle/manual/7/starting-painting/pigments/). Krita's Bristle engine exposes the depletion half of the same idea.
- **Relevance:** One pixel-shader pass reading (g, d, paper h) and writing (g, d). Note the Win2D constraint: a PixelShaderEffect writes one RGBA output, so updating both g and d requires two passes over the same inputs, or packing g and d into one RGBA (2 pigments' worth) and running a pass per pigment pair. This is the sizing constraint on 'how many pigments can Quill support' — 4 pigments = 2 RGBA textures = 2 passes.

### Procedural polygon-splat watercolor (the cheap alternative that actually ships)
Instead of a grid fluid solve, represent each brush dab as a small set of polygons ('splats') whose vertices are individually animated, plus one coarse scalar 'wet map' grid. A stamp emits N splats (typically 3-10) at jittered positions. Each splat vertex has a velocity driven by: an outward radial term scaled by local wetness, a global gravity vector (canvas tilt), per-vertex noise/roughness (default ~1px, this creates the crenellated wet edge), and the brush's flow parameter. Splats live in a three-state lifecycle — Flowing (vertices moving), Fixed/committed (movement halted but re-activatable), Dried (frozen, retired from the simulation, baked into the canvas raster).

The wet map is a coarse 2D grid where the brush rasterises a constant value representing 'remaining time this cell stays wet'; it decrements each tick. When a Fixed splat's vertex is found inside a newly-wetted cell, its velocity is restored and it starts flowing again — this is the cheap analogue of re-wetting/backruns. Different brush presets (simple, wet-on-dry, wet-on-wet, blobby, crunchy) are just different (splat count, roughness, flow %, lifetime) tuples.

Rendering: splats are drawn as translucent polygons and composited multiplicatively/subtractively; overlapping splat edges naturally accumulate to darker rims, giving edge darkening for free without any pressure solve. Because splats are vector geometry, the result is resolution-independent — the same simulation state renders at screen resolution or print resolution.

- **Used by:** DiVerdi, Krishnaswamy, Mech & Ito, 'Painting with Polygons: A Procedural Watercolor Engine', IEEE TVCG 19(5), 2013 (Adobe) — https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1109/TVCG.2012.295. Shipped in Adobe Photoshop Eazel and is the lineage behind Adobe Fresco's watercolor Live Brush ('custom algorithms for dynamic simulation, including polygon-based watercolor rendering and tile-based processing for efficiency', Adobe Research, https://research.adobe.com/news/fresco-the-future-of-painting-born-of-a-union-of-art-and-science/). Reimplemented as Krita's procedural watercolor GSoC engine (https://phabricator.kde.org/T6224).
- **Relevance:** This is almost certainly the right default engine for Quill. The paper's explicit motivation was that grid simulations 'required powerful compute hardware and were limited to screen resolutions', while artists want print resolution on laptops and slates — which is precisely Quill's situation (WinUI 3, no compute shaders, unknown GPU). It needs zero custom shaders: Win2D CanvasPathBuilder + FillGeometry with the right blend mode renders splats directly. Cost scales with active splat count, not canvas area, so it is free when the user isn't painting. Recommend this as Tier A and the Curtis grid as an opt-in Tier B.

### Why RGB mixing is wrong, and what each 'fix' actually fixes
There are three distinct defects that get conflated, and they need three different fixes.

(1) Gamma error. Lerping 8-bit sRGB values averages gamma-encoded numbers, which darkens the midpoint of every blend and every antialiased edge. Fix: decode to linear light, lerp, re-encode. Cheap and unambiguously correct for anything that is a *light* operation (blur, downsample, alpha coverage, additive glow).

(2) Perceptual non-uniformity. Even in linear light, a lerp between two saturated hues passes through a desaturated, perceptually lopsided middle. Fix: interpolate in Oklab or OkLCH, whose L axis is perceptually uniform. Good for UI gradients and colour ramps.

(3) Wrong physics. Paint is subtractive: pigment particles absorb and scatter light, and a mixture's reflectance is governed by the combined absorption/scattering, not by an average of the two reflectances. This is why blue + yellow is green on paper and grey on screen. NEITHER (1) nor (2) fixes this. Linear-light blue+yellow is still grey (brighter grey). Oklab blue+yellow is still an unsaturated near-neutral (it takes the short hue path through a desaturated region, not through green). Only a spectral or Kubelka-Munk model produces green, because green is what you get when a blue pigment's absorption of long wavelengths is combined multiplicatively with a yellow pigment's absorption of short wavelengths, leaving only the middle of the spectrum reflected.

- **Used by:** The gamma issue: NVIDIA GPU Gems 3 ch.24 'The Importance of Being Linear' (https://developer.nvidia.com/gpugems/gpugems3/part-iv-image-effects/chapter-24-importance-being-linear); Bottosson, 'How software gets color wrong' (https://bottosson.github.io/posts/colorwrong/). Perceptual: Oklab (https://bottosson.github.io/posts/oklab/), Levien's critique (https://raphlinus.github.io/color/2021/01/18/oklab-critique.html). Physics: Sochorova & Jamriska, ACM TOG 40(6) 2021, whose abstract states the problem exactly — 'the colors do not mix like actual paints. E.g., blue and yellow make gray instead of green' (https://dcgi.fel.cvut.cz/en/publications/2021/sochorova-tog-pigments/).
- **Relevance:** Quill must do (1) unconditionally — all sim buffers and compositing in linear light, and explicitly defeat Direct2D's implicit colour-space conversions on non-colour data buffers. Quill should do (3) for the paint mixing path. Quill should NOT reach for (2) as a substitute for (3), which is a very common and very visible mistake.

### Mixbox / Sochorova-Jamriska latent pigment space
An RGB-in/RGB-out black box that internally does Kubelka-Munk. RGB is decomposed into a latent representation consisting of concentrations of four canonical primary pigments — phthalo blue, quinacridone magenta, Hansa yellow, titanium white — plus a 3-channel additive residual that captures whatever those four pigments cannot reach in the sRGB gamut. Latent is therefore 7 numbers. The RGB->latent decomposition is precomputed and shipped as a lookup table (mixbox_lut.png). Mixing is a plain linear interpolation of the latent vectors (this is the key practical property: it is associative, order-independent, and works for N-way mixes and for filtering/blurring). latent->RGB evaluates the K-M mixture and adds the residual back. Because mixing is linear in latent space, you can advect, blur, or alpha-blend latent buffers with ordinary hardware blending and still get correct pigment behaviour.

The fundamental limitation is baked into the primaries: the four-pigment basis is essentially a CMYK gamut, so some sRGB colours are not reachable by any pigment mixture. The residual term is the escape hatch, and it is the part that does not mix physically — heavily-residual colours (electric cyan, pure display blue) degrade back toward RGB-like behaviour.

- **Used by:** Sochorova & Jamriska, 'Practical Pigment Mixing for Digital Painting', SIGGRAPH Asia 2021 / ACM TOG 40(6). Reference implementation at https://github.com/scrtwpns/mixbox with bindings for C/C++, C#, Java, JS, Python, Rust, Unity, Godot, and GLSL/HLSL/Metal shaders. Shipping in Rebelle 5+ Pro as the 'Rebelle Pigments' feature, and in the Flip Fluids Blender addon.
- **Relevance:** There is a C# binding and an HLSL shader version, so it drops straight into a Win2D/ComputeSharp.D2D1 pipeline. LICENSING IS THE BLOCKER: Mixbox is CC BY-NC 4.0, i.e. non-commercial only; shipping it in Quill requires a paid commercial licence from mixbox@scrtwpns.com. Decide this early, because the architecture implication (canvas stored in 7-channel latent rather than RGBA) is not something to retrofit.

### Spectral upsampling + weighted geometric mean (the free alternative to Mixbox)
Convert each RGB colour to a reflectance curve R(lambda) sampled at 36 bands (380-730nm at 10nm), mix the curves with a weighted geometric mean, convert back.

Upsampling: Burns solves for the *smoothest* reflectance curve that reproduces the target XYZ under D65, i.e. minimise sum of squared second differences of R subject to the CIE integration constraint and 0 < R < 1 — a small constrained least-squares problem. For real-time use he then provides a 'fast' variant: three precomputed basis reflectance curves R_r, R_g, R_b for the sRGB primaries, so R(lambda) ~= r*R_r(lambda) + g*R_g(lambda) + b*R_b(lambda) — three multiply-adds per band, no solve at runtime.

Mixing: R_mix(lambda) = product over i of R_i(lambda)^{w_i}, with sum w_i = 1. Equivalently, arithmetic mean in log-reflectance space, which is why it behaves like subtractive absorption. MyPaint's implementation writes it directly as color_1[i]^ratio * color_2[i]^(1-ratio) per band, with ratios derived from normalised alpha.

Downsampling: integrate R_mix against the CIE 1931 observer and D65 to get XYZ, then XYZ->linear sRGB.

WGM is not full Kubelka-Munk (it has no separate scattering term, so it cannot model a translucent glaze over a dark substrate) but it captures the essential subtractive behaviour and is far cheaper and more numerically robust.

- **Used by:** Scott Allen Burns, 'Subtractive Color Mixture Computation', arXiv:1710.06364 (http://scottburns.us/subtractive-color-mixture/) and 'Fast RGB to Spectrum Conversion for Reflectances'. Shipped in MyPaint, which documents 'a modified Meng function' for upsampling and the WGM formula, using lookup tables for the RGB->spectral step (https://github.com/mypaint/mypaint/blob/master/doc/spectral/spectral.md). MyPaint notes it works for sRGB/Rec.709 and is hard to extend to Rec.2020 because of spectral primary limitations.
- **Relevance:** This is the licence-clean path: published method, open implementations, no NC restriction. Cost is the problem — 36 floats per colour cannot live in a pixel-shader-friendly texture. Two viable reductions for Quill: (a) reduce to 8-12 bands, which is what IMPaSTo did for interactive rates and is visually adequate; (b) do the WGM in C# on the CPU only for the brush colour / palette mixing (a handful of colours per stroke), and let the canvas stay in a small pigment-concentration space. Do not attempt 36-band spectral per-pixel over the whole canvas in Win2D.

### Kubelka-Munk two-flux rendering of pigment layers
Per wavelength (or per RGB channel as a crude approximation), a pigment layer of thickness x with absorption K and scattering S has:
  a = 1 + K/S
  b = sqrt(a^2 - 1)
  c = a*sinh(b*S*x) + b*cosh(b*S*x)
  R = sinh(b*S*x) / c        (reflectance)
  T = b / c                  (transmittance)
Equivalently R = 1 / (a + b*coth(b*S*x)). For an infinitely thick layer this collapses to the familiar R_inf = 1 + K/S - sqrt((K/S)^2 + 2K/S).

Stacked glazes composite optically, not alpha-blend:
  R = R1 + T1^2 * R2 / (1 - R1*R2)
  T = T1 * T2 / (1 - R1*R2)
applied bottom-up. The 1/(1-R1*R2) term is inter-reflection between layers — it is why real glazing is luminous rather than muddy, and it is the thing alpha compositing cannot reproduce.

Authoring K and S: rather than asking artists for physical coefficients, Curtis has the user pick the appearance of a unit thickness of the pigment over white and over black, giving R_w and R_b per channel with the constraint 0 < R_b < R_w < 1, then inverts:
  a = 0.5 * (R_w + (R_b - R_w + 1)/R_b)
  b = sqrt(a^2 - 1)
  S = (1/b) * arccoth((a^2 - a*R_w - 1) / (b*(1 - R_w)))
  K = S * (a - 1)

- **Used by:** Curtis et al. 1997 for watercolor glazes; Baxter et al., 'IMPaSTo: A Realistic, Interactive Model for Paint', NPAR 2004 (http://gamma.cs.unc.edu/IMPASTO/publications/Baxter-IMPaSTo_Web-NPAR04.pdf) which used a small number of spectral samples plus a basis/lookup texture to make K-M interactive on 2004 GPUs; the theory itself, Kubelka & Munk 1931 (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kubelka%E2%80%93Munk_theory). WPI's course writeup gives the layer compositing formulas verbatim (https://davis.wpi.edu/~matt/courses/watercolor/rendering.html).
- **Relevance:** Doing full K-M per-pixel per-layer is the expensive path — sinh/cosh/coth per channel per layer. Two mitigations: bake R and T for each pigment at a quantised set of thicknesses into a 1D or 2D lookup texture (thickness x pigment), turning the per-pixel work into two texture fetches and the compositing arithmetic; or accept RGB-channel K-M (3 samples) rather than spectral, which is what Curtis did and which is visually acceptable for watercolor. Note the inversion is numerically unstable as R_w -> 1 or R_b -> 0; clamp inputs to roughly [0.005, 0.995].

### Impasto: height field + normal reconstruction + relighting
Maintain a scalar paint-thickness height field h(x,y) co-located with the colour. Every dab adds volume to h, shaped by the brush footprint and modulated by remaining paint load; the brush also *displaces* existing paint sideways (a bow wave ahead of the stroke and ridges at the edges), which is what makes a thick stroke read as thick rather than embossed.

Rendering is trivially cheap: reconstruct the surface normal from the height gradient by central differences, n = normalize(vec3(-(h(x+1,y)-h(x-1,y))*sx, -(h(x,y+1)-h(x,y-1))*sy, 1)), then shade with a fixed key light using Blinn-Phong or a GGX microfacet lobe with a low roughness for the wet-paint sheen. Modern implementations use GGX and let the light direction be user-draggable, which is a strong 'this is real paint' demo. The canvas weave is folded into the same height field as a static base, so bare canvas catches light too.

Paint displacement/transport uses a *conservative* advection scheme rather than semi-Lagrangian: each cell hands a fraction of its volume to neighbours based on the brush velocity, and volume is never created or destroyed. Conservation matters more than stability here because non-conservation manifests as paint mysteriously thinning or piling up over a long stroke.

- **Used by:** Baxter, Wendt & Lin, IMPaSTo (NPAR 2004) — grid-based paint flow with conservative advection plus pigment-based colour instead of RGB; and Baxter et al., 'DAB: Interactive Haptic Painting with 3D Virtual Brushes', SIGGRAPH 2001 (http://gamma.cs.unc.edu/DAB/files/DAB.pdf). Shipped: Rebelle's Impasto engine uses 'a height map of the painting's structure'; Corel Painter's thick-paint/Fluid Paint engine; ArtRage's oil paint; Adobe Fresco's oil Live Brush ('the oil paint looks thick and creamy'); Procreate's brush 'wet mix' + light source.
- **Relevance:** This is the highest visual-payoff-per-unit-cost item in the entire dimension, and Quill should implement it first. Relighting is ONE Win2D PixelShaderEffect pass over one extra texture — no fluid solve, no iteration, no multi-pass. Store h in R16Float (8-bit terraces visibly in the specular highlight). Even without any paint transport, just accumulating h under the brush and relighting it makes strokes look like physical media. Add sideways displacement later.

### Bristle brush models: skeleton, spring dynamics, footprint
The high-fidelity model (Chu & Tai) represents the brush as a skeleton — a chain of line segments with progressively shorter segments toward the tip, so angular resolution is concentrated where the expressive detail is — plus a lofted surface swept around the skeleton defining the bristle bundle. Dynamics are a spring-mass system with bending stiffness at each joint; the tablet's pressure/tilt/azimuth drives the root, the skeleton lags and bends, and the brush's contact with the paper is resolved by pushing the skeleton up out of the paper plane. Later refinements add anisotropic friction (bristles slide along their length more easily than across), lateral spine nodes so the brush can flatten and splay under pressure, and bristle *spreading* implemented as a static alpha map that gets stretched and sheared according to the deformation rather than simulating individual hairs. The footprint — the intersection of the deformed bundle with the paper — is rasterised per input sample and the stroke is the union of footprints along the path.

The cheap version that gets 80% of the look: N independent bristles (16-64), each an offset from the brush centre in brush-local space. Each bristle position exponentially lags the ideal position (p_i += k*(target_i - p_i)), which produces the trailing splay on direction changes. Each bristle carries its own colour load and its own depletion counter. Render each as a tapered quad strip or a stamped soft dot along its path. This gives you: splay under pressure (scale the offsets by pressure), directional streaking, per-bristle colour pickup, and dry-brush breakup — with no physics solver at all.

- **Used by:** Chu & Tai, 'Real-Time Painting with an Expressive Virtual Chinese Brush' (IEEE CG&A 2004) and 'MoXi' SIGGRAPH 2005; Baxter's DAB 3D virtual brushes with haptic feedback; Krita's Bristle Brush Engine, which exposes per-bristle ink depletion over stroke length and lets bristles individually pick up canvas colour so 'the bristles take on the colors of the first area they touch' (https://docs.krita.org/en/reference_manual/brushes/brush_engines/bristle_engine.html). Expresii's 'Yibi Brush Engine' is the commercial descendant of MoXi's deformable brush.
- **Relevance:** Bristles are geometry, not simulation — Win2D can draw them directly via CanvasPathBuilder/DrawGeometry or a CanvasCommandList, and per-bristle state lives in ordinary C# objects. This is the one part of natural media that is *not* GPU-constrained in Quill's stack. Note the input coupling: a bristle brush is only convincing if it consumes tilt and azimuth from the pen, so Quill's ink input path must surface those, not just pressure. Do not attempt full 3D FEM bristles; the lagged-offset model is the right cost/quality point.

### Paint loading, depletion, and colour pickup (the two-well model)
The standard architecture is two paint reservoirs on the brush. The *reservoir well* holds the colour the artist selected and has large capacity; the *pickup well* receives paint only from the canvas and is continuously re-mixed with what the brush is passing over. Three parameters govern it:
- Wet: how much paint can be lifted off the canvas into the pickup well (0% = canvas is dry and nothing lifts; 100% = everything lifts).
- Load: how much paint is in the reservoir; low load means strokes dry out and break up quickly; 100% means an effectively infinite stroke.
- Mix: the ratio of pickup-well paint to reservoir paint in what gets deposited (100% = deposit only canvas colour, i.e. pure smudge; 0% = deposit only brush colour).

Per input sample: pickup = lerp(pickup, sample_canvas(footprint), wet); deposit_colour = mix_pigment(reservoir, pickup, mix); deposit_alpha *= load_remaining; load_remaining -= depletion_rate * distance_travelled. The characteristic behaviour is a *transient*: a white-loaded brush dragged through wet red paints a pink smear that gradually returns to white as the picked-up red is depleted — the smear has a finite, physically-plausible length rather than being a uniform blend.

The richer variant (DAB) makes the brush surface itself a small texture with per-texel paint, bidirectionally exchanged with the canvas. This lets the brush carry a *spatial arrangement* of colour — dip one side in blue and one in yellow from a palette and the stroke shows both, which no scalar two-well model can do.

- **Used by:** Adobe Photoshop Mixer Brush (helpx.adobe.com/photoshop/using/painting-mixer-brush.html) with the reservoir/pickup-well architecture documented in Adobe patents US8599213 ('configurable wetness, drying, and mixing parameters') and US8654143 ('non-uniform loading of digital paint brushes'). Baxter et al. DAB's 'bidirectional, two-layer paint model' combined with a palette interface for loading complex blends. Krita's Color Smudge and MyPaint engines expose smudge + Color Pickup Radius. Procreate's 'wet mix' group is the same model.
- **Relevance:** Cheap and entirely CPU-side except for the canvas sample. The one real cost is reading back the canvas colour under the footprint each input sample — a GPU->CPU readback per pen event would stall the pipeline badly. Solutions: keep a small CPU-side mip/proxy of the canvas updated lazily, or do the whole brush-state update in a shader on a 1x1 (or 16x16) render target and never read it back to the CPU at all. CRITICAL: the mix must happen in pigment/latent space, not composited RGB, or the muddy-mixing problem reappears exactly where users will notice it most.

### Drying model and the wet-on-wet / wet-on-dry distinction
A single scalar wet map W(x,y) is enough to unify the whole family. W is written by the brush (rasterise a constant representing 'remaining wet time') and decays per tick, modulated by paper absorbency and a global drying rate. Everything else keys off it:
- W = 0 (wet-on-dry): a new dab is composited directly, the fluid solver is not invoked for that region, the edge is hard, and only the paper height texture modulates coverage. This is the crisp, controlled look.
- W > 0 (wet-on-wet): the dab's pigment enters the suspended pool g rather than the deposited layer d, the solver runs, edges soften, and pigments from the new dab and the existing wash advect into each other. Blooming happens.
- Re-wetting: a new dab with high water content raises W in a region that had dried, which de-deposits some d back into g. Rebelle's 'Re-wet' slider is literally the gain on this term: low values give soft blending with the paint below, high values produce 'strong watercolor edges' because the re-mobilised pigment gets swept to a new perimeter.
- Drying is also the retirement mechanism: when W hits zero everywhere in a region, that region's simulation state (splats, velocities, suspended pigment) can be discarded and baked into the static canvas raster. This is what bounds the cost of a long painting session.

Rebelle exposes three orthogonal controls over this: Diffusion Speed (1-10), Re-wet (1-10), Absorbency (0-10, 'when set to 0, the washes diffuse for a longer time'), plus a visual overlay tinting wet areas blue so the artist can see the wet map.

- **Used by:** Rebelle 7 (https://escapemotions.com/products/rebelle/manual/starting-painting/water/); DiVerdi et al.'s wet map with per-cell remaining-wet-time and splat reactivation; Krita's GSoC watercolor engine, which uses exactly the same 'remaining amount of time each cell will stay wet' formulation; Adobe Fresco ('the watercolor stays wet' until the layer is explicitly dried, and Fresco makes drying a user action).
- **Relevance:** The wet map is the load-bearing data structure for both correctness and performance — it is simultaneously the physics state, the UI affordance (show the artist where the canvas is wet), and the memory/cost governor (dried regions leave the sim). Design it in from the start. Also: make 'dry the layer' an explicit user command as Fresco does, because artists need deterministic control over when a wash stops moving, and it gives Quill a clean point to retire simulation state.

### Win2D/Direct2D cost envelope: what is and is not feasible
Hard constraints of the stack: Direct2D does not support linking compute or vertex shaders — custom effects are PIXEL SHADERS ONLY. Win2D's PixelShaderEffect takes 0-8 ICanvasImage sources and compiles HLSL via fxc, typically targeting ps_4_0 (or 4_0_level_9_3 for feature-level-9.3 hardware compatibility). ComputeSharp.D2D1.WinUI lets you author these shaders in C# and exposes PixelShaderEffect<T> and CanvasEffect that implement ICanvasImage, so they compose into Win2D graphs; it supports D2D1ShaderProfile.PixelShader50. ComputeSharp's DX12 compute path exists but is a separate device with no documented Win2D interop — bridging would require a shared D3D12 resource and is a serious engineering lift.

What this permits: any gather-only stencil over a fixed grid, iterated by ping-ponging between two CanvasRenderTargets and calling DrawImage per pass. That covers everything in the Curtis model — velocity update, Jacobi pressure relaxation, upwind advection, pigment transfer, capillary diffusion — and also lattice-Boltzmann in principle.

What it forbids or heavily penalises:
- No multiple render targets. A pixel shader writes one RGBA. Any solver with more than 4 state channels needs one full pass per output texture, re-reading all inputs each time. A D2Q9 lattice-Boltzmann solver (9 floats/cell) therefore costs ~3x its natural cost. This is the single biggest hidden multiplier.
- No scatter writes, no atomics, no shared memory, no prefix sums. SPH/particle fluids with neighbour search are off the table.
- Precision: use R16G16B16A16Float render targets minimum for velocity/pressure/pigment. 8-bit UNorm will quantise velocities to zero and the pressure relaxation will simply not converge.
- Direct2D applies implicit colour-space handling in effect graphs. Simulation data is not colour and must not be gamma-converted or premultiplied; set buffer precision explicitly, use CanvasAlphaMode.Ignore/Straight for data buffers, and CanvasComposite.Copy when writing them.

Order-of-magnitude budget: a full Curtis tick is roughly 20-50 full-screen passes (dominated by 10-40 Jacobi iterations). At a 1024x1024 sim grid that is ~20-50M pixel-shader invocations per tick — comfortable on a discrete GPU at 60Hz, marginal on integrated Intel graphics, and completely infeasible at 4096x4096 print resolution (16x the work).

- **Used by:** Microsoft Learn, 'Implementing custom effects' for Win2D (https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/apps/develop/win2d/custom-effects) and 'Effect shader linking' (https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/direct2d/effect-shader-linking), which states Direct2D does not support linking compute or vertex shaders. ComputeSharp D2D1 wiki (https://github.com/Sergio0694/ComputeSharp/wiki/4.-D2D1-support-%F0%9F%93%9A). Contrast with Expresii, which runs MoXi's lattice-Boltzmann solver on full CUDA/compute, and Fresco, which reached for tiled processing to fit a mobile GPU budget.
- **Relevance:** This is the decisive constraint on the whole Art Mode design. It rules IN: Curtis-style grid watercolor at bounded resolution, impasto height/normal relighting, per-pixel pigment mixing via LUT, splat rendering. It rules OUT: particle fluids, anything needing MRT-heavy state, print-resolution grid simulation. Decouple sim resolution (fixed, e.g. 512-1024 square, tiled to the active wet region) from canvas resolution, and reconstruct high-frequency detail at present time by modulating the upsampled sim output with a full-resolution paper texture. Degrade the Jacobi iteration count under load, never the sim resolution mid-stroke.

### Paper as a single shared height field with three jobs
One tileable high-resolution paper/canvas texture drives three separate parts of the model, and using the *same* texture for all three is what makes the result cohere:
1. Flow perturbation — the gradient of h biases the velocity field (u -= dh/dx) in MoveWater, producing streaks parallel to the flow direction and pooling in the valleys.
2. Granulation and capillary capacity — h feeds the gamma coupling in TransferPigment (pigment settles where h is low) and sets the per-cell capacity c in the capillary layer (rough paper holds more water and wicks further).
3. Final grain and impasto lighting — h is added to the paint height field so the bare paper catches the same key light as the paint, and it modulates coverage for dry-brush breakup.
If these three use different textures, or if only (3) is implemented (the common shortcut of overlaying a paper texture at the end), the result looks like a photo of paper with a painting pasted on it. When the same h drives flow, deposition, and lighting, the pigment is visibly *in* the paper.

- **Used by:** Curtis et al. (paper height h appears in the velocity update, the granulation term, and the capillary capacity); Rebelle, ArtRage and Corel Painter all ship canvas/paper libraries where the surface affects behaviour, not just appearance; Rebelle's Impasto engine shares the height map between canvas weave and paint thickness.
- **Relevance:** Cheap — it is one texture fetch reused across passes that are already running. But it must be architected in, not bolted on. Store paper height at high resolution (it is static, so cost is memory not compute), sample it at sim resolution for (1) and (2), and at full canvas resolution for (3). Making the paper selectable per notebook is a natural first-class feature for a notebook app and costs nothing beyond a texture swap.

### Recommendations
- Build in two tiers. Tier A (default, always on): the DiVerdi-style procedural polygon-splat engine plus a coarse wet map. It needs no custom shaders at all — Win2D CanvasPathBuilder/FillGeometry renders splats — it is resolution-independent so it works at print resolution, its cost scales with active splat count rather than canvas area, and it was explicitly designed for exactly Quill's hardware situation. Tier B (opt-in 'wet engine'): the Curtis grid simulation, running at fixed 512-1024 square resolution over only the tiled active wet region. Do not make Tier B the default and do not make it the first thing built.
- Implement impasto FIRST, before any fluid simulation. A scalar paint-height field, normal reconstruction by central differences, and a Blinn-Phong or GGX relight is ONE Win2D PixelShaderEffect pass over one R16Float texture. It is by far the highest ratio of 'looks like real media' to engineering cost in this entire dimension, it works identically for oil, gouache, and dry media, and it validates the render pipeline before any solver exists. Store height at R16Float — 8-bit terraces visibly in the specular.
- Store the canvas in pigment space, not RGB. Whether Quill uses Mixbox's 7-channel latent or a small set of pigment concentrations, the canvas buffer, the brush wells, the smudge/pickup path, and any blur or advection must all operate on pigment concentrations, converting to RGB only in a single present-time shader. Retrofitting this later means rewriting every blend path in the app. The payoff is that pigment mixing then happens correctly and automatically everywhere, including in hardware alpha blending, because mixing is linear in latent space.
- Resolve the Mixbox licence question before committing to an architecture. Mixbox is CC BY-NC 4.0; shipping it in a commercial Quill requires a paid licence from Escape Motions/scrtwpns. If that is not acceptable, the licence-clean fallback is Burns' spectral upsampling with weighted geometric mean (arXiv:1710.06364), reduced from 36 bands to 8-12 and applied on the CPU to brush/palette colours only, with a small pigment-concentration canvas. Both are viable; the decision is legal and must be made first because it determines the canvas format.
- Do all sim and compositing in linear light, and explicitly defeat Direct2D's implicit colour management on data buffers. Use R16G16B16A16Float render targets for velocity, pressure, and pigment; CanvasAlphaMode.Ignore or Straight (never premultiplied) for non-colour data; CanvasComposite.Copy when writing sim state. 8-bit UNorm buffers will make the pressure relaxation fail to converge and the velocity field quantise to zero.
- Use explicit CanvasRenderTarget ping-pong with one DrawImage per pass rather than building a deep Direct2D effect graph. This gives per-buffer precision control, avoids D2D re-evaluating the whole graph, and makes the pass count directly measurable. Author the shaders in C# via ComputeSharp.D2D1.WinUI (PixelShaderEffect<T> / CanvasEffect implement ICanvasImage and compose with Win2D cleanly) rather than hand-managing HLSL and fxc.
- Implement FlowOutward explicitly — p -= eta * (1 - GaussianBlur(wetMask, large sigma)) — using Win2D's built-in hardware GaussianBlurEffect. This one term is the entire edge-darkening mechanism and it costs almost nothing because the blur is a native accelerated effect. Without it the wet engine produces flat, soft, unconvincing washes and the reason will not be obvious.
- Implement the capillary layer with the wet-mask expansion rule (if s > sigma then M = 1) and the de-deposition path in TransferPigment. This is what produces backruns, blooms, and cauliflower edges — the effects users cite as proof an engine is real — for maybe three additional single-channel pixel shader passes.
- Run the simulation on a fixed-timestep accumulator decoupled from both the render loop and the pen input rate. Pen report rates vary from 60 to 240+ Hz across devices and frame rates vary under load; if the solver steps per input event or per frame, the same stroke gesture produces visibly different marks on different hardware, which is unacceptable in a note-taking app where consistency matters.
- Build the wet map as a first-class, visible, user-controllable object: it is the physics state, the UI affordance (tint wet regions as Rebelle does), and the cost governor (dried regions retire from the simulation and bake into the static raster). Ship an explicit 'dry layer' command as Fresco does — artists need deterministic control over when a wash stops moving, and it gives Quill a clean point to reclaim simulation state.
- Use a single high-resolution paper height texture for all three of its jobs — flow perturbation (u -= dh/dx), granulation coupling and capillary capacity, and final grain plus impasto lighting. Sample it at sim resolution for the first two and at full canvas resolution for the third. Using different textures, or only doing the final overlay, is what makes digital paper look pasted on.
- For bristle brushes use the lagged-offset model, not physics: 16-64 independent bristles in brush-local space, each exponentially lagging its target position, each with its own colour load and depletion counter, each rendered as a tapered geometry strip. This is pure Win2D geometry with C#-side state — the one part of natural media that is not GPU-constrained in this stack. It requires surfacing pen tilt and azimuth through Quill's ink input path, not just pressure; plan for that in the input layer.
- Implement brush loading as the two-well model (reservoir + pickup well, with Wet/Load/Mix parameters) and update brush state entirely on the GPU in a tiny render target rather than reading the canvas back to the CPU per pen event. A GPU->CPU readback per input sample will stall the pipeline and is the most likely source of stroke latency in a smudge-capable brush.
- Set an explicit per-frame pass budget and degrade the Jacobi iteration count under load — never the sim resolution mid-stroke. Changing sim resolution while a stroke is in flight changes the stroke's appearance visibly and non-recoverably. Measure on integrated Intel graphics, not a discrete GPU: a 20-50 pass tick at 1024x1024 is comfortable on a dGPU and marginal on integrated.

### Pitfalls
- Believing that linear-light blending or Oklab fixes pigment mixing. They fix different problems. Linear-light fixes midpoint darkening; Oklab fixes perceptual non-uniformity of ramps. NEITHER makes blue + yellow into green — linear-light gives a brighter grey and Oklab gives a desaturated near-neutral along the short hue path. Only a spectral or Kubelka-Munk model produces green. Several secondary sources and colour-tool blogs actively claim Oklab makes blue+yellow go 'through a clean teal-ish green'; this is false and will send an implementation down the wrong road.
- Expecting edge darkening to emerge from a correct fluid simulation. It does not. It is produced by an explicit added term — FlowOutward, which subtracts a large-sigma Gaussian blur of the wet mask from the pressure field to create an outward pressure gradient. This is the single most common reason a from-scratch Curtis implementation looks flat and wrong despite the Navier-Stokes solve being correct.
- Expecting backruns and blooms to emerge from the shallow-water layer. They require the separate capillary layer plus the wet-mask expansion rule (if saturation > threshold, set M = 1) plus the de-deposition path that lifts settled pigment d back into suspension g. Omit any of the three and there are no blooms.
- Using semi-Lagrangian advection because it is unconditionally stable. It is heavily dissipative and will smear pigment washes into mush, destroying granulation and the crisp deposited edges that are the entire point. Curtis uses explicit upwind flux precisely because it conserves pigment mass exactly. In paint simulation, conservation matters more than stability — non-conservation appears as colour drifting lighter or darker over a long stroke, which artists notice immediately.
- Assuming Win2D can run compute shaders. It cannot — Direct2D does not support linking compute or vertex shaders, only pixel shaders. Any design document that assumes shared memory, atomics, scatter writes, or prefix sums is invalid for this stack. ComputeSharp's DX12 compute path is a separate device with no documented Win2D interop.
- Forgetting that a Direct2D pixel shader has exactly ONE RGBA output. Any solver state wider than 4 channels needs a full additional pass per output texture, re-reading all inputs each time. This silently triples or quadruples the cost of multi-field solvers — a D2Q9 lattice-Boltzmann scheme (9 floats/cell) looks attractively cheap on paper and is roughly 3x its natural cost here. Budget passes, not FLOPs.
- Running the simulation at canvas resolution. A 20-50 pass Curtis tick at 1024x1024 is ~20-50M pixel-shader invocations and is fine; the same at 4096x4096 print resolution is 16x that and is not. The DiVerdi paper's entire stated motivation was that grid simulations 'required powerful compute hardware and were limited to screen resolutions' — this is a known, documented wall, not a tuning problem.
- Using 8-bit UNorm buffers for simulation state. Velocities quantise to zero, the Jacobi pressure relaxation fails to converge, and the height field terraces visibly in the specular highlight. R16G16B16A16Float is the floor for velocity/pressure/pigment; consider R32 float for pressure specifically if fp16 relaxation stalls.
- Letting Direct2D apply implicit gamma or premultiplied-alpha conversions to simulation buffers. Velocity, pressure, saturation, and pigment concentration are not colours. A silent sRGB round-trip on a velocity field is a bug that produces plausible-looking but subtly wrong flow and is extremely hard to diagnose.
- Doing brush colour pickup and wet-on-wet blending on composited RGB. This reintroduces the muddy-mixing problem at exactly the point users care about most — dragging a brush through wet paint. The mix must happen on pigment concentrations.
- Reading the canvas back to the CPU per pen input event to sample colour for the smudge/pickup well. This stalls the GPU pipeline and is a top source of stroke latency. Keep brush well state on the GPU in a tiny render target, or maintain a lazily-updated CPU-side proxy.
- Numerically unstable Kubelka-Munk inversion. Deriving K and S from artist-picked appearance over white and black requires 0 < R_b < R_w < 1; as R_w approaches 1 or R_b approaches 0 the arccoth term blows up. Clamp inputs to roughly [0.005, 0.995]. Similarly, weighted-geometric-mean spectral mixing takes logs of reflectance, so R = 0 must be clamped away from zero.
- Treating Mixbox as free. It is CC BY-NC 4.0 — non-commercial only. Shipping it in a commercial product requires a paid licence. Discovering this after the canvas format has been built around its 7-channel latent space is an expensive mistake.
- Assuming Kubelka-Munk's residual/gamut limitation is invisible. Mixbox's four-pigment basis is essentially a CMYK gamut; saturated display colours outside it are carried by an additive residual term that does NOT mix physically. Colours that are heavily residual (electric cyan, pure display blue, neon greens) degrade back toward RGB-like mixing behaviour, and users who pick colours from a saturated on-screen palette will hit this constantly.
- Stepping the solver per frame or per pen event rather than on a fixed-timestep accumulator. Pen report rates range from 60 to 240+ Hz across devices and frame rate varies under load, so the same gesture produces visibly different marks on different hardware or under different system load.
- Changing simulation resolution mid-stroke to recover performance. The stroke's appearance changes visibly and irreversibly at the switch point. Degrade the Jacobi iteration count instead — it costs some pressure accuracy but does not produce a visible discontinuity.
- Applying paper texture only as a final overlay. Paper must drive flow perturbation (via its height gradient), granulation (via the gamma coupling in pigment transfer), and capillary capacity — not just the final grain. Overlay-only paper reads as a painting pasted onto a photo of paper.
- Attempting a physically-simulated 3D bristle bundle (FEM or full spring-mass with collision) as a first implementation. Chu and Tai's model took multiple papers to get right and needs anisotropic friction, lateral spine nodes for flattening, and a bristle-spreading alpha map to look correct. The lagged-offset multi-bristle model gets most of the expressive range for a fraction of the effort and none of the stability risk.
